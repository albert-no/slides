01

DNN as Markov Chain

Each layer processes, each layer loses

Why IT for Deep Learning?

Information theory offers tools that classical learning theory lacks:

  • DPI: fundamental limits on what any representation can achieve
  • MI: a single quantity that measures both compression and prediction
  • Variational bounds: tractable surrogates for intractable MI (BA, DV, NWJ from Lectures MI-1,2)

The IB gives deep learning a principled objective: not just "minimize the loss," but "minimize the loss while compressing the representation."

Classical IT asks: how many bits to describe data?
IB asks: which bits matter?

Layer Representations

An $L$-layer network maps input $X$ to output $\hat Y$ through hidden representations:

$X$ $\to$ $T_1$ $\to$ $T_2$ $\to$ $\cdots$ $\to$ $T_L$ $\to$ $\hat Y$

Each $T_l = f_l(T_{l-1})$ is a deterministic function of the previous layer.

Together with target $Y$: the chain $Y - X - T_1 - T_2 - \cdots - T_L$ is Markov.

DPI for Every Layer

Recall (DPI). $Y - X - T$ Markov $\implies$ $I(T;Y) \le I(X;Y)$.

Applied layer-by-layer:

$$I(X;Y) \ge I(T_1;Y) \ge I(T_2;Y) \ge \cdots \ge I(T_L;Y).$$

Deeper layers can only lose information about $Y$.

A good network loses as little as possible about $Y$ while discarding irrelevant information about $X$.

Layers in the Information Plane

Each layer $l$ maps to a point $(I(X;T_l),\; I(T_l;Y))$.

DPI constrains both coordinates monotonically:

$$\begin{aligned} I(X;T_1) &\ge I(X;T_2) \ge \cdots \ge I(X;T_L), \\ I(T_1;Y) &\ge I(T_2;Y) \ge \cdots \ge I(T_L;Y). \end{aligned}$$

Layers trace a path from upper-right to lower-left in the plane.

An ideal network: layers hug the IB curve, shedding $I(X;T)$ without losing $I(T;Y)$.

02

Information Plane Hypothesis

Does training drive compression?

The Hypothesis

Conjecture (Shwartz-Ziv and Tishby, 2017).

DNN training via SGD has two distinct phases in the information plane:

Phase 1 (fitting): $I(T_l;Y)$ increases rapidly for all layers.

Phase 2 (compression): $I(X;T_l)$ decreases for hidden layers.

Phase 1 learns the task. Phase 2 discards irrelevant features.

Shwartz-Ziv and Tishby, "Opening the Black Box of DNNs via Information", 2017.

The Fitting Phase

Early training epochs:

  • Gradients are large, weights change rapidly
  • $I(T_l; Y)$ increases for all layers: the network learns to predict
  • $I(X; T_l)$ also increases: representations become more informative about $X$

Both coordinates move up and to the right in the information plane.

Duration: typically short (few hundred iterations).

The Compression Phase

Later training epochs:

  • Gradients shrink, SGD noise dominates the signal
  • $I(T_l; Y)$ stays roughly constant: prediction quality is stable
  • $I(X; T_l)$ decreases: hidden layers forget irrelevant input details

Layers drift leftward in the information plane, approaching the IB curve.

Claim: SGD noise acts as a natural regularizer, driving representations toward the IB-optimal tradeoff.

Why Compression Might Help

Compressed representations generalize better. The IB perspective explains why:

  • Low $I(X;T)$: fewer bits to memorize = fewer training-specific patterns
  • High $I(T;Y)$: bits that remain are predictive of the target

The IB curve is the Pareto frontier: any representation not on the curve either wastes bits or loses prediction quality.

Generalization bound (informal): excess risk $\lesssim \sqrt{I(S;W)/n}$, where $S$ is the training set and $W$ are the weights.

Xu and Raginsky, "Information-Theoretic Analysis of Generalization", ISIT 2017.
03

Variational Information Bottleneck

Making the IB objective tractable for neural networks

The Tractability Problem

The IB objective $\min I(X;T) - \beta\, I(T;Y)$ requires computing two MI terms.

For continuous neural representations, both are intractable:

  • $I(X;T)$: requires the marginal $p(t) = \mathbb{E}_{p(x)}[p(t|x)]$ (expectation over data)
  • $I(T;Y)$: requires the posterior $p(y|t)$ (Bayes with intractable marginal)

Solution: variational bounds on each term. The same bounds we derived in Lectures MI-1 and Diffusion-1.

Upper Bound on $I(X;T)$

Lemma (Rate Upper Bound).

For any distribution $r(t)$:

$$I(X;T) \le \mathbb{E}_{p(x)}\!\left[D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(p(t|x)\,\big\|\, r(t)\right)\right].$$

Equality iff $r(t) = p(t)$.

Proof. $\;\mathbb{E}_{p(x)}[D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p(t|x)\| r(t))] - I(X;T) = D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p(t)\| r(t)) \ge 0. \;\square$

Choose $r(t) = \mathcal{N}(0, I)$: the bound becomes a closed-form KL.

Lower Bound on $I(T;Y)$

Recall (Barber-Agakov, Lecture MI-1). $I(T;Y) \ge \mathbb{E}_{p(t,y)}[\log q(y|t)] + H(Y).$

For any variational decoder $q_\psi(y|t)$:

$$I(T;Y) \ge \mathbb{E}_{p(t,y)}[\log q_\psi(y|t)] + H(Y).$$

Equality iff $q_\psi(y|t) = p(y|t)$.

For classification: $q_\psi(y|t) = \mathrm{Cat}(y;\, \mathrm{softmax}(W_\psi t))$.

The bound becomes the negative cross-entropy loss.

VIB Objective

Definition (Variational Information Bottleneck, Alemi et al., 2017).

Combining both bounds, the VIB objective is:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{VIB}} = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{p(x,y)}\,\mathbb{E}_{p_\phi(t|x)}\!\left[\log q_\psi(y|t)\right]}_{\text{prediction}} - \beta\,\underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{p(x)}\!\left[D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(p_\phi(t|x)\,\big\|\, r(t)\right)\right]}_{\text{compression}}.$$

Maximize over encoder $\phi$ and decoder $\psi$.

Alemi, Fischer, Dillon, and Murphy, "Deep Variational Information Bottleneck", ICLR 2017.

VIB Architecture

Instantiate with Gaussian encoder $p_\phi(t|x) = \mathcal{N}(\mu_\phi(x),\, \sigma^2_\phi(x)\,I)$ and prior $r(t) = \mathcal{N}(0, I)$:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{VIB}} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left[\log q_\psi(y_i | t_i) - \frac{\beta}{2}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\!\left(1 + \log\sigma^2_{\phi,k}(x_i) - \mu^2_{\phi,k}(x_i) - \sigma^2_{\phi,k}(x_i)\right)\right],$$

where $t_i = \mu_\phi(x_i) + \sigma_\phi(x_i) \odot \varepsilon$, $\;\varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,I)$ (reparameterization trick).

Connection to the VAE

Recall (VAE loss, Lecture Diff-1). $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{VAE}} = \mathbb{E}[\log f_\theta(x|z)] - D_{\mathrm{KL}}(g_\phi(z|x)\| f(z)).$

VAE VIB
First term $\log f_\theta(x|z)$ (reconstruction) $\log q_\psi(y|t)$ (prediction)
KL term $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(g_\phi \| f)$ $\beta\, D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_\phi \| r)$
Goal Reconstruct $X$ Predict $Y$

VIB = VAE with a classifier decoder instead of a reconstruction decoder. Same architecture, different objective.

What $\beta$ Controls

  • $\beta = 0$: standard cross-entropy training, no compression
  • Small $\beta$: mild regularization, representations slightly compressed
  • Large $\beta$: heavy compression, representations approach the prior $r(t) = \mathcal{N}(0,I)$

Empirical finding (Alemi et al.):

  • Moderate $\beta$ improves robustness to adversarial examples
  • Compressed representations generalize better to distribution shifts
  • Too large $\beta$ degrades accuracy (over-compression)
04

The Debate

Does compression really happen in deep networks?

The Estimation Problem

For deterministic layers $T_l = f_l(T_{l-1})$ with continuous input:

$$I(X; T_l) = H(T_l) - \underbrace{H(T_l | X)}_{= 0} = H(T_l).$$

Differential entropy $H(T_l)$ of a deterministic function can be infinite (or undefined).

Shwartz-Ziv & Tishby used binning to estimate $I(X;T_l)$: discretize $T_l$ into bins, compute discrete MI.

Binning introduces a resolution parameter. The observed compression may reflect the binning, not the network.

Activation Function Dependence

Key Finding (Saxe et al., 2019).

The compression phase depends on the activation function:

tanh: compression observed (saturating nonlinearity clusters activations).

ReLU: no clear compression phase.

Why? Saturating activations like tanh map many inputs to similar outputs, effectively discretizing the representation. ReLU preserves the input structure.

Saxe, Bansal, Dapello, Advani, and Kolchinsky, "On the IB Theory of Deep Learning", JSTAT 2019.

What Does Not Survive

  • Universal two-phase claim: compression is not a general property of DNN training with SGD
  • SGD noise as the driver: compression occurs with full-batch gradient descent for tanh networks too
  • Binned MI as a diagnostic: the value depends on bin size, making comparisons fragile

The specific mechanism (SGD noise → compression) does not hold in general.

What Survives

  • The information plane as a diagnostic: plotting $(I(X;T_l), I(T_l;Y))$ remains a useful visualization, even if the values are approximate
  • VIB as a training objective: well-motivated, practical, and provably controls the rate-relevance tradeoff
  • Geometric compression: hidden representations do become more clustered by class, even without MI compression
  • Generalization bounds via MI: the $I(S;W)$ framework provides valid (if loose) bounds

The IB principle is sound. The debate is about whether standard training implicitly solves IB, or whether we need to do so explicitly (via VIB).

IT Implications for Deep Learning

Regardless of the compression debate, the IB framework yields concrete insights:

  • Generalization $\leftrightarrow$ compression: $I(S;W)$ bounds the generalization gap. Lower MI between data and weights means better generalization
  • Representation quality: the IB curve defines the Pareto frontier. Representations below the curve are suboptimal
  • Regularization as rate control: dropout, weight decay, and noise injection all reduce $I(X;T)$. The IB explains why they help
  • Sufficient statistics: the optimal representation at $\beta \to \infty$ is a minimal sufficient statistic. Feature selection $\equiv$ IT compression

Summary

DNN layers form a Markov chain: DPI constrains each layer in the information plane.

Fitting-then-compressing (Shwartz-Ziv & Tishby): observed for saturating activations, not universal.

Variational IB: tractable IB for neural networks via variational bounds on both MI terms. Same architecture as VAE, different objective.

The IB principle is a valid lens for representation learning, whether or not training implicitly implements it.
