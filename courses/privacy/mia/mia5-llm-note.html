Companion to mia5-llm.html. Walks through the why of each LLM-MIA construction and the boundaries of what current attacks can detect.

Why LLMs break the classical MIA toolkit

Scale collapses shadow models

LiRA needs 64–256 shadow models, each a full training run. For a 7B-parameter LLM trained on a trillion tokens, this is not 100× harder than CIFAR — it is hundreds of millions of dollars of compute. No academic group, and frankly few industry labs, can produce LiRA-style shadow LLMs at pre-training scale. Every LLM MIA in the deck is engineered around this constraint.

Training distribution is unknown

For CIFAR you know the data: it is a published dataset. For GPT-4 the training mix is proprietary and the size is enormous. An auditor cannot in general sample "non-members" from the same distribution to calibrate a reference model. This kills any attack that assumes access to a clean held-out distribution.

One pass through the training set

Classical MIA assumes a model overfits to its training set through many epochs. Modern LLMs typically see most pre-training text exactly once. The overfitting signal that drove Yeom's threshold and Shokri's confidence gap is much weaker. Empirically, base-model pre-training MIA AUC is often barely above 0.5 (random), even with sophisticated attacks.

Three threat models, three difficulties

  • Pre-training: hardest. One epoch, vast data, unknown distribution. Most academic work struggles here.
  • Fine-tuning: easier. Smaller dataset, more epochs, attacker may know the fine-tuning data style. SPV-MIA achieves AUC 0.7+ here.
  • Context / RAG: distinct problem. The "training set" is the context window or retrieval corpus — already partially observable.

Reported numbers in the deck and the literature should always be read against which of these regimes is being tested.

§1 Perplexity baseline and its failure mode

The natural first attack

Given a text $x = (x_1, \dots, x_T)$ and a language model $p_\theta$, the per-token cross-entropy is

$$\text{NLL}(x) \;=\; -\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T}\log p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t}), \quad \text{PPL}(x) \;=\; \exp(\text{NLL}(x)).$$

Apply Yeom's threshold: $\mathcal{A}(x) = \mathbf{1}[\text{PPL}(x) < \tau]$. Members should have lower perplexity than non-members because the model has seen them. This is the published baseline in essentially every LLM MIA paper.

Why it fails

Perplexity confounds memorization with intrinsic predictability. A non-member sentence like "the cat sat on the mat" has very low perplexity because each token is highly likely given the prefix — not because the model saw the sentence. A member sentence drawn from a niche technical paper may have higher perplexity because its tokens are individually surprising. Yeom's bound goes wrong here because $R_{\text{pop}}$ is not constant across texts: different texts have different intrinsic difficulty.

Calibration is the entire problem

Once you accept that the perplexity baseline is biased by text difficulty, every subsequent LLM MIA is some kind of calibration. The five families of calibration the deck covers:

  1. Reference model: subtract the perplexity of a separately-trained model that almost certainly did not see $x$.
  2. Neighbourhood: compare $p_\theta(x)$ to $p_\theta$ on locally-perturbed neighbours of $x$.
  3. Self-prompt: query the target model to generate its own calibration distribution (SPV-MIA).
  4. Context variance: condition on multiple prefixes and look at variance.
  5. Token weighting: weight tokens by their informativeness (InfoRMIA).

§2 Neighbourhood attack (Mattern et al. 2023)

The mask-and-fill trick

To remove the "text difficulty" confounder, replace the unknown reference model with a local reference: small perturbations of $x$ itself. Concretely, mask out random spans of $x$ and fill them with a separate masked-LM (typically T5 or a smaller open model). This produces a set of neighbours $\{\tilde x^{(1)}, \dots, \tilde x^{(K)}\}$ that share most of $x$'s content but differ in surface form.

The score

$$s(x) \;=\; \log p_\theta(x) \;-\; \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K} \log p_\theta(\tilde x^{(k)}).$$

If $x$ is memorized, the target model strongly prefers the original wording over any perturbation, so $s(x)$ is large and positive. If $x$ is novel but easy, both $x$ and its neighbours have similar log-probability and $s(x)$ is near zero. The crucial feature: no shadow training is required, which is what makes the attack viable for frozen LLMs.

Where neighbourhood breaks

  • Highly structured text: code, math, legal boilerplate — most perturbations are syntactically invalid, so $p_\theta$ of any neighbour collapses to zero and the score is inflated.
  • Short text: with few tokens to perturb, the neighbourhood is small and noisy.
  • Reference-mask-LM mismatch: a small T5 may not produce realistic neighbours for highly technical text.

§3 SPV-MIA — self-prompting (Fu et al. 2024)

The trick

For fine-tuned models, neighbourhood-style masking is heavy-handed. SPV-MIA observes that the target model itself can generate calibration data: prompt the target with a short prefix derived from $x$ and let it sample its own continuation. This produces model-natural alternatives without any external mask-LM.

Mechanism in three steps

  1. Take a prefix of $x$ (say the first 20%).
  2. Sample $K$ continuations from the target model itself.
  3. Score $x$ as the gap between the log-probability of the actual $x$ and the average log-probability of the $K$ self-generated continuations under the target.

Members surface because the model assigns the exact original continuation higher probability than its own sampled alternatives. SPV-MIA reaches AUC ~0.85 on LLaMA fine-tuning regimes — substantially above neighbourhood attacks.

Why it works for fine-tuning but not pre-training

Fine-tuning typically sees data 3–10 epochs, producing a sharp memorization peak around fine-tuning examples. Self-prompt sampling exposes that peak. Pre-training, with its one-epoch regime, lacks this sharp peak and SPV-MIA degrades to roughly perplexity-baseline performance.

§4 Context-aware MIA

The variance idea

If the model has truly memorized $x$, its probability for $x$ should be high and stable across different prefixes $c$. If $x$ is just an easy sentence, its conditional probability $p_\theta(x \mid c)$ varies with the prefix because the prefix changes the relevant prior. The context-aware score

$$s(x) \;=\; \mathrm{Var}_{c}\big[\log p_\theta(x \mid c)\big]$$

(or its negative) flips the calibration direction: members show low variance because memorization is prefix-insensitive.

Why this catches things others miss

Perplexity-based attacks all compare absolute probabilities. Variance across contexts measures something different: how "robust" the probability is to the conditioning. A novel-but-easy sentence has high variance across prefixes; a memorized one has low variance. The two signals are complementary, and the best LLM MIAs combine them.

§5 InfoRMIA — token-level weighting

What perplexity throws away

Per-token cross-entropy averages across all tokens equally. In a typical sentence, function words ("the", "and", "of") dominate the count, and their probability is near 1 for any reasonable model. Memorization signal lives in the rare tokens: a proper noun, a number, a specific technical term. Averaging dilutes the signal by a factor of roughly (token count)/(informative-token count).

The weighting

InfoRMIA weights each token by its informativeness under a reference model:

$$s(x) \;=\; \sum_{t=1}^{T} w_t \cdot \big[\log p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t}) - \log p_{\text{ref}}(x_t \mid x_{<t})\big],$$

with $w_t$ small when the reference is confident (predictable token) and large when the reference is uncertain (informative token). The reference-model term subtracts intrinsic predictability; the weight focuses the gap on tokens where memorization could plausibly show. The construction is a Bayes-optimal-style importance weighting of the per-token log-likelihood ratio.

Information-theoretic foundation

The deck's "Information-Theoretic Foundation" slide identifies the population-level mutual information

$$I(\mathbf{1}[z \in D]\,;\, x_t \mid x_{<t})$$

between the membership bit and a single token, conditioned on its prefix. Tokens that maximise this mutual information are exactly the informative ones the weighting up-weights. InfoRMIA can be viewed as a Monte-Carlo estimator of this MI.

Reported results

On the WikiMIA benchmark, InfoRMIA reaches AUC ~0.74 and 12% TPR at 1% FPR, substantially above perplexity and neighbourhood baselines (typically 0.55–0.65 AUC, <5% TPR @ 1% FPR). The result holds across model scales from 1B to 70B parameters.

§6 Scaling: bigger models memorize more

Carlini's scaling laws

Carlini et al. (2022, 2023) measured extraction risk vs. model size and dataset repetition. Two robust trends:

  1. Bigger models memorize more verbatim text, holding training data constant. Capacity correlates roughly log-linearly with the fraction of a dataset that can be extracted.
  2. More duplicates → more memorization. A document appearing 100 times in the training corpus is memorized far more reliably than one appearing once.

The practical implication for MIA: vulnerable subpopulations exist. Even when average MIA AUC is near 0.55, a small set of duplicated rare documents may be reliably identifiable.

What does this mean for evaluation

Reporting average AUC on a benchmark like WikiMIA gives a population-level number. Reporting TPR @ low FPR catches the vulnerable tail. Reporting per-document scores and inspecting the top of the score distribution is the auditor's gold standard.

§7 Benchmarks

WikiMIA

Constructed by Shi et al. (2024). Uses Wikipedia articles published before and after a known cutoff date. Articles created after the cutoff are non-members for models trained up to the cutoff. Easy to construct, ambient noise from the data distribution being similar across periods. Typical scores: perplexity baseline AUC ~0.6, modern attacks 0.7–0.75.

MIMIR

Constructed by Duan et al. (2024). Specifically built to test MIA on open-source LLMs (Pythia, OPT) where the exact training set is known. Reports much lower attack performance than WikiMIA — most LLM MIAs hover near AUC 0.55 here. The interpretation: WikiMIA's pre/post cutoff structure leaks distributional shift signal that masquerades as membership signal. MIMIR is the more honest evaluation, and the news is sobering: true pre-training MIA on open LLMs is barely above random with current methods.

Implication

Headlines like "MIA detects training data on LLaMA" should be read against MIMIR results. Fine-tuning attacks (SPV-MIA, supervised fine-tuning audits) are reliable; pre-training attacks are not yet there.

§8 From MIA to extraction

The pipeline

Combining MIA scoring with prompted generation gives a training-data extraction attack:

  1. Prompt the model with diverse continuations.
  2. Score each generated continuation with an MIA detector.
  3. Surface the top-scoring continuations as candidate verbatim training data.

Carlini et al.'s original GPT-2 extraction work used a perplexity + duplicate-detection pipeline. Modern variants substitute neighbourhood or InfoRMIA for the scoring step, yielding higher extraction precision.

Extractable vs. inferable

The deck distinguishes extractable (the model produces the exact verbatim training text) from inferable (an MIA detector can recognise the text given to it). The inferable set is larger: many documents the model would not spontaneously produce can still be recognised when offered as a query. This matters for legal frameworks like GDPR's right to be forgotten — both notions of memorization create obligations.

§9 Defenses for LLMs

DP fine-tuning

DP-SGD applied to fine-tuning works in practice: $\varepsilon = 1$–$8$ fine-tuning typically costs <2% of task accuracy and substantially reduces SPV-MIA AUC. Recent work (Ganesh et al., Anil et al.) extends this to LoRA and PEFT regimes. DP pre-training at full scale is still open; the per-example sensitivity bounds force batch sizes into the millions, and the noise overhead is large.

Deduplication

Carlini's scaling-law work suggests that aggressive deduplication of the training corpus is by far the highest-impact and cheapest defense. Removing exact and near-duplicate documents cuts extractable memorization by roughly an order of magnitude. The major industry labs all run dedup pipelines.

Machine unlearning

Given a request to forget a specific document, retrain or fine-tune to push the model toward a "never saw this" state. Verification is itself an MIA problem: an unlearning method is convincing only if a strong MIA cannot detect the document post-unlearning. This places MIA in the role of audit tool, not just attack.

Legal context

GDPR Article 17 (right to erasure), the New York Times v. OpenAI lawsuit (2023), and ongoing litigation around training-data provenance all use MIA-style evidence. The chain is: an MIA detector identifies training documents → plaintiff alleges the training was unauthorised → the deployer must prove unlearning. MIA is becoming a regulatory tool, not just a research curiosity.

Pseudocode (full side-by-side)

Perplexity baseline

def ppl_attack(model, text, τ):
    ppl = exp(-mean([log_prob(model, t, text[:i]) for i, t in text]))
    return ppl < τ

Neighbourhood attack

def neighbourhood_attack(model, ref_model, text, K):
    neighbours = [mask_and_fill(ref_model, text) for _ in range(K)]
    score = log_prob(model, text) - mean([log_prob(model, n) for n in neighbours])
    return score

Token-level InfoRMIA

def informia_attack(target, ref, text):
    scores = []
    for t, token in enumerate(text):
        diff = log_prob(target, token, text[:t]) - log_prob(ref, token, text[:t])
        w = informativeness(ref, token, text[:t])
        scores.append(w * diff)
    return sum(scores)

Reading the comparison

  • Perplexity conflates "memorized" with "easy to predict from the prefix" — it cannot distinguish memorization from intrinsic predictability.
  • Neighbourhood isolates "memorization of this exact wording" by comparing to neighbours that share semantic content but differ in surface form.
  • InfoRMIA reweights by token informativeness, surfacing memorization signal that perplexity averages away.

Open problems

  • Reliable pre-training MIA on frontier LLMs. MIMIR results suggest current attacks are essentially random for true pre-training data on Pythia-scale models. New ideas are needed — possibly leveraging gradient information from the post-training fine-tuning step.
  • Multi-modal MIA. Vision-language models (CLIP, Flamingo, GPT-4V) train on image-text pairs. MIA in this setting is barely studied.
  • Reference-free attacks. Every strong attack except neighbourhood requires a reference model the auditor cannot always obtain.
  • Agents and RAG. When a system retrieves context at inference time, the "training set" includes the retrieval corpus and the context window — a moving target for MIA.
  • Audit-grade certificates. Translating MIA results into provable DP $\varepsilon$ bounds for deployed LLMs is the path to regulatory acceptance, and remains open.
