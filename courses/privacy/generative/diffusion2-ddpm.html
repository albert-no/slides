01

The Variance Preserving Process

Scaled noise so variance does not explode

Why Variance Preserving?

  • VE process: variance $\sigma_0^2 + n\sigma^2 \to \infty$.
  • Terminal distribution depends on data variance.
  • Prefer a fixed terminal, ideally $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$.
  • Trivial to sample from.

Shrink $X^{(n-1)}$ before adding noise: signal decays, noise accumulates to unit variance.

VP Forward Process

Fix schedule $\{\beta_n\}_{n=1}^N$ with $0 < \beta_n < 1$:

$$X^{(n)} = \sqrt{1-\beta_n}\,X^{(n-1)} + \sqrt{\beta_n}\,Z^{(n)},\quad Z^{(n)} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)\ \text{i.i.d.}$$
  • Equivalently, $X^{(n)}\,|\,X^{(n-1)} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(\sqrt{1-\beta_n}\,X^{(n-1)},\; \beta_n\right)$.
  • If $\mathrm{Var}(X^{(n-1)}) = 1$, then $\mathrm{Var}(X^{(n)}) = (1-\beta_n) + \beta_n = 1$.

Exercise. Verify VP preserves variance and find $\mathrm{Var}(X^{(N)})$ as $N \to \infty$.

$n$ Steps in One Shot

Define $\bar{\alpha}_n = \prod_{i=1}^n (1-\beta_i)$. Unrolling the recursion:

$$X^{(n)} \;\stackrel{(d)}{=}\; \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_n}\,X^{(0)} + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_n}\,Z,\quad Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1).$$
  • Training at any noise level: single Gaussian draw.
  • As $\bar{\alpha}_n \to 0$: signal fades, noise approaches $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$.

Moment Generating Function

For random variable $X$,

$$M_X(t) \;=\; \mathbb{E}\bigl[e^{tX}\bigr],\quad t \in \mathbb{R}.$$
  • Independence: $M_{X+Y}(t) = M_X(t)\,M_Y(t)$.
  • Gaussian: $M_{\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2)}(t) = \exp\!\bigl(\mu t + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 t^2\bigr)$.
  • Lévy continuity: $M_{X_n}(t) \to M_X(t)$ pointwise $\Rightarrow$ $X_n \xrightarrow{d} X$.

Strategy: show $M_{X^{(N)}}(t) \to e^{t^2/2}$ to conclude $X^{(N)} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$.

Convergence: MGF Proof

Using independence of $X^{(n-1)}$ and $Z^{(n)}$:

$$\begin{aligned} M_{X^{(n)}}(t) &= \mathbb{E}\bigl[e^{t\sqrt{1-\beta_n}\,X^{(n-1)}}\bigr]\,e^{\beta_n t^2 / 2} \\ &\;\;\vdots \\ &= \mathbb{E}\bigl[e^{t\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_n}\,X^{(0)}}\bigr]\,e^{(1-\bar{\alpha}_n)\,t^2 / 2}. \end{aligned}$$

As $\bar{\alpha}_n \to 0$: first factor $\to 1$, second $\to e^{t^2/2}$.

Terminal $X^{(N)} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$, independent of data law.

02

DDPM Reverse Model

Plug in a network — and choose a training objective

Reverse Conditional, VP Version

By the Bayes-plus-Tweedie argument (Lecture 1), for small $\beta_n$:

$$X^{(n-1)}\,|\,X^{(n)} \;\approx\; \mathcal{N}\bigl(\mu(X^{(n)}, n),\; \beta_n\bigr),$$

where the mean is

$$\mu(X^{(n)}, n) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-\beta_n}}\left(X^{(n)} + \beta_n\,\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\log P_{X^{(n)}}(X^{(n)})\right).$$
Ho, Jain & Abbeel, "Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models", NeurIPS 2020.

Why Not Train $\mu$ Directly?

Tempting: parameterize $\mu_\theta(X^{(n)}, n)$ as a network, regress on data.

The mean's only unknown is the score:

$$\mu(X^{(n)}, n) = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-\beta_n}}\bigl(X^{(n)} + \beta_n\,\underbrace{\partial_x\log P_{X^{(n)}}(X^{(n)})}_{\text{unknown}}\bigr).$$

Learning $\mu_\theta$ is learning the score — same obstacle, different name.

Data gives us samples, not score values: no direct supervision target.

Two Routes to the Score

(a) Denoiser + Tweedie

Train $f_\theta(X^{(n)}; n) \approx \mathbb{E}[X^{(0)}\,|\,X^{(n)}]$ by squared-loss regression.

Tweedie converts the denoiser into a score.

Lecture 1.

(b) Variational lower bound

Start from MLE on data, bound the intractable likelihood, optimize the bound.

Yields $\epsilon$-prediction — plain regression.

This lecture (DDPM).

Both routes train networks; they differ in the loss derivation.

03

Variational Lower Bound

MLE → forward Markov factorization → $\epsilon$-prediction

Maximum Likelihood Objective

Data $X^{(0)}_1,\ldots,X^{(0)}_M \sim q$ i.i.d. Fit $p_\theta$ by MLE — equivalently, minimize the NLL (entropy is $\theta$-free):

$$\min_\theta \;\mathbb{E}_{X^{(0)}\sim q}\bigl[-\log p_\theta(X^{(0)})\bigr] \;=\; D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\bigl(q\,\Vert\,p_\theta\bigr) \;+\; H(q).$$

Maximize $\log p_\theta$ $\Leftrightarrow$ minimize NLL $\Leftrightarrow$ close the KL gap to the data law.

Marginal Is Intractable

Reverse chain ($p(X^{(N)}) = \mathcal{N}(0,1)$, $X^{(i:j)} = (X^{(i)},\ldots,X^{(j)})$):

$$\begin{aligned} p_\theta\bigl(X^{(0:N)}\bigr) &= p\bigl(X^{(N)}\bigr)\prod_{n=1}^{N} p_\theta\bigl(X^{(n-1)}\,|\,X^{(n)}\bigr), \\ p_\theta\bigl(X^{(0)}\bigr) &= \int p_\theta\bigl(X^{(0:N)}\bigr)\,dX^{(1:N)}. \end{aligned}$$

Marginal: high-dimensional integral, not directly computable.

Strategy: minimize a tractable upper bound on $-\log p_\theta(X^{(0)})$.

Forward Chain Is Markov

$X^{(n)}$ depends only on $X^{(n-1)}$:

$X^{(0)}$ $\to$ $X^{(1)}$ $\to$ $\cdots$ $\to$ $X^{(n-1)}$ $\to$ $X^{(n)}$ $\to$ $\cdots$ $\to$ $X^{(N)}$

Chain rule on $(X^{(n-1)}, X^{(n)})$ given $X^{(0)}$ ($n \geq 2$):

$$q\bigl(X^{(n)}\,|\,X^{(n-1)}\bigr)\,q\bigl(X^{(n-1)}\,|\,X^{(0)}\bigr) \;=\; q\bigl(X^{(n-1)}\,|\,X^{(n)}, X^{(0)}\bigr)\,q\bigl(X^{(n)}\,|\,X^{(0)}\bigr).$$

Exercise. Prove this identity from Bayes plus the Markov property.

Likelihood to VLB (Jensen)

Insert forward joint $q(X^{(1:N)}\,|\,X^{(0)}) = \prod_{n=1}^{N} q(X^{(n)}\,|\,X^{(n-1)})$ in num/denom — the $q$'s cancel — then apply Jensen:

$$\begin{aligned} -\log p_\theta\bigl(X^{(0)}\bigr) &= -\log \int q\bigl(X^{(1:N)}\,|\,X^{(0)}\bigr)\,\frac{p_\theta(X^{(0:N)})}{q(X^{(1:N)}\,|\,X^{(0)})}\,dX^{(1:N)} \\ &= -\log \mathbb{E}_q\!\left[\frac{p_\theta(X^{(0:N)})}{q(X^{(1:N)}\,|\,X^{(0)})}\,\middle|\,X^{(0)}\right] \\ &\leq \mathbb{E}_q\!\left[-\log \frac{p_\theta(X^{(0:N)})}{q(X^{(1:N)}\,|\,X^{(0)})}\,\middle|\,X^{(0)}\right] \;=:\; \mathrm{VLB}(X^{(0)}). \end{aligned}$$

Factor and Telescope

Expand both products inside the VLB ratio (split off the $n=1$ factors):

$$\frac{p_\theta(X^{(0:N)})}{q(X^{(1:N)}|X^{(0)})} \;=\; \frac{p(X^{(N)})\,p_\theta(X^{(0)}|X^{(1)})\,\prod_{n=2}^{N} p_\theta(X^{(n-1)}|X^{(n)})}{q(X^{(1)}|X^{(0)})\,\prod_{n=2}^{N} q(X^{(n)}|X^{(n-1)})}.$$

For $n \geq 2$, the Markov identity replaces $q(X^{(n)}|X^{(n-1)})$ with the posterior form. Telescoping cancels $q(X^{(1)}|X^{(0)})$ and leaves $q(X^{(N)}|X^{(0)})$:

$$=\; \underbrace{\frac{p(X^{(N)})}{q(X^{(N)}|X^{(0)})}}_{\to\,L_N}\;\cdot\;\prod_{n=2}^{N}\underbrace{\frac{p_\theta(X^{(n-1)}|X^{(n)})}{q(X^{(n-1)}|X^{(n)}, X^{(0)})}}_{\to\,L_{n-1}}\;\cdot\;\underbrace{p_\theta(X^{(0)}|X^{(1)})}_{\to\,L_0}.$$

Three-Term Decomposition

Take $-\log$ inside $\mathbb{E}_q$ — the product becomes a sum:

$$\mathbb{E}_q\!\left[\underbrace{-\log\frac{p(X^{(N)})}{q(X^{(N)}|X^{(0)})}}_{L_N}\; +\sum_{n=2}^{N}\underbrace{\biggl(-\log\frac{p_\theta(X^{(n-1)}|X^{(n)})}{q(X^{(n-1)}|X^{(n)}, X^{(0)})}\biggr)}_{L_{n-1}}\; \underbrace{-\log p_\theta(X^{(0)}|X^{(1)})}_{L_0}\right].$$

Three groups: prior match ($L_N$), reverse-step KL ($L_{n-1}$, summed), boundary ($L_0$).

$L_N$: Match the Prior

$$L_N \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{X^{(0)}\sim q}\Bigl[D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\bigl(q(X^{(N)}\,|\,X^{(0)})\,\Vert\,p(X^{(N)})\bigr)\Bigr].$$
  • Measures proximity of the forward endpoint to the Gaussian prior.
  • No $\theta$ dependence: constant from the optimizer's view.

$L_N \to 0$ by VP schedule design.

Ignored during training.

$L_{n-1}$: Match Each Reverse Step

$$L_{n-1} \;=\; \mathbb{E}_q\Bigl[D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\bigl(q(X^{(n-1)}\,|\,X^{(n)}, X^{(0)})\,\Vert\,p_\theta(X^{(n-1)}\,|\,X^{(n)})\bigr)\Bigr].$$
  • Both sides are Gaussian: KL available in closed form.
  • The actual training signal — every reverse step.
  • $L_0 = \mathbb{E}_q[-\log p_\theta(X^{(0)}\,|\,X^{(1)})]$: boundary term, negligible, dropped.

Exercise. Show $D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\bigl(\mathcal{N}(\mu_0,\sigma^2 I)\,\Vert\,\mathcal{N}(\mu_1,\sigma^2 I)\bigr) = \tfrac{1}{2\sigma^2}\|\mu_1-\mu_0\|^2$.

Target Posterior Is Exactly Gaussian

Forward chain given $X^{(0)}$ (closed form, from one-shot rule):

$$q\bigl(X^{(n)}\,|\,X^{(0)}\bigr) \;=\; \mathcal{N}\bigl(\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_n}\,X^{(0)},\; 1-\bar{\alpha}_n\bigr).$$

Then the reverse posterior conditioned on $X^{(0)}$ is

$$q\bigl(X^{(n-1)}\,|\,X^{(n)}, X^{(0)}\bigr) \;=\; \mathcal{N}\bigl(\mu_n(X^{(n)}, X^{(0)}),\; \beta_n\bigr).$$

Exact equality — no small-$\beta_n$ approximation.

Conditioning on $X^{(0)}$ is what makes it Gaussian; $q(X^{(n-1)}\,|\,X^{(n)})$ alone is only approximately so.

Bayes Derivation

Conditioning on $X^{(0)}$ throughout, Bayes gives

$$q\bigl(X^{(n-1)}\,|\,X^{(n)}, X^{(0)}\bigr) \;\propto\; q\bigl(X^{(n)}\,|\,X^{(n-1)}\bigr)\,\cdot\,q\bigl(X^{(n-1)}\,|\,X^{(0)}\bigr).$$
  • $q(X^{(n)}\,|\,X^{(n-1)}) = \mathcal{N}\bigl(\sqrt{1-\beta_n}\,X^{(n-1)},\,\beta_n\bigr)$ — Gaussian in $X^{(n-1)}$.
  • $q(X^{(n-1)}\,|\,X^{(0)}) = \mathcal{N}\bigl(\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_{n-1}}\,X^{(0)},\,1-\bar{\alpha}_{n-1}\bigr)$ — Gaussian in $X^{(n-1)}$.
  • Product of Gaussians in the same variable $\Rightarrow$ Gaussian.

Complete the square in $X^{(n-1)}$ for mean $\mu_n(X^{(n)},X^{(0)})$ and variance $\beta_n$.

Not Ordinary Mean Matching

Same variance $\beta_n$ on both sides — KL collapses to a squared-mean mismatch:

$$L_{n-1} \;=\; \frac{1}{2\beta_n}\,\mathbb{E}_q\bigl[\bigl\|\mu_n(X^{(n)}, X^{(0)}) - \mu_\theta(X^{(n)}, n)\bigr\|^2\bigr].$$

Asymmetric inputs: $\mu_n$ sees $X^{(0)}$; $\mu_\theta$ does not.

Optimal $\mu_\theta(X^{(n)}) = \mathbb{E}_q[\mu_n(X^{(n)}, X^{(0)})\,|\,X^{(n)}]$ — exactly the score (Tweedie).

Rigorous link to score matching in Lecture 3.

Mean Matching $\to$ Score Matching

The conditional score is closed-form:

$$\partial_{X^{(n)}} \log q\bigl(X^{(n)}\,|\,X^{(0)}\bigr) \;=\; -\,\frac{X^{(n)} - \sqrt{\bar\alpha_n}\,X^{(0)}}{1-\bar\alpha_n}.$$

Substituting Bayes-derived $\mu_n$ and $\mu_\theta = (X^{(n)}+\beta_n s_\theta)/\sqrt{1-\beta_n}$:

$$L_{n-1} \;=\; \frac{\beta_n}{2(1-\beta_n)}\,\mathbb{E}_q\!\left\|\partial_{X^{(n)}}\log q(X^{(n)}\,|\,X^{(0)}) \;-\; s_\theta(X^{(n)}, n)\right\|^2.$$

$\epsilon$ and Score: Same Vector

$$\nabla_{X^{(n)}}\log q\bigl(X^{(n)}\,|\,X^{(0)}\bigr) \;=\; -\,\frac{X^{(n)}-\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_n}\,X^{(0)}}{1-\bar{\alpha}_n} \;=\; -\,\frac{\epsilon}{\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_n}}.$$
$\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_n}\,X^{(0)}$ $X^{(n)}$ noise: $+\,\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_n}\,\epsilon$ score: $-\,\epsilon\,/\,\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_n}$

Anti-parallel, scaled by $\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_n}$.

$\epsilon$-Reparameterization

Forward identity: $X^{(n)} \stackrel{(d)}{=} \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_n}\,X^{(0)} + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_n}\,\epsilon_n$ with $\epsilon_n \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$.

Switch the network from score to noise prediction:

$$s_\theta(X^{(n)}, n) \;=\; -\,\frac{\epsilon_\theta(X^{(n)}, n)}{\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_n}}.$$

Predicting the noise $\epsilon$ is predicting the score, up to a known scale.

$\epsilon$-Prediction Loss

Substitute the score-noise identity into $L_{n-1}$:

$$L_{n-1} \;=\; \frac{\beta_n}{2(1-\beta_n)(1-\bar{\alpha}_n)}\,\bigl\|\epsilon_n - \epsilon_\theta\!\bigl(\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_n}\,X^{(0)} + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_n}\,\epsilon_n,\; n\bigr)\bigr\|^2.$$

$\epsilon_\theta$ predicts the Gaussian noise mixed into $X^{(0)}$.

Plain regression on $(X^{(n)}, n) \mapsto \epsilon$.

04

Training and Sampling

$\epsilon$-prediction loss in practice

Training Algorithm

DDPM Training — repeat until converged
  1. Sample $X^{(0)} \sim p_{\text{data}}$.
  2. Sample $n \sim \text{Unif}\{1, \ldots, N\}$.
  3. Sample $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$.
  4. Form $X^{(n)} = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_n}\,X^{(0)} + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_n}\,\epsilon$.
  5. Gradient step on $\tilde{\lambda}_n\,\bigl\|\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(X^{(n)}, n)\bigr\|^2$.

Original DDPM: $\tilde{\lambda}_n = 1$ (unweighted).

Sampling Algorithm

DDPM Sampling
  1. Sample $X^{(N)} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$.
  2. For $n = N, N-1, \ldots, 1$:
    1. Sample $Z^{(n)} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$.
    2. $X^{(n-1)} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-\beta_n}}\!\left(X^{(n)} - \dfrac{\beta_n}{\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_n}}\,\epsilon_\theta(X^{(n)}, n)\right) + \sqrt{\beta_n}\,Z^{(n)}$.
  3. Return $X^{(0)}$.

Three Approximation Errors

Forward $q$: $X^{(0)}$ $\to$ $X^{(1)}$ $\to$ $\cdots$ $\to$ $X^{(N-1)}$ $\to$ $X^{(N)}$
Reverse $p_\theta$: $\bar{X}^{(0)}$ $\leftarrow$ $\bar{X}^{(1)}$ $\leftarrow$ $\cdots$ $\leftarrow$ $\bar{X}^{(N-1)}$ $\leftarrow$ $\bar{X}^{(N)}$

① Gaussian reverse

$p_\theta \approx \mathcal{N}$ needs $\beta_n$ small.

② Imperfect score

$\epsilon_\theta \neq \epsilon$. Errors compound.

③ Prior mismatch

$\bar{\alpha}_N \not\to 0$ exactly.

Lecture Recap

  • VP forward: $X^{(n)} = \sqrt{1-\beta_n}\,X^{(n-1)} + \sqrt{\beta_n}\,Z^{(n)}$. Unit variance preserved.
  • Terminal: converges to $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ when $\bar{\alpha}_n \to 0$ (MGF proof).
  • Reverse: approximately Gaussian, mean given by the score function.
  • VLB: $L_N$ (constant) $+\ \sum L_{n-1}$ (matching) $+\ L_0$ (boundary).
  • Matching term collapses to $\epsilon$-prediction regression.
