01

Beyond $f$-Divergence

Energy models and the score function

Why Another Divergence?

Every $f$-divergence reads the ratio $p(x)/q(x)$, requiring normalized densities.

Many useful models are specified up to an unknown constant:

$$f(x) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{Z}\,e^{\phi(x)}, \qquad Z = \int e^{\phi(x)}\,dx.$$

$Z$ is high-dimensional, generally intractable. Need a divergence that ignores $Z$.

Use $\nabla_x \log f$ instead of $f$: the gradient kills the constant.

Energy-Based Density

Definition (energy-based pdf).

A pdf on $\mathbb{R}^K$ is energy-based if

$$f(\mathbf{x}) \;=\; \frac{1}{Z}\,e^{\phi(\mathbf{x})}, \qquad Z = \int_{\mathbb{R}^K} e^{\phi(\mathbf{x})}\,d\mathbf{x},$$

for some integrable $e^{\phi}$. Includes every exponential family; covers Markov random fields, Boltzmann machines, neural energy models.

Difficulty. $Z$ requires integrating in $\mathbb{R}^K$; closed form only for special $\phi$ (Gaussian, exponential).

Score Function

Definition (score).

For a differentiable pdf $f$ on $\mathbb{R}^K$,

$$s_f(\mathbf{x}) \;=\; \nabla_{\mathbf{x}}\log f(\mathbf{x}) \quad \text{(taken } = \mathbf{0} \text{ where } f = 0\text{).}$$

Energy form, $Z$ disappears: $\;s_f(\mathbf{x}) = \nabla_{\mathbf{x}}[\phi(\mathbf{x}) - \log Z] = \nabla_{\mathbf{x}}\phi(\mathbf{x})$. The score never touches $Z$.

Example — Gaussian Score

For $f(\mathbf{x}) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x};\boldsymbol\mu,\sigma^2 I)$,

$$\log f(\mathbf{x}) \;=\; -\tfrac{1}{2\sigma^2}\|\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol\mu\|^2 + \text{const}, \qquad s_f(\mathbf{x}) \;=\; -\tfrac{1}{\sigma^2}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol\mu).$$

The score is a linear "pull to the mean" with stiffness $1/\sigma^2$. Same role a force field plays in physics.

Score, not density, is the right object when normalization is intractable.

02

Fisher Divergence

A score-based distance between distributions

Overview — Where We Are Going

Goal: a divergence that compares scores, not densities.

  • Lemma. Score determines the pdf (up to measure-zero).
  • Definition. Fisher divergence $=$ expected squared score gap.
  • Properties. Non-negative, asymmetric, well-defined on energy models.
  • Drawback. Direct minimization needs $\nabla\log p_{\mathrm{data}}$, which is intractable. Section 03 fixes this.

Lemma — Score Identifies the Density

Lemma.

If $f, g$ are differentiable pdfs on $\mathbb{R}^K$ with $s_f(\mathbf{x}) = s_g(\mathbf{x})$ almost everywhere, then $f(\mathbf{x}) = g(\mathbf{x})$ almost everywhere.

Proof. $s_f = s_g$ means $\nabla\log(f/g)=\mathbf{0}$, so $f(\mathbf{x})/g(\mathbf{x}) = c$ a.e. Both integrate to $1$, so $c=1$. $\quad\blacksquare$

Comparing scores is a faithful surrogate for comparing densities.

Definition — Fisher Divergence

Definition (Hyvärinen, 2005).

For differentiable pdfs $f, g$ on $\mathbb{R}^K$,

$$D_F(f\,\Vert\,g) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,\int f(\mathbf{x})\,\bigl\|\,s_f(\mathbf{x}) - s_g(\mathbf{x})\,\bigr\|_2^2\,d\mathbf{x}.$$

Equivalently $D_F(f\,\Vert\,g) = \tfrac12\,\mathbb{E}_f\!\bigl[\,\|\nabla\log f - \nabla\log g\|^2\,\bigr]$.

Hyvärinen, "Estimation of Non-Normalized Statistical Models by Score Matching", JMLR 2005.

Theorem — Properties of Fisher Divergence

Theorem.

  1. $D_F(f\,\Vert\,g) \ge 0$, with equality iff $f = g$ almost everywhere.
  2. Asymmetric in general: $D_F(f\,\Vert\,g) \ne D_F(g\,\Vert\,f)$.
  3. Scaling. If $Y = aX$ with $a\ne 0$, then $D_F(f_Y\,\Vert\,g_Y) = \tfrac{1}{a^2}\,D_F(f_X\,\Vert\,g_X)$.
  4. Gaussian comparator. If $g = \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ and $X\sim f$,
$$D_F(f\,\Vert\,g) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,\mathbb{E}_f\!\Bigl[\bigl\|X + \nabla\log f(X)\bigr\|^2\Bigr].$$

Proof — Non-Negativity and Scaling

(1) Non-negativity. Integrand is a squared norm weighted by $f\ge 0$. Zero iff scores agree $f$-a.e.; combined with the Lemma, iff $f=g$ a.e.

(3) Scaling. $Y=aX$ gives $f_Y(y) = \tfrac{1}{|a|}f_X(y/a)$. Differentiating:

$$\nabla_y \log f_Y(y) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{a}\,\nabla_x\log f_X(y/a).$$

Substitute into $D_F$, change variables $\mathbf{y} = a\mathbf{x}$ (Jacobian $|a|^K$ cancels), pull out $1/a^2$ from the squared score difference. $\quad\blacksquare$

03

Denoising Score Matching

Gaussian smoothing makes the score tractable

The Problem — Direct Score Matching is Intractable

Match $s_\theta$ to the data score:

$$\theta^* \;=\; \arg\min_\theta\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,\mathbb{E}_{p_{\mathrm{data}}}\!\bigl[\,\|s_\theta(X) - \nabla\log p_{\mathrm{data}}(X)\|^2\,\bigr].$$

The data score $\nabla\log p_{\mathrm{data}}$ is unknown. Hyvärinen 2005 gave a partial fix (integration by parts) requiring second derivatives.

Vincent 2011: smooth the data with Gaussian noise; the new score becomes a tractable conditional expectation.

Setup — Add Gaussian Noise

Let $X\sim p_{\mathrm{data}}$ and $Z\sim\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\sigma^2 I)$ independent. Define

$$\mathbf{Y} \;=\; \mathbf{X} + \mathbf{Z}.$$

$f_{\mathrm{data}}(\mathbf{y})$ is the convolution; always differentiable because of the Gaussian smoothing, with score

$$\nabla_{\mathbf{y}} \log f_{\mathrm{data}}(\mathbf{y}) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{f_{\mathrm{data}}(\mathbf{y})}\,\nabla_{\mathbf{y}} \int f_{\mathrm{data}}(\mathbf{x})\, \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{y};\mathbf{x},\sigma^2 I)\,d\mathbf{x}.$$

Goal: learn $s_\theta(\mathbf{y}) \approx \nabla\log f_{\mathrm{data}}(\mathbf{y})$ using samples $(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{y}_i)$.

Theorem — Denoising Score Matching

Theorem (Vincent, 2011).

Up to an additive constant independent of $\theta$,

$$D_F\!\bigl(f_{\mathrm{data}}(\mathbf{y})\,\Vert\,f_\theta(\mathbf{y})\bigr) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,\mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}}\!\left[\,\Bigl\|\,\tfrac{\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}}{\sigma^2} \;-\; s_\theta(\mathbf{y})\,\Bigr\|^2\,\right] + c.$$

The expectation is over $(\mathbf{X},\mathbf{Y}) = (\mathbf{X}, \mathbf{X}+\mathbf{Z})$ with $\mathbf{X}\sim p_{\mathrm{data}}$, $\mathbf{Z}\sim\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\sigma^2 I)$.

Vincent, Score matching $\equiv$ denoising AE, Neural Comp. 2011.

Intuition — The Denoising Direction

The target $(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y})/\sigma^2$ is the score of the Gaussian kernel:

$$\nabla_{\mathbf{y}}\log\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{y};\,\mathbf{x},\,\sigma^2 I) \;=\; \frac{\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}}{\sigma^2}.$$

Points from noisy $\mathbf{y}$ back toward clean $\mathbf{x}$, scaled by $1/\sigma^2$.

The regression target is the denoising direction: "which way is clean data, and how far."

Intuition — Tweedie's Bridge

The marginal score is a posterior average of per-sample directions:

$$\nabla_{\mathbf{y}}\log f_{\mathrm{data}}(\mathbf{y}) \;=\; \mathbb{E}\!\left[\frac{\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}}{\sigma^2}\;\middle|\;\mathbf{y}\right].$$

Tweedie's formula. Posterior mean of conditional score $=$ marginal score.

MSE over data pairs $(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})$ automatically averages the per-sample targets into the correct marginal score.

Proof — Step 1 (Expand the Square)

Expand $D_F$:

$$\begin{aligned} D_F\!\bigl(f_{\mathrm{data}}\,\Vert\,f_\theta\bigr) &= \tfrac12 \int f_{\mathrm{data}}(\mathbf{y})\,\bigl\|\nabla\log f_{\mathrm{data}}(\mathbf{y}) - s_\theta(\mathbf{y})\bigr\|^2\,d\mathbf{y} \\ &= \underbrace{\tfrac12 \int f_{\mathrm{data}}\,\|\nabla\log f_{\mathrm{data}}\|^2}_{c_1\,(\theta\text{-free})} \\ &\quad + \tfrac12 \int f_{\mathrm{data}}\,\|s_\theta\|^2\,d\mathbf{y} - \int f_{\mathrm{data}}\,\bigl(\nabla\log f_{\mathrm{data}}\bigr)^{\!\top} s_\theta\,d\mathbf{y}. \end{aligned}$$

The first term is independent of $\theta$; absorb into $c$. The hard term is the cross term.

Proof — Step 2 (Rewrite the Cross Term)

Use $f_{\mathrm{data}}(\mathbf{y})\,\nabla\log f_{\mathrm{data}}(\mathbf{y}) = \nabla f_{\mathrm{data}}(\mathbf{y})$, then condition on $\mathbf{X}$:

$$\begin{aligned} \int f_{\mathrm{data}}(\mathbf{y})\,\bigl(\nabla\log f_{\mathrm{data}}\bigr)^{\!\top} s_\theta\,d\mathbf{y} &= \int \bigl(\nabla f_{\mathrm{data}}(\mathbf{y})\bigr)^{\!\top} s_\theta(\mathbf{y})\,d\mathbf{y} \\ &= \int \Bigl[\nabla_{\mathbf{y}}\!\!\int p_{\mathrm{data}}(\mathbf{x})\,\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{y};\mathbf{x},\sigma^2 I)\,d\mathbf{x}\Bigr]^{\!\top} s_\theta\,d\mathbf{y} \\ &= \iint p_{\mathrm{data}}(\mathbf{x})\,\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{y};\mathbf{x},\sigma^2 I)\,\tfrac{\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}}{\sigma^2}^{\!\top}\!s_\theta(\mathbf{y})\,d\mathbf{x}\,d\mathbf{y}. \end{aligned}$$

Used $\nabla_{\mathbf{y}}\log\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{y};\mathbf{x},\sigma^2 I) = (\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y})/\sigma^2$.

Proof — Step 3 (Assemble)

Recombine the $\|s_\theta\|^2$ term (also a $p_{\mathrm{data}}\,\mathcal{N}$ expectation by marginalization):

$$\begin{aligned} D_F &= c_1 + \tfrac12\iint p_{\mathrm{data}}\mathcal{N}\,\|s_\theta(\mathbf{y})\|^2 - \iint p_{\mathrm{data}}\mathcal{N}\,\tfrac{\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}}{\sigma^2}^{\!\top}\!s_\theta(\mathbf{y}) \\ &= c + \tfrac12\iint p_{\mathrm{data}}(\mathbf{x})\,\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{y};\mathbf{x},\sigma^2 I)\,\Bigl\|\tfrac{\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}}{\sigma^2} - s_\theta(\mathbf{y})\Bigr\|^2 d\mathbf{x}\,d\mathbf{y}. \end{aligned}$$

$c = c_1 - \tfrac{1}{2}\iint p_{\mathrm{data}}\mathcal{N}\,\|(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y})/\sigma^2\|^2$ is also $\theta$-free. $\quad\blacksquare$

Recap — the Denoising Form

The training objective becomes a conditional MSE on $\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}$:

$$\theta^* \;=\; \arg\min_\theta\;\mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}}\!\left[\,\Bigl\|\tfrac{\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}}{\sigma^2} - s_\theta(\mathbf{y})\Bigr\|^2\,\right].$$
  • Both sides are known from data: sample $\mathbf{x}\sim p_{\mathrm{data}}$, $\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{x}+\sigma\mathbf{z}$.
  • No partition function. No second derivatives.
  • Reduces score matching to least-squares denoising: the practical recipe of every score-based generative model.

Recap

Three families of divergence

Three Divergence Families

$$\begin{array}{l|c|c} \text{Family} & \text{Sees} & \text{Drives} \\\hline \text{$f$-divergence} & \text{ratio } p/q & \text{ML, VI, ELBO, GAN, MI bounds} \\ \text{Fisher} & \text{score } \nabla\log p & \text{Score matching, diffusion, EBM} \\ \text{Wasserstein} & \text{transport cost} & \text{WGAN (not this course)} \end{array}$$

Choice of divergence selects which models are tractable to train.

Energy-based and diffusion models exist because Fisher divergence lets us train without normalization.
