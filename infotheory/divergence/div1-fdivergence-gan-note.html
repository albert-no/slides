Divergence 1 · $f$-Divergence and GAN — Notes

Why these two pathologies matter

The Theis–van den Oord–Bethge note (ICLR 2016) makes a sharper version of the "high likelihood, bad samples" claim: in image space, every density estimator with finite support on the data manifold can be mixed with an ambient noise floor and still have likelihood within $\log(1/\alpha)$ of the optimum. So any evaluation that compares models by held-out likelihood alone is asking the wrong question for sample quality.

The overfit pathology is the symmetric failure: a model that memorizes the empirical distribution achieves perfect sample quality (every sample is data) but $-\infty$ held-out likelihood. The two failures sit on opposite sides of the bias–variance trade-off. Both motivate divergences that don't reduce to maximum likelihood.

Conventions in the $f$-divergence definition, in detail

The three conventions in the definition handle the degenerate cases that arise on supports of $p$ and $q$ that don't agree:

  • $0 \cdot f(0/0) = 0$ — both densities vanish at $x$; the point contributes nothing.
  • $f(0) = \lim_{t\to 0^+} f(t)$ — for $f(t)=t\log t$ this is $0$; for $f(t)=-\log t$ it's $+\infty$; this is why $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\Vert p)$ blows up whenever $p$ misses mass that $q$ has.
  • $0 \cdot f(a/0) = a \lim_{t\to 0^+} t\,f(a/t) = a\,f'(\infty)$ — controls the contribution at $q(x)=0$, $p(x)>0$. For KL, $f'(\infty)=\infty$, so any such $x$ blows up KL. For TV, $f'(\infty)=1/2$ — finite.

These edge-case conventions are why $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p\Vert q)$ is finite iff $p \ll q$, but $\mathrm{TV}$ is always finite. Important when the model class doesn't cover the data support (a real concern in deep generative models).

The "asymmetry partner" $\widetilde f(t) = t\,f(1/t)$

Given $f$, define $\widetilde f(t) = t\,f(1/t)$. Then $\widetilde f$ is also convex with $\widetilde f(1)=0$, and

\[ D_{\widetilde f}(p\Vert q) = D_f(q\Vert p). \]

Proof: $\sum_x q(x)\widetilde f(p/q) = \sum_x q(x)(p/q)\,f(q/p) = \sum_x p(x)\,f(q/p) = D_f(q\Vert p)$.

So $f(t)=t\log t$ (KL) has partner $\widetilde f(t)=-\log t$ (reverse KL). $f(t)=(t-1)^2$ ($\chi^2$) has partner $\widetilde f(t)=(t-1)^2/t$. TV is self-partnered: $\widetilde f = f$, which is why TV is symmetric.

Pinsker's inequality and the chain $\mathrm{TV} \le \sqrt{\tfrac12 D_{\mathrm{KL}}} \le \sqrt{\tfrac12 \chi^2}$

The three divergences interrelate quantitatively. Pinsker's inequality says $\mathrm{TV}(p,q) \le \sqrt{\tfrac12 D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p\Vert q)}$. A standard Taylor expansion at $p=q$ gives $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p\Vert q) \le \chi^2(p\Vert q)$ (whenever both are small). So

\[ \mathrm{TV}(p,q) \;\le\; \sqrt{\tfrac12 D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p\Vert q)} \;\le\; \sqrt{\tfrac12 \chi^2(p\Vert q)}. \]

For the Bernoulli example in the deck ($p=\mathrm{Bern}(0.9)$, $q=\mathrm{Bern}(0.5)$): $\mathrm{TV}=0.4$, $\sqrt{\tfrac12 D_{\mathrm{KL}}}=\sqrt{0.184}\approx 0.43$, $\sqrt{\tfrac12\chi^2}=\sqrt{0.32}\approx 0.57$. Chain confirmed numerically.

Hockey-stick divergence and differential privacy

For $\gamma\ge 1$ and $\delta\in[0,1]$, a randomized mechanism $\mathcal{M}$ is $(\varepsilon,\delta)$-DP if and only if

\[ E_{e^\varepsilon}\!\bigl(\mathcal{M}(D)\,\Vert\,\mathcal{M}(D')\bigr) \;\le\; \delta \]

for every pair of neighbouring datasets $D \sim D'$. The hockey-stick literally measures "how often does the privacy loss exceed $\varepsilon$, weighted by the excess". Composition theorems for DP are easier in this language than in the original $(\varepsilon,\delta)$ form (Kairouz, Oh, Viswanath 2015; Balle and Wang 2018).

See privacy/dp/DP-FL.html for the DP track; that deck uses the hockey-stick formulation when stating advanced-composition results.

Why JS is bounded above by $\log 2$

Each KL term in $D_{\mathrm{JS}}(p\Vert q) = D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p\Vert m) + D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\Vert m)$, with $m=(p+q)/2$, is bounded by $\log 2$:

\[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p\Vert m) = \sum_x p(x)\log\frac{p(x)}{(p(x)+q(x))/2} \le \sum_x p(x)\log\frac{p(x)}{p(x)/2} = \log 2. \]

The inequality is $q(x)\ge 0$. Equality is approached when $p,q$ have disjoint supports. So $D_{\mathrm{JS}}(p\Vert q) \in [0, 2\log 2]$ — bounded, unlike KL.

Some texts normalize JS to lie in $[0,\log 2]$ by halving the definition: $\tfrac12 D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p\Vert m) + \tfrac12 D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\Vert m)$. The deck uses Goodfellow's convention (sum without the half) so that the GAN theorem reads $V = 2D_{\mathrm{JS}} - 2\log 2$ rather than $V = D_{\mathrm{JS}} - \log 2$.

Why we proved DPI through joint $\ge$ marginal

The cleanest proof of DPI for $f$-divergences proceeds by sandwich: the joint $D_f(p(x,y)\Vert q(x,y))$ is between the marginal-only and conditional-only values. Two equalities and one Jensen, no calculus.

This is the same shape as the DPI proof for $D_{\mathrm{KL}}$ via mutual information ($I(X;Y) = I(X;f(Y))$ for invertible $f$, etc.) but works without any KL-specific machinery. The argument generalizes verbatim to integral form for densities.

The $f$-GAN minimax in detail

Plugging the variational representation $D_f(p\Vert q) = \sup_T\{\mathbb{E}_p T - \mathbb{E}_q f^*(T)\}$ into a generative training loop:

\[ \min_\theta\, D_f(p_{\mathrm{data}}\Vert p_\theta) \;=\; \min_\theta\,\sup_T\,\bigl\{\mathbb{E}_{p_{\mathrm{data}}}[T(X)] - \mathbb{E}_{p_\theta}[f^*(T(X))]\bigr\}. \]

For $f(t)=t\log t$ (KL): $f^*(s)=e^{s-1}$, recovering the NWJ-style critic. For $f(t)=(t-1)^2$ ($\chi^2$): $f^*(s) = s^2/4 + s$, a quadratic critic. For $f(t)=t\log\tfrac{2t}{t+1}+\log\tfrac{2}{t+1}$ (JS): $f^*(s) = -\log(2-e^s)$, recovering Goodfellow's GAN after a reparameterization.

This is also exactly the same machinery as the variational MI lower bounds in infotheory/mi/mi1-bounds.html — DV is the KL variational form, NWJ is its tightened linear surrogate. The "MI estimation" and "GAN training" frames are two views of the same dual representation.

Practical instability of vanilla GAN training

The clean JS theorem hides the practical reality. When the supports of $p_{\mathrm{data}}$ and $p_\theta$ are nearly disjoint (typical at initialization in image space), $D_{\mathrm{JS}}$ saturates at $\log 2$ and its gradient with respect to $\theta$ vanishes. The discriminator wins decisively, no signal flows to the generator, training stalls.

This is the motivation for Wasserstein GAN (Arjovsky, Chintala, Bottou 2017), which replaces JS with the Earth-Mover (1-Wasserstein) distance — not an $f$-divergence, so it lies outside this lecture's family. Wasserstein has a useful gradient even when supports are disjoint.
