01

The Encoder Problem

Shannon-optimal VQ is rate-distortion right and operationally dead.

What a Real Quantizer Must Do

LLM inference: $\sim 10^{10}$ weights, every layer, every forward pass.

  • Encode in $\mathcal{O}(d)$ per block — not $\mathcal{O}(2^{dR})$.
  • Tiny codebook — fits in cache, no GB-scale lookups.
  • Embarrassingly parallel across blocks and rows.
  • MSE close to the rate-distortion optimum.
Compression is not enough. The encoder has to be cheap.

Optimal VQ — What the Encoder Does

Group $d$ scalars into $\mathbf{w}\in\mathbb{R}^d$. Encoder finds the nearest codeword.

$$\mathcal{C} = \{\mathbf{c}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{c}_M\}\subset\mathbb{R}^d, \;\; M = 2^{dR}, \qquad \widehat{\mathbf{w}} \;=\; \arg\min_{k=1,\ldots,M}\,\|\mathbf{w} - \mathbf{c}_k\|^2.$$

Encoder picks index $k$; decoder looks up $\mathbf{c}_k$.

Decoder is trivial (one lookup). The encoder is the wall.

The Codebook Is Gigantic

$M = 2^{dR}$ codewords, each a $d$-dim vector (4-byte floats). Storage $= d \cdot 2^{dR} \cdot 4$ bytes.

$d$ $R$ $M = 2^{dR}$ Codebook size
$2$$4$$256$$2$ KB  fits in L1
$4$$4$$65{,}536$$1$ MB
$6$$4$$16.7 \times 10^6$$400$ MB
$8$$4$$4.3 \times 10^9$$137$ GB  won't fit anywhere

Shannon says $d \to \infty$. Storage says $d$ stops at single digits.

Encoding — A Search You Cannot Run

Per block: scan all $M$ codewords, compute $\|\mathbf{w}-\mathbf{c}_k\|^2$, take the min.

$$\text{Per-block cost} \;=\; \mathcal{O}(d \cdot 2^{dR}) \;\;\text{ops} \;+\; \mathcal{O}(d \cdot 2^{dR}) \;\;\text{memory reads}.$$

For $d=8$, $R=4$, one LLM forward pass over $10^{10}$ weights:

  • $1.25 \times 10^9$ blocks $\times$ $4.3 \times 10^9$ codeword checks.
  • $\approx 5 \times 10^{18}$ operations per layer, per token.
  • Plus: streaming $137$ GB of codebook through the cache.
No clever indexing rescues this — search is fundamentally linear in $M$.

Two Escape Routes

Uniform quantization

Round each coordinate to a fixed grid. No codebook, no search.

Cheap; small MSE penalty vs. optimal VQ.

Lattice codes

Codebook with linear algebraic structure.

Encode in $\mathcal{O}(d)$ via algebra, decode by index.

Both replace the $M$-way search with an algebraic step.
02

Computing in Quantized Space

Uniform quantization preserves linear arithmetic.

Uniform Quantization

Fix a step $\Delta > 0$. Round to the nearest multiple of $\Delta$:

$$\hat x \;=\; Q_\Delta(x) \;=\; \Delta \cdot \mathrm{round}(x/\Delta).$$

Store the integer $n = \mathrm{round}(x/\Delta)$; the decoder multiplies by $\Delta$.

  • Codebook: implicit — just the step $\Delta$.
  • Encode: $\mathcal{O}(1)$ per coordinate, no search.
  • Quantization error: $|x - \hat x| \le \Delta/2$.

The Naive Path — Round-Trip Through Floats

Stored as integers $n_a, n_b$. To add, the natural reflex:

$$n_a \xrightarrow{\text{dequant}} \hat a = n_a\Delta \;\xrightarrow{+}\; \hat a + \hat b \;\xrightarrow{\text{requant}}\; \mathrm{round}\!\left(\tfrac{\hat a + \hat b}{\Delta}\right)\,\Delta.$$
  • Two float multiplies per add.
  • A second rounding $\Rightarrow$ extra error.
Dequantize $\to$ add $\to$ requantize. Slow, and lossy on a step that shouldn't lose anything.

Quantized Values Still Add

If $\hat a = n_a \Delta$ and $\hat b = n_b \Delta$, then

$$\hat a + \hat b \;=\; (n_a + n_b)\Delta \;\in\; \Delta\,\mathbb{Z}.$$

The sum is exactly on the grid — integer add in the index domain, no dequant, no requant.

Integer addition in the index domain $\Leftrightarrow$ exact sum in the value domain.

For weights $\hat w_i = n_i \Delta_w$ and inputs $\hat x_i = m_i \Delta_x$, partial sums stay in integers until the final $\Delta_w \Delta_x$ scale-out.

Quantized Values Multiply (by Integers)

For any integer $k$:

$$k \cdot \hat a \;=\; (k\,n_a)\Delta \;\in\; \Delta\,\mathbb{Z}.$$

Pairwise integer multiply, then scale by $\Delta_w \Delta_x$ at the end:

$$\hat w_i \cdot \hat x_i \;=\; (n_i\,m_i)\,\Delta_w \Delta_x.$$

The inner sum $\sum_i n_i m_i$ runs in integer arithmetic.

Matrix-Vector Product, Integer GEMM

A linear layer with quantized $W$ and quantized activation $x$:

$$\hat y_j \;=\; \sum_i \hat W_{ji}\,\hat x_i \;=\; \Delta_w \Delta_x \sum_i n_{ji}\, m_i.$$

Inner sum: pure integer MAC (multiply-accumulate). GPU INT8 cores run this $\sim 4\times$ faster than FP16 with $\sim 4\times$ less memory traffic.

Uniform quantization is what makes quantized inference fast — not just compact.

The MSE Penalty Is Small

Uniform grid $\Delta \mathbb{Z}^d$ on i.i.d. data: MSE $= \Delta^2/12$ per coord, any $d$.

Best possible at fixed rate (high-$d$ sphere bound): $\sim \Delta^2/(2\pi e)$ per coord.

$$\text{Penalty ratio} \;=\; \frac{1/12}{1/(2\pi e)} \;=\; \frac{\pi e}{6} \;\approx\; 1.42 \;\;(\le 1.53\text{ dB}).$$
$\le 1.53$ dB MSE penalty buys you $\mathcal{O}(d)$ encode and zero codebook RAM.
03

Lattice Codes

A regular grid: linear structure replaces search.

From Cube Grid to Lattice

A lattice $\Lambda$ is the integer span of a basis $\{b_1, \ldots, b_d\}$:

$$\Lambda \;=\; \{\,n_1 b_1 + n_2 b_2 + \cdots + n_d b_d \;:\; n_i \in \mathbb{Z}\,\}.$$

$\mathbb{Z}^2$ — cube basis

$A_2$ — hex basis

Voronoi Cells — Round Beats Square

By translation symmetry, every Voronoi cell has the same shape — one fundamental cell, repeated.

$\mathbb{Z}^2$ — square cell

corner dist $\approx 0.71\,a$

$A_2$ — hex cell

corner dist $\approx 0.62\,a$

Same cell area; smaller worst-case distance $\Rightarrow$ smaller MSE per bit.

Encoding: Round, Don't Search

The lattice's algebra picks the closest point. No list of $M$ codewords is ever consulted.

$\mathbb{Z}^d$. Round each coordinate independently:

$$\widehat{n}_i \;=\; \lfloor x_i + \tfrac12 \rfloor, \quad i = 1, \ldots, d.$$

e.g. $x = (1.7,\, -0.4,\, 2.9,\, 1.1) \;\Rightarrow\; \widehat n = (2,\, 0,\, 3,\, 1)$. Cost: $d$ rounds.

$E_8$. Round $x$ to $\mathbb{Z}^8$ and to $\mathbb{Z}^8 + \tfrac12\mathbf{1}$ (two cosets); return the closer of the two. Cost: $\sim 2d$ rounds.

Decoding: Index $\to$ Codeword

Stored per block: one integer index $\widehat n$ (or $k$). Decoder reconstructs $\widehat{\mathbf{w}}$.

$\mathbb{Z}^d$ — no table

$\widehat{\mathbf{w}} \;=\; \Delta \cdot \widehat n$. Zero codebook RAM.

$E_8 \cap$ ball

Keep only lattice points inside a ball — the shape that matches the typical set of $\mathcal{N}(0, I_8)$.

$\sim 256$ codewords at $R{=}2$, $\sim 1$ KB table. Fits in L1.

Encode: $\mathcal{O}(d)$ algebra. Decode: $\mathcal{O}(1)$ index lookup. Both cheap.

$\mathbb{Z}^8$ Is Just Scalar Quantization

$\mathbb{Z}^8 = \{(n_1,\ldots,n_8) : n_i \in \mathbb{Z}\}$. Round each coordinate independently.

  • Voronoi cell is an 8-D cube — corners stick far from the center.
  • Kissing number $16$ ($\pm e_i$). Sparse for 8 dimensions.
8-D scalar quant. No gain over per-coordinate rounding.

$E_8$ — One Extra Coset Buys a Round Cell

Take $\mathbb{Z}^8$ and add the half-shifted copy (only when all coords sum to an even integer):

$$E_8 \;=\; D_8 \;\cup\; \bigl(D_8 + \tfrac12 \mathbf{1}\bigr), \qquad D_8 = \bigl\{n \in \mathbb{Z}^8 : \textstyle\sum_i n_i \text{ even}\bigr\}.$$
  • Points fall into the gaps left by $\mathbb{Z}^8$ $\Rightarrow$ denser packing.
  • Voronoi cell becomes nearly a ball; corner distance shrinks.
  • Kissing number jumps $16 \to 240$.
Same dim, same encode cost — but cells are round instead of cubic.

Why Dimension 8?

Higher $d$ rounds the Voronoi cell more. But codebook and rounding both get more expensive.

$E_8$ is special

$240$ tangent neighbors, vs. $16$ for $\mathbb{Z}^8$.

Viazovska (2017, Fields Medal): densest sphere packing in $\mathbb{R}^8$.

Why not 16 or 24?

Leech in 24-D is tighter, but encode and table costs blow up.

$E_8$ at $R{=}2$: $M{=}256$, 1 KB. Sweet spot for LLMs.

Viazovska, "The sphere packing problem in dimension 8", Ann. of Math. 2017.

Finite Codebook from a Lattice

Three knobs:

  1. Base lattice $\Lambda$ — e.g. $E_8$. Stored implicitly.
  2. Scale $\Delta$ — distortion variance $\propto \Delta^2$.
  3. Shape $\mathcal{S}$ — intersect with a ball that matches the data.
$$\mathcal{C} \;=\; (\Delta\,\Lambda) \cap \mathcal{S}.$$

For Gaussian-like data $X\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I_d)$, $\|X\|^2 \approx d$ — a ball $\mathcal{S}$ matches the typical set.

Picking the Ball — Match the Typical Set

For $X \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I_d)$, the squared norm concentrates:

$$\|X\|^2 \;\approx\; d\sigma^2 \;\;(\text{std.\ dev.\ } O(\sigma^2\sqrt d)).$$

So pick the ball radius $r = \sigma\sqrt{d\,(1+\epsilon)}$:

  • Almost all data lives inside — tiny clipping loss.
  • Almost no lattice points outside — no wasted codewords.
The ball is just big enough to cover the typical set, no bigger.

Rounding to $E_8$ in $\mathcal{O}(d)$

$E_8 = \mathbb{Z}^8 \;\cup\; (\mathbb{Z}^8 + \tfrac12 \mathbf{1})$. Two cosets, round each, keep the closer.

1. $a \;=\; \mathrm{round}(x)$   ($d$ scalar rounds, $\mathcal{O}(d)$).

2. $b \;=\; \mathrm{round}(x - \tfrac12 \mathbf{1}) + \tfrac12 \mathbf{1}$   ($d$ scalar rounds, $\mathcal{O}(d)$).

3. Return $a$ if $\|x - a\|^2 \le \|x - b\|^2$, else $b$   ($\mathcal{O}(d)$).

Total: $\mathcal{O}(d)$ — no codeword scan, no $2^{dR}$ search.

Same cost as scalar rounding, but the cell is round instead of cubic.

Is It OK to Use a Lattice Codebook?

Encode (the hard part)

Algebraic rounding in $\mathcal{O}(d)$.

For $E_8$: two cosets, $\mathcal{O}(d)$ rounds each, keep the closer.

Output: integer index $k$.

Decode (the easy part)

Lookup $\widehat{\mathbf{w}} = \mathbf{c}_k$ from a small table.

Table size $= |\Lambda \cap \mathcal{S}|$ entries.

$E_8$ at $R=2$: 256 entries, 1 KB. Fits in L1.

Small codebook $+$ cheap encoder $+$ decoder by index. All three.
04

QUIP: Incoherence Processing

Make weights "boring" so a coarse quantizer works.

We Care About Output, Not Weights

Natural objective:

$$\mathbb{E}\bigl\|\widehat W - W\bigr\|_F^2 \quad\text{(weight MSE — easy to measure, wrong target)}.$$

Real objective:

$$\mathbb{E}_{x \sim \text{data}}\,\bigl\|f_{\widehat W}(x) - f_W(x)\bigr\|^2 \quad\text{(output MSE — what we actually want)}.$$

The full network $f$ is nonlinear and intractable to characterize. We need a tractable surrogate.

Surrogate — One Layer at a Time

If every layer's output stays close to the original, the network's output does too.

For one linear layer $y = Wx$, replace $W$ with $\widehat W$. Set the weight error $W_\Delta = \widehat W - W$ (a matrix, not "small change in $x$").

Per-input output error:

$$\bigl\|f_{\widehat W}(x) - f_W(x)\bigr\|^2 \;=\; \bigl\|W_\Delta\,x\bigr\|^2.$$

Average over the data distribution to get a per-layer proxy loss $\ell(\widehat W) = \mathbb{E}_x \|W_\Delta\, x\|^2$.

Next: rewrite $\mathbb{E}_x\|W_\Delta\, x\|^2$ in a form that depends on $W_\Delta$ and a fixed matrix $H$.

The Trace Trick

Expand $\|W_\Delta\, x\|^2$ and pull the expectation inside the trace.

$$\begin{aligned} \|W_\Delta\, x\|^2 &\;\stackrel{(1)}{=}\; (W_\Delta\, x)^\top (W_\Delta\, x) \;=\; x^\top W_\Delta^\top W_\Delta\, x \\ &\;\stackrel{(2)}{=}\; \mathrm{tr}\!\bigl(x^\top W_\Delta^\top W_\Delta\, x\bigr) \;\stackrel{(3)}{=}\; \mathrm{tr}\!\bigl(W_\Delta\, x x^\top\, W_\Delta^\top\bigr). \end{aligned}$$

Now take the expectation. $W_\Delta$ is non-random; pull it out:

$$\mathbb{E}_x\,\|W_\Delta\, x\|^2 \;\stackrel{(4)}{=}\; \mathrm{tr}\!\bigl(W_\Delta\, \mathbb{E}_x[xx^\top]\, W_\Delta^\top\bigr) \;=\; \mathrm{tr}\!\bigl(W_\Delta\, H\, W_\Delta^\top\bigr).$$

(1) definition $\|v\|^2 = v^\top v$. (2) scalar = its own trace. (3) cyclic. (4) linearity, $W_\Delta$ outside.

The Proxy Loss

Collecting, with $W_\Delta = \widehat W - W$:

$$\ell(\widehat W) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_x\,\|W_\Delta\, x\|^2 \;=\; \mathrm{tr}\!\bigl(W_\Delta\, H\, W_\Delta^\top\bigr),$$
$$H \;=\; \mathbb{E}_x[xx^\top] \quad\text{(PSD, fixed by calibration data).}$$

Per-layer Hessian-weighted MSE.

Depends on $\widehat W$ only through $W_\Delta$. Decouples across rows.

Chee et al., "QuIP", NeurIPS 2023.

What Is $H$?

$H$ is the input covariance from calibration data — not a loss Hessian.

$$H \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \text{calib}}\bigl[\,x x^\top\,\bigr] \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}, \;\; \text{PSD}.$$

Compute once from a few hundred forward passes on a calibration corpus — same $H$ used by every quantization step of the layer.

$H$ encodes which input directions matter to the layer's output.

The Hessian Tells Us What Matters

Diagonal $H_{ii} = \mathbb{E}[x_i^2]$: variance of input coordinate $i$.

Off-diagonal $H_{ij} = \mathbb{E}[x_i x_j]$: how inputs correlate.

  • Error along high-variance directions of $H$ hurts the output more.
  • Error along directions with $H \approx 0$ is invisible to the output.
A good quantizer should be careful where $H$ is large and sloppy where $H$ is small.

The Idea — Predict and Correct

Quantize columns of $W$ in order $1, 2, \ldots, n$ — one at a time.

Before rounding column $k$, predict it from the already-rounded $\widehat W_1, \ldots, \widehat W_{k-1}$.

Round the residual, not the raw column.

  • Correlated inputs $\Rightarrow$ residual is small $\Rightarrow$ less MSE.
  • Same idea as DPCM in speech/image coding (differential PCM).
LDLQ = DPCM for columns of $W$, with prediction tuned to $H$.

The Linear-Feedback Family

Round columns sequentially with a linear correction from past errors:

$$\widehat W_k \;=\; Q\!\left(\,W_k \;+\; \sum_{j\,<\,k}\,(W_j - \widehat W_j)\,U_{jk}\,\right), \qquad k = 1, \ldots, n.$$

Stack the coefficients into a strictly upper-triangular $U \in \mathbb R^{n\times n}$:

$$\widehat W \;=\; Q\!\bigl(\,W \;+\; (W - \widehat W)\,U\,\bigr).$$
  • $U = 0$  $\Rightarrow$  plain per-coordinate round-to-nearest.
  • Goal. Choose $U$ (as a function of $H$, not $W$) to minimize the proxy.

Why "Upper Triangular"?

The feedback must be causal: when rounding column $k$, only $\widehat W_1, \ldots, \widehat W_{k-1}$ are known.

$$U_{jk} \neq 0 \iff j \;<\; k.$$

Equivalently, $U$ is strictly upper triangular — $(W - \widehat W)\,U$ at column $k$ depends only on columns $1, \ldots, k-1$.

"Only past errors influence the next decision."

Quantization Residual $\eta$

Recall the goal — minimize the per-layer proxy loss:

$$\ell(\widehat W) \;=\; \mathrm{tr}\!\bigl((\widehat W - W)\,H\,(\widehat W - W)^\top\bigr).$$

Inside the linear-feedback update, let $z = W + (W - \widehat W)\,U$ be the pre-rounded matrix. The per-coordinate quantization error is

$$\eta_{ij} \;=\; Q(z_{ij}) - z_{ij}, \qquad |\eta_{ij}| \;\le\; \tfrac12.$$

So $\widehat W = z + \eta$. We will express the proxy in terms of $\eta$.

Proxy in Feedback Form

From $\widehat W = W + (W - \widehat W)\,U + \eta$, rearrange:

$$(\widehat W - W)\,(I + U) \;=\; \eta \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad \widehat W - W \;=\; \eta\,(I + U)^{-1}.$$

Plug into $\ell(\widehat W) = \mathrm{tr}\bigl((\widehat W - W)\,H\,(\widehat W - W)^\top\bigr)$:

$$\ell(\widehat W) \;=\; \mathrm{tr}\!\bigl(\,\eta\,(I+U)^{-1}\,H\,(I+U)^{-\top}\,\eta^\top\,\bigr).$$
Same proxy loss — rewritten as a quadratic in the quantization error $\eta$. Which $U$ makes the middle matrix small?

LDL of $H$ Cancels the Cross-Terms

$H$ is PSD, so it has an upper-LDL$^\top$ factorization:

$$H \;=\; (I + \bar U)\,D\,(I + \bar U)^\top, \qquad \bar U \text{ str. upper-triangular},\;\; D \succeq 0 \text{ diagonal}.$$

Set $U \leftarrow \bar U$: the $(I+U)^{\pm 1}$ factors collapse against $H$ and the proxy decouples,

$$\ell(\widehat W) \;=\; \mathrm{tr}\!\bigl(\eta\,D\,\eta^\top\bigr) \;=\; \sum_{k=1}^n D_{kk}\,\|\eta_{\,\cdot\, k}\|^2.$$

Cross-terms gone — column $k$ of the quantization error $\eta$ is paid for at rate $D_{kk}$ alone.

LDLQ — Round With Memory

The choice $U \leftarrow \bar U$ defines the LDLQ algorithm.

1. Factor $H = (I + \bar U)\, D\, (I + \bar U)^\top$ once from calibration.

2. For $k = 1, 2, \ldots, n$:

$\circ$  correction   $\;c_k \;=\; \sum_{j\,<\,k}\, (W_j - \widehat W_j)\,\bar U_{jk}$

$\circ$  round         $\;\widehat W_k \;=\; Q\bigl(W_k + c_k\bigr)$   (per-coordinate nearest)

Sequential rounding with linear feedback — DPCM in matrix form.

What $\bar U$ and $D$ Mean

The LDL$^\top$ of $H$ is Gram-Schmidt under the $H$-inner-product:

  • $\bar U_{jk}$ ($j < k$) = best linear predictor coefficient of coord $k$ from coord $j$.
  • $D_{kk}$ = residual variance of coord $k$ after removing the influence of $1, \ldots, k-1$.

$H$ diagonal $\Rightarrow$ $\bar U = 0$.

No prediction possible — LDLQ collapses to round-to-nearest.

$H$ off-diagonal mass $\Rightarrow$ $\bar U \ne 0$.

Subtract the predictable part of column $k$ before rounding.

Worst-Case and Average Proxy Loss

For a rounding rule $A$ (taking $W, H$ and returning $\widehat W$), define two losses:

$$\mathcal{L}_\text{worst}(A, H) \;=\; \sup_{W \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n}}\, \mathbb{E}\,\mathrm{tr}\!\bigl((A(W,H) - W)\,H\,(A(W,H) - W)^\top\bigr),$$
$$\mathcal{L}_\text{avg}(A, H) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{W \sim \text{Unif}[0,1]^{m\times n}}\,\mathrm{tr}\!\bigl((A(W,H) - W)\,H\,(A(W,H) - W)^\top\bigr).$$

Both reduce to $\sum_k D_{kk} \,\mathbb{E}\|\eta_{\cdot k}\|^2$ for LDLQ. With $|\eta_{ij}| \le \tfrac12$:

  • Worst: $\eta_{ij}^2 = \tfrac14$ at $W_{ij} = k+\tfrac12$  $\Rightarrow$  $\mathcal{L}_\text{worst} = \tfrac{m}{4}\,\mathrm{tr}(D)$.
  • Average: $\mathbb{E}\,\eta_{ij}^2 = \tfrac1{12}$ (nearest) or $\tfrac16$ (stochastic)  $\Rightarrow$  $\mathcal{L}_\text{avg} = \tfrac{m}{c}\,\mathrm{tr}(D)$.

LDLQ Is Optimal (Theorem)

Within the linear-feedback family with $U$ chosen as a function of $H$ (not $W$):

Theorem 1 (Chee et al., 2023).

For any PSD $H$ and any rounding rule $A$ in the family,

$$\tfrac{m}{4}\,\mathrm{tr}(D) \;=\; \mathcal{L}_\text{worst}(\text{LDLQ}, H) \;\le\; \mathcal{L}_\text{worst}(A, H),$$
$$\tfrac{m}{c}\,\mathrm{tr}(D) \;=\; \mathcal{L}_\text{avg}(\text{LDLQ}, H) \;\le\; \mathcal{L}_\text{avg}(A, H),$$

$c = 12$ for nearest, $c = 6$ for stochastic. Moreover $\mathrm{tr}(D) \le \mathrm{tr}(H)$, equality iff $H$ diagonal.

Chee, Cai, Kuleshov, De Sa, "QuIP", NeurIPS 2023.

What Theorem 1 Means

  • $\mathrm{tr}(H)$ = total input variance — the cost paid by round-to-nearest.
  • $\mathrm{tr}(D)$ = residual variance after LDL strips cross-coordinate predictability.
  • Gap is strict whenever $H$ has off-diagonal mass.
$$\underbrace{\tfrac{m}{4}\,\mathrm{tr}(H)}_{\text{round-to-nearest}} \quad \text{vs.} \quad \underbrace{\tfrac{m}{4}\,\mathrm{tr}(D)}_{\text{LDLQ}}.$$

Empirically, across every layer of OPT 125m–2.7b, $\;\mathrm{tr}(D)/\mathrm{tr}(H) \le 0.65$ (Chee et al., 2023, Supplement C).

LDLQ wins whenever inputs are correlated — i.e., every real LLM layer.

Why Outliers Wreck Quantization

Uniform quantizer needs a step $\Delta$ that covers the whole range $[-W_{\max}, W_{\max}]$.

With $M = 2^{R}$ bins:

$$\Delta \;=\; \frac{2 W_{\max}}{M} \;\propto\; W_{\max}.$$

One big entry $\Rightarrow$ huge $\Delta$.

Most weights live in a small region — but $\Delta$ is sized for the outlier.

Almost every bit of resolution is wasted.

Incoherence — No Outliers

$W \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n}$ is $\mu$-incoherent if

$$\max_{i,j} |W_{ij}| \;\le\; \mu \cdot \frac{\|W\|_F}{\sqrt{mn}}.$$

RHS is the average magnitude. So the maximum is within a factor $\mu$ of the typical scale.

Incoherent = max-to-typical bounded. Quantizer step $\Delta$ stops being dominated by a few entries.

Analogous definition for the Hessian $H = Q\Lambda Q^\top$: eigenbasis is spread out, $\max_{ij}|Q_{ij}| \le \mu/\sqrt n$.

Conjugation Preserves the Proxy

Two independent random orthogonals:  $U \in O(m)$ rotates outputs,  $V \in O(n)$ rotates inputs.

$$\widetilde W \;=\; U\, W\, V^\top \quad\text{(rows by $U$, columns by $V$).}$$

$H = \mathbb{E}[xx^\top]$ depends only on the input $x$. After rotation $x \to V x$:

$$\widetilde H \;=\; \mathbb{E}\bigl[(Vx)(Vx)^\top\bigr] \;=\; V\, H\, V^\top.$$

$U$ never enters $\widetilde H$ — $H$ has no output index. Cyclic trace then gives

$$\mathrm{tr}\bigl(\widetilde W\,\widetilde H\,\widetilde W^\top\bigr) \;=\; \mathrm{tr}\bigl(W\, H\, W^\top\bigr).$$
Rotation $U, V$ are different objects from LDLQ's $\bar U$ (the upper-triangular factor of $H$).

Random Rotation Erases Outliers

Pick $U, V$ random orthogonal: each entry of $\widetilde W = U W V^\top$ is a weighted sum of all of $W$.

$$\widetilde W_{ij} \;=\; \sum_{k,\ell} U_{ik}\,V_{j\ell}\,W_{k\ell} \;\approx\; \mathcal{N}\!\bigl(0,\, \tfrac{\|W\|_F^2}{mn}\bigr).$$

Outliers diffuse across all entries; QUIP uses Kronecker $V = V_1 \otimes V_2$: polylog-$\mu$, $\Theta(n\sqrt n)$ multiply.

Random rotation turns any $W$ into a "boring" $\widetilde W$ that a coarse quantizer handles. Now run LDLQ on $\widetilde W$ using $\widetilde H$.

QUIP — End to End

  1. Rotate. $\widetilde W = U W V^\top$, $\widetilde H = V H V^\top$ — Kronecker random orthogonals.
  2. Round. LDLQ on $\widetilde W$ using $\widetilde H$.
  3. Inference. Apply $V$ to inputs, $U^\top$ to outputs around the quantized layer.

First viable 2-bit LLM quantization with theoretical guarantees.

QUIP#: Hadamard + Lattice

Faster rotation, ball-shaped codebook.

Two Upgrades Over QUIP

Rotation

Kronecker $\to$ Randomized Hadamard Transform.

$\Theta(n \log n)$ time, sign-only multiplies, tighter incoherence bound.

Codebook

Scalar $\to$ $E_8$ lattice on blocks of 8.

Ball-shaped codebook matches the post-rotation Gaussian data.

Tseng, Chee, Sun, Kuleshov, De Sa, "QuIP#", ICML 2024.

What Is a Hadamard Matrix?

$H_8$

Blue $= +1$,  white $= -1$

$H_n \in \{-1,+1\}^{n\times n}$, $H_n H_n^\top = n I$.

$$H_1 = [1], \;\;\; H_{2n} = \begin{bmatrix} H_n & H_n \\ H_n & -H_n \end{bmatrix}.$$
  • Built recursively (Sylvester).
  • Rows orthogonal $\Rightarrow$ $\tfrac{1}{\sqrt n} H_n$ is a rotation.
  • $H_n x$ in $\Theta(n\log n)$ via FWHT.

Why Hadamard Is Fast Enough

$\frac{1}{\sqrt n} H_n$ is orthogonal — so it's a valid rotation.

Fast Walsh-Hadamard: pair up entries, sum and difference, recurse.

$$H_n x \;\text{in}\; \Theta(n\log n) \;\text{additions} \;\;(\text{no float multiplies}).$$

Cf. Kronecker rotation: $\Theta(n\sqrt n)$ multiplies. Hadamard is asymptotically faster and simpler in hardware.

$\Theta(n\log n)$ sign-only ops — essentially free next to the quantized matmul.

Random Signs Make It Incoherent

Plain $H_n$ alone fails on adversarial $W$ (e.g. $W$ aligned with a Hadamard column). Fix: random sign flips.

$$\widetilde W \;=\; \bigl(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt m} H_m S_U\bigr)\,W\,\bigl(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt n} H_n S_V\bigr)^\top, \quad S_U, S_V \sim \mathrm{Unif}\{\pm 1\}^{\text{diag}}.$$

Each entry $\widetilde W_{ij}$ is a sign-randomized sum of $mn$ terms $\Rightarrow$ CLT.

$$\widetilde W_{ij} \;\approx\; \mathcal{N}\!\bigl(0,\,\tfrac{\|W\|_F^2}{mn}\bigr), \qquad \mu \;=\; \mathcal{O}\!\bigl(\sqrt{\log(mn/\delta)}\bigr).$$
RHT gives $\sqrt{\log}$ incoherence, vs. $\log^2$ for Kronecker. Strictly tighter.

$E_8$ Codebook — Decoded by Index

Group rotated weights into blocks of 8. Each block quantizes to one of $\sim 256$ codewords from a scaled $E_8$ lattice (called E8P).

  • Encode: $E_8$ nearest point in $\mathcal{O}(8)$ — round to $\mathbb{Z}^8$ and the half-shift coset, pick the closer.
  • Decode: table lookup by 8-bit index. 1 KB table, fits in L1.
  • Rate: 8 bits / 8 weights $= 1$ bit / weight, plus a sign-flip bit $\to$ 2 bits / weight effective.
The codebook is allowed to be a lattice because both encode and decode are cheap.

Scalar LDLQ vs. BlockLDLQ

QUIP rounds one column at a time. To use $E_8$, round $g=8$ columns at once.

Scalar LDLQ  (QUIP, $g = 1$)

$$\widehat W_k \;=\; Q\bigl(W_k + R_{1:k-1}\,\bar U_{\,\cdot,k}\bigr), \qquad R_j = W_j - \widehat W_j.$$

Per-coordinate round.

BlockLDLQ  (QUIP#, $g = 8$)

$$\widehat W_k \;=\; Q_{E_8}\bigl(W_k + R_{1:k-g}\,A_k\bigr).$$

Group of 8 rounded by $E_8$ nearest-point.

Same predict-and-correct — only the per-step rounder changes.

BlockLDLQ — Bound

$A_k$ from a block LDL$^\top$ of $H$ (block size $g$).

Theorem 4.1 (Tseng et al., 2024).

$$\mathbb{E}\,\ell(\widehat W) \;\le\; \frac{g\,m\,\mu^2}{n}\,\sigma^2\,\mathrm{tr}(H^{1/2})^2.$$

Same $\mathrm{tr}(H) \to \mathrm{tr}(H^{1/2})^2/n$ improvement as scalar QUIP — now with vector quantization.

Two-Faced Effective Codebook

To the data

Random rotation + ball-shaped $E_8$ codebook.

Looks like an i.i.d. random Gaussian codebook (Shannon-optimal).

To the hardware

Rigid $E_8$ grid. 1 KB table fits in L1.

Encode $\mathcal{O}(8)$, rotate $\Theta(n\log n)$.

Rate-distortion benefits without the exponential cost of unstructured VQ.

Recap

  • Wall: optimal VQ has $2^{dR}$ codewords and a $2^{dR}$-way search.
  • Linear quantization: add and multiply stay on the grid $\Rightarrow$ integer GEMM.
  • Lattices: linear structure replaces search; $E_8$ is the round one in 8D.
  • QUIP: output-aware proxy $+$ random rotation $+$ LDLQ feedback.
  • QUIP#: RHT $+$ $E_8$ BlockLDLQ. Sub-2-bit per weight in practice.
