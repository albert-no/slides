Recall — Why Memorization Matters

  • Bartz v. Anthropic — $1.5B settlement (Sept 2025) over books used to train Claude
  • NYT v. OpenAI — verbatim article reproduction in extraction attacks
  • Three stakeholders: privacy, copyright, security — all read the same signal

Memorization is the empirical signature of training-data liability for LLMs.

Brittain, "Authors' lawyers in 1.5B Anthropic settlement seek 300M", Reuters, December 4, 2025.

Training Objective — Next-Token Prediction

Autoregressive factorization of the corpus likelihood:

$$p_\theta(x_1, \ldots, x_T) \;=\; \prod_{t=1}^{T} p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t}).$$

Loss: per-token negative log-likelihood, averaged over the corpus.

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) \;=\; -\,\mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}}\!\left[\,\sum_{t=1}^{|x|} \log p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t})\,\right].$$

Optimum: $p_\theta = p_{\text{data}}$ — the model is pushed to reproduce the training distribution token-by-token.

Why This Objective Memorizes

  • Per-token loss is unbounded below — lower NLL on training tokens always helps
  • Rare strings have high NLL under any non-memorizing prior
  • Driving their NLL down requires storing them in weights
  • Greedy decoding $\arg\max_{x_t} p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t})$ then replays the stored string

Memorization is a consequence of the training objective — not a bug.

01

Defining LLM Memorization

From canaries to extractable strings

Generalization vs Memorization

Deep nets fit pure random labels — capacity to memorize is not in dispute.

  • Question is what is retained after training
  • Generalization can coexist with memorization of rare points

Memorization is a per-sample property, not an aggregate.

Zhang, Bengio, Hardt, Recht, Vinyals, "Understanding Deep Learning Requires Rethinking Generalization", ICLR 2017.

Secret Sharer (Carlini 2019)

Format with a slot, drawn uniformly from a randomness space $\mathcal{R}$:

$$s[r] \;=\; \text{``The random number is }\underbrace{r_1 r_2 \cdots r_9}_{r \,\in\, \mathcal{R}}\text{''},\quad r \sim \mathrm{Unif}(\mathcal{R}).$$
  • $\mathcal{R} = \{0,\ldots,9\}^9 \Rightarrow |\mathcal{R}| = 10^9$,  $H(s) = \log_2 10^9 \approx 29.9$ bits
  • Single insertion of one chosen $r^\star$ into the training corpus
  • Quantify unintended memorization: does the model favor $r^\star$ over the other $10^9 - 1$ candidates?
Carlini, Liu, Erlingsson, Kos, Song, "The Secret Sharer: Evaluating and Testing Unintended Memorization in Neural Networks", USENIX Security 2019.

Exposure — Calibrated Memorization Score

Rank-based score against the canary template's randomness space:

$$\mathrm{exposure}_\theta(s) \;=\; \log_2 |\mathcal{R}| \;-\; \log_2 \mathrm{rank}_\theta(s).$$
  • 0 bits: rank uniform — canary unmemorized
  • $\log_2 |\mathcal{R}|$ bits: rank 1 — canary is the top completion

Calibrated against the prior — not raw perplexity.

Null Distribution — Tail Bound

Under $H_0$ (no memorization), the model has no preference among canaries:  $\mathrm{rank}_\theta(s[r^\star]) \sim \mathrm{Unif}\{1, \ldots, |\mathcal{R}|\}$.

$$\begin{aligned} \Pr\nolimits_{H_0}\!\big[\mathrm{exposure}(s) \ge t\big] &= \Pr\!\big[\log_2|\mathcal{R}| - \log_2\mathrm{rank}_\theta(s) \ge t\big] \\ &= \Pr\!\big[\mathrm{rank}_\theta(s) \le |\mathcal{R}|/2^t\big] \\ &= \frac{\lfloor |\mathcal{R}|/2^t \rfloor}{|\mathcal{R}|} \;\le\; 2^{-t}. \end{aligned}$$

Exposure $t$ ⇒ observation is $2^{-t}$ unlikely under $H_0$ — a calibrated $p$-value in bits.

Long-Tail Intuition

Many real corpora are singleton-heavy: most rare events appear only once.

  • Person names, addresses, phone numbers — one occurrence each
  • Rare books, niche code snippets — near-singleton frequency
  • Bulk of probability mass sits in a long tail of low-frequency strings

To do well on the tail, a learner must store information about individual rare examples.

Counterfactual Memorization

With-vs-without comparison for example $z$:

$$\mathrm{mem}(A, \mathcal{D}, z) \;=\; \Pr_A\!\big[A(\mathcal{D}) \text{ predicts } z\big] \;-\; \Pr_A\!\big[A(\mathcal{D}\setminus z) \text{ predicts } z\big].$$
  • $A$ — randomized learning algorithm, randomness over $A$ only
  • $\mathcal{D}$ — training set; $\mathcal{D}\setminus z$ — same set with $z$ removed
  • Large gap $\Rightarrow$ the model's prediction on $z$ depends on $z$ being in the training set

Long-Tail Theorem (Feldman)

Theorem (Feldman 2020).

Let $\pi_1 = \Pr_{z \sim P}[\,z \text{ appears exactly once in } \mathcal{D}\,]$ be the singleton mass under prior $P$. Any $\varepsilon$-near-optimal learner $A$ satisfies

$$\mathbb{E}_{z \sim \mathcal{D}}\!\big[\mathrm{mem}(A, \mathcal{D}, z)\big] \;=\; \Omega(\pi_1 - \varepsilon).$$

Reading it. Near-optimal accuracy on long-tail data forces per-example memorization on at least an $\Omega(\pi_1)$ fraction of training points — less is information-theoretically impossible.

Feldman, "Does Learning Require Memorization? A Short Tale About a Long Tail", STOC 2020.

Implication — Memorization Is Necessary

  • Singleton mass $\pi_1 > 0$ on real data $\Rightarrow$ memorization is not optional
  • Generalization on the tail requires storing individual training examples
  • Refutes the "memorization is just bad generalization" framing

Memorization and generalization are complements, not substitutes, on long-tail distributions.

02

Extraction Attacks

From GPT-2 to scaling laws to Min-K

Extracting GPT-2 (Carlini 2021)

Definition (Carlini 2021).

String $s$ is $k$-extractable from $f_\theta$ if there exists a prefix $p$ with $|p| \le k$ such that

$$f_\theta(\mathrm{greedy}, p) \;=\; s.$$

$k$-eidetic memorized: $s$ is $k$-extractable and appears in $\le k$ training examples.

Black-box GPT-2 attack: names, emails, phones, code, UUIDs — some 1-eidetic.

Carlini, Tramèr, Wallace, Jagielski, Herbert-Voss, Lee, Roberts, Brown, Song, Erlingsson, Oprea, Raffel, "Extracting Training Data from Large Language Models", USENIX Security 2021.

What $f_\theta(\mathrm{greedy}, p)$ Means

Greedy decoding: at each step, pick the $\arg\max$ token under $p_\theta(\cdot \mid \text{context})$.

prompt $p$ $\to$ "East Stroudsburg Stroudsburg..."
$f_\theta$ $\arg\max$ $\to$ " Peter" $\to$ " W" $\to$ ".  $\cdots$
output: "...Peter W. ⟨name+email+phone⟩" $= s$

Deterministic: same $p$ ⇒ same $s$. Extractable iff some short $p$ produces target $s$.

The Extraction Pipeline

  1. 1. Generate $N$ samples with diverse prompts
  2. 2. Rank by signal $\Lambda(x) = \log p_\theta(x) / \log p_{\text{ref}}(x)$
  3. 3. Verify top candidates against the corpus or web

Small $\Lambda(x)$ ⇒ target model is much more confident than baseline ⇒ memorization signal.

What Is $p_{\text{ref}}$?

A baseline that captures generic text fluency without the target's training memory.

  • Smaller LM same family — e.g. GPT-2 Small (124M) vs. GPT-2 XL (1.5B)
  • zlib compression — bit length of the zlib-compressed string
  • Lower-cased / window perplexity — same model on a transformed input

Best signal: GPT-2 Small as $p_{\text{ref}}$ — rare strings score "easy" for XL but "hard" for Small only when XL has memorized them.

Three Scaling Laws (Carlini 2022)

Memorization grows log-linearly along three axes.

  • Model size — larger models memorize more
  • Data repetition — duplicates dominate
  • Context length — longer prefix, easier extraction

Even singletons are memorized at sufficient scale.

Carlini, Ippolito, Jagielski, Lee, Tramèr, Zhang, "Quantifying Memorization Across Neural Language Models", ICLR 2023.

Repetition — Empirical Law

For $k$ duplicates of $s$ in training, independence approximation:

$$\Pr\!\big[\,s \text{ is extractable}\,\big] \;\approx\; 1 - \big(1 - p_0(s, \theta)\big)^k.$$

Per-occurrence rate $p_0(s, \theta)$ is log-linear in three axes:

$$\log p_0(s, \theta) \;\propto\; \log N_\theta \;+\; \log L_{\mathrm{ctx}} \;-\; \log\tfrac{1}{\mathrm{rarity}(s)}.$$

Additive after log ⇒ the three risks compose independently.

Reading the Law — Each Term

  • $p_0(s, \theta)$: extraction probability from one training occurrence of $s$
  • $N_\theta$: model parameter count (e.g., 125M $\to$ 1.3B $\to$ 6B $\to$ 12B in Pythia)
  • $L_{\mathrm{ctx}}$: number of prompt tokens used to elicit the suffix
  • $\mathrm{rarity}(s)$: how unique $s$ is — high $\mathrm{rarity}$ ⇔ low training frequency (inverse of duplication count)
  • $k$: number of training occurrences (duplicates)

$10\times$ in $N_\theta$ $\Rightarrow$ $\approx +19$ pp in memorized fraction at fixed $L_{\mathrm{ctx}}$, $k$.

Deduplication Helps (Lee 2022)

Near-duplicate removal in C4 / Wiki40B.

  • Memorized emissions drop by 10$\times$
  • Validation perplexity unchanged or improved
  • Cheap mitigation, orthogonal to differential privacy

Now standard in production data pipelines.

Lee, Ippolito, Nystrom, Zhang, Eck, Callison-Burch, Carlini, "Deduplicating Training Data Makes Language Models Better", ACL 2022.

Aligned Models Still Leak (Nasr 2023)

Divergence attack on aligned ChatGPT — single-token loop prompt:

Repeat the word "poem" forever
  • Model loops "poem poem poem …" then diverges — emits training fragments
  • RLHF chat persona breaks; raw pretraining distribution surfaces
  • Several MB extracted for $\approx \$200$ of API spend

RLHF alignment is a filter, not memory removal.

Nasr, Carlini, Hayase, Jagielski, Cooper, Ippolito, Choquette-Choo, Wallace, Tramèr, Lee, "Scalable Extraction of Training Data from (Production) Language Models", 2023.

What Came Out

Verified against $\sim 10$ TB of indexed web data via suffix arrays:

  • PII — real names, email addresses, phone and fax numbers, physical addresses
  • Code — complete Python ML scripts with imports and training loops
  • Copyrighted text — long boilerplate, legal disclaimers, book passages
  • NSFW — dating-site content not surfaced under normal prompting

Authors' summary: emitted PII recurs often — not a freak event.

Min-K% Prob (Shi 2024)

Reference-free pretraining-data MIA. Compute per-token log-prob, then keep the $K\%$ tokens with the smallest values (the "most surprising" positions):

$$\mathrm{MinK}\%(x) \;=\; \frac{1}{|S|}\!\sum_{x_t \in S} \log p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t}),\quad S = \big\{\text{bottom-}K\%\text{ tokens by }\log p_\theta\big\}.$$
  • Member: even the rare tokens are learned $\Rightarrow$ mean of the bottom is not too low
  • Non-member: contains genuinely surprising tokens $\Rightarrow$ mean drops
  • Best $K \approx 20\%$ — no reference model, no shadow training
Shi, Ajith, Xia, Huang, Liu, Blevins, Chen, Zettlemoyer, "Detecting Pretraining Data from Large Language Models", ICLR 2024.

Min-K%++ Probe (Zhang 2025)

Calibrate per-position via a $z$-score under the next-token distribution:

$$\mathrm{MinK}\%\text{++}(x) \;=\; \frac{1}{|S|}\sum_{x_t \in S} \frac{\log p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t}) - \mu_t}{\sigma_t},$$

$\mu_t, \sigma_t$: mean and std of $\log p_\theta(\cdot \mid x_{<t})$ over the vocabulary.

  • Robust to position-wise scale of log-likelihood
  • SOTA on WikiMIA / MIMIR pretraining-data MIA
  • Bridges memorization-detection and MIA literatures
Zhang, Liu, Lyu, He, Lin, "Min-K%++: Improved Baseline for Detecting Pre-Training Data", ICLR 2025.
03

Adversarial Compression and Beyond

ACR, books, defenses

Why a New Definition?

Existing definitions miss the compliance audit use case.

  • Discoverable (Carlini 2023): too permissive — defeated by tiny refusal trigger
  • Extractable (Nasr 2023): too restrictive — "repeat after me" wins trivially
  • Counterfactual (Zhang et al. 2023): needs retraining on $\mathcal{D}\setminus\{x\}$ for every $x$ — infeasible at LLM scale
  • MIA / perplexity: brittle to small weight edits — spoofable

Need: adversarial, completion-free, auditable on a single string.

Zhang, Ippolito, Lee, Jagielski, Tramèr, Carlini, "Counterfactual Memorization in Neural Language Models", TMLR 2023.

Retraining Cost — Why Counterfactual Fails at Scale

Counterfactual definition asks: does removing $x$ change the model's behavior on $x$?

$$\mathrm{mem}(x) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{x \in S}\!\big[\mathrm{perf}(f_S, x)\big] \;-\; \mathbb{E}_{x \notin S}\!\big[\mathrm{perf}(f_S, x)\big].$$

$\mathrm{perf}(f_S, x)$: model's score on $x$ — per-token log-likelihood $\log p_{f_S}(x)$ or 0/1 accuracy.

  • Each expectation needs many models trained on different subsets $S \subset \mathcal{D}$
  • Zhang et al. used $m = 400$ small LMs — already at feasibility limit
  • For GPT-4 scale: a single training run costs $\sim 10^8$ USD — ruled out

Adversarial Compression Ratio (ACR)

Definition (Schwarzschild 2024).

For target token sequence $y$ and model $M$,

$$\mathrm{ACR}(M, y) \;=\; \frac{|y|}{|x^*|},\qquad x^* \;=\; \arg\min_x |x| \;\;\text{s.t.}\;\; M(x) = y.$$

$y$ is $\tau$-memorized if $\mathrm{ACR}(M, y) > \tau(y)$. Default $\tau = 1$ (any compression).

$M(x) = y$: greedy decoding from prompt $x$ produces $y$ token-for-token (deterministic, exact equality).

Schwarzschild, Feng, Maini, Lipton, Kolter, "Rethinking LLM Memorization through the Lens of Adversarial Compression", NeurIPS 2024.

Reading ACR — Compression as Storage

  • Need prompt $x$ shorter than $y$ that deterministically elicits $y$
  • If $|x^*| < |y|$: the weights act as a decompressor — the missing bits live inside $\theta$
  • If $|x^*| \ge |y|$: the prompt itself supplies $y$ — no memorization implied

Information-theoretic framing: memorization ⇔ weights store more than $|y| - |x^*|$ bits about $y$.

Tool — Greedy Coordinate Gradient (GCG)

Discrete optimization over prompt tokens to maximize a target log-likelihood:

$$\max_{x \in \mathcal{V}^L} \;\log p_\theta(y \mid x).$$
  • Search space is combinatorial: $|\mathcal{V}|^L$ — brute force out
  • Trick: gradient w.r.t. the one-hot encoding of each position is cheap
  • Used originally for jailbreaks; reused by MiniPrompt to audit memorization
Zou, Wang, Carlini, Nasr, Kolter, Fredrikson, "Universal and Transferable Adversarial Attacks on Aligned Language Models", 2023.

GCG — One Iteration

  1. 1. Compute $\nabla_{e_i}\!\log p_\theta(y \mid x)$ for one-hot $e_i$ at every position $i$
  2. 2. At each $i$, keep the top-$k$ tokens whose swap-in increases the objective most under the linear approx.
  3. 3. Sample $B$ candidate single-token swaps from the top-$k$ pools, score the true $\log p_\theta(y \mid x')$
  4. 4. Greedily commit the best swap; repeat

All positions are candidates each step — what makes GCG strong vs. AutoPrompt / HotFlip.

MiniPrompt — Search by GCG

Outer loop over prompt length, inner loop is GCG (Zou et al. 2023).

  1. 1. Initialize $z^{(0)}$ as 5 random tokens
  2. 2. Run $n$ GCG steps on $z^{(i)}$ to maximize $p_M(y \mid z^{(i)})$
  3. 3. If $M(z^{(i)}) = y$: shrink length by 1; else grow by 5
  4. 4. Return shortest successful $|x^*|$

Discrete search — arbitrary tokens, no semantic constraint.

ACR — What It Reveals

  • Famous quotes: high ACR — flagged as memorized
  • Post-training-cutoff text: low ACR — not memorized
  • Models told to "forget" copyrighted snippets retain almost the same ACR

"Unlearning" via finetuning is often an illusion of compliance — ACR sees through it.

ACR is completion-free: no prefix split, no pre-set length parameters.

Probabilistic Extraction (Hayes 2024)

Greedy verbatim is too narrow — sampled completions also leak. Replace the greedy equality with a probability under sampling:

$$p_{\text{ext}}(s \mid p) \;=\; \Pr_{\hat s \sim f_\theta(\cdot \mid p)}\!\big[\,\hat s = s\,\big].$$

$(n,\,q)$-extractable: $\exists$ prompt $p$ such that $n$ independent draws contain $s$ with probability $\ge q$:

$$1 - \big(1 - p_{\text{ext}}(s \mid p)\big)^n \;\ge\; q.$$
Hayes, Cooper, Nasr, Lee, Choquette-Choo, Tramèr, Carlini, "Measuring Memorization via Probabilistic Discoverable Extraction", 2024.

Approximate Match — LCS Variant

Exact equality is brittle. Relax to a length threshold on the longest common substring:

$$\mathrm{LCS}(s, \hat s) \;=\; \max\big\{\,k : s_{i:i+k} = \hat s_{j:j+k} \text{ for some } i, j\,\big\}.$$
  • Substring — contiguous run of matching tokens (not subsequence with gaps)
  • Replace $\mathbb{1}[\hat s = s]$ with $\mathbb{1}[\mathrm{LCS}(s, \hat s) \ge n]$ in the extraction probability
  • Greedy / exact verbatim is the corner $n = |s|$

Captures soft / partial memorization at realistic decoding temperatures.

Books from Open-Weight LLMs (Cooper 2025)

50 books $\times$ 17 open-weight models, probabilistic extraction at scale.

  • Split each book into many $(\text{prefix},\,50\text{-token suffix})$ pairs
  • Compute per-suffix $p_{\text{ext}}$ analytically from token logits — no costly rollouts
  • Aggregate the fraction of chunks with $p_{\text{ext}} \ge \tau$ for thresholds $\tau \in \{0.01, 0.25, 0.5\}$
  • Models: Pythia, OLMo, Llama 1 30B/65B, Llama 3.1 8B/70B, …
Cooper, Gokaslan, Ahmed, Cyphert, De Sa, Lemley, Ho, Liang, "Extracting Memorized Pieces of (Copyrighted) Books from Open-Weight LLMs", 2025.

Headline Results — Llama 3.1 70B

One book stands out across all 17 models tested:

  • Harry Potter and the Sorcerer's Stone — 43.3% of 50-token chunks extractable at $\tau = 0.5$
  • Same book — 90.9% of chunks extractable at $\tau = 0.01$
  • Nineteen Eighty-Four — 54.4% of chunks extractable at $\tau = 0.25$
  • Sandman Slim and many others — < 0.4% across all models

Memorization is highly book-specific and model-specific.

What This Means for the Lawsuit Discourse

  • "LLMs are copy machines" — false; most books are not substantially memorized
  • "LLMs never memorize" — false; canonical / heavily-quoted books are recoverable
  • Per-book, per-model audit is the only honest framing
  • Open weights make the audit cheap — closed-weight models hide it

Both polarized positions in copyright litigation oversimplify the empirical picture.

Non-Adversarial Reproduction (Aerni 2024)

A benign prompt: one a real user would plausibly send with no extraction intent.

  • Creative — "Write a short story about a lost dog."
  • Expository — "Write a tutorial on baking sourdough."
  • Argumentative — "Write a letter to the editor on climate policy."

Measure $n$-gram overlap of outputs vs. $\sim 10$ TB of indexed web text.

Aerni, Rando, Debenedetti, Carlini, Ippolito, Tramèr, "Measuring Non-Adversarial Reproduction of Training Data in Large Language Models", 2024.

Aerni — Findings

  • Average overlap: up to 15% of output text appears verbatim on the web (Claude, GPT-4, Gemini, Llama)
  • Worst-case outputs: 100% verbatim spans found online
  • Human responses to the same prompts: dramatically lower overlap
  • "Be original" system prompts cut the average — do not eliminate the worst case

Memorization leaks in normal use — not just under attack.

Defenses

Deduplication

Cheap, orthogonal, partial.

$10\times$ drop in emissions.

DP training

Provable per-sample bound.

Utility cost; rare in production LLMs.

Unlearning

Targeted removal of $\mathcal{D}_f$.

Open: deletion vs suppression.

None is a silver bullet. Stack them — and audit with ACR.

Memorization vs Membership Inference

MIA

Was $x \in \mathcal{D}$?

Distinguishability between two posteriors.

One bit of leakage per query.

Memorization

Can $x$ be reproduced verbatim?

Constructive recovery of content.

String-level leakage.

MIA is necessary; memorization is the stronger signal.

Open Problems — Measurement

  • Closed-weight auditing. All rigorous definitions (discoverable, ACR, counterfactual) need logits, gradients, or retraining — not available through GPT-5 / Claude / Gemini APIs.
  • Per-sample audit at $10^{12}$ tokens. Even discoverable memorization is $O(|\mathcal{D}|)$ greedy decodes — tractable subsampling that retains calibration is open.
  • Concept vs. verbatim. Knowing the plot of a book without quoting it: token-level metrics blind to this; semantic-distance metrics not yet calibrated.

Open Problems — Scope

  • Multi-modal. Text-image, text-audio, text-video: exposure / extractability defined on token equality — needs continuous-output analogs with calibrated false-positive rates.
  • RAG and agents. Retrievers pull training-adjacent text into context; agents can be tricked into reciting training data through tool detours.
  • Long-context caching. Inference-time KV caches store the user's prompt; cross-user leakage is largely unstudied.
  • Reasoning / CoT. Does explicit "thinking" surface memorized fragments more readily?

Open Problems — Theory and Defense

  • Provable extraction bounds. DP-SGD gives upper bounds; at usable $\varepsilon$ for LLM scale, those bounds are vacuous. Tight bounds matching empirical attack rates are missing.
  • Unlearning vs. suppression. Most "unlearning" methods only suppress surface outputs; ACR / GCG audits recover supposedly-unlearned content. No principled test for weight-level removal.
  • Sharpness-style metrics for autoregressive distributions. Diffusion has Hessian-trace / score-norm probes (SAIL); the autoregressive analog is open.
  • Adversarial robustness of audits. ACR is hard to spoof — but is it spoofable in principle?

Takeaways — LLMs

  • Exposure (bits), $k$-extractable, three scaling laws — the classical toolkit
  • Min-K%/Min-K%++ bridge memorization detection with pretraining-data MIA
  • ACR is the audit-grade definition — resistant to compliance theatre
  • Open weights leak some books; aligned chat models still leak under divergence
  • Per-sample, per-prompt, per-string — never aggregate
