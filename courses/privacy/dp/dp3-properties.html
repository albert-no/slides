Composition

Privacy budgets add when you re-query the same data.

Basic Composition (Sequential, Adaptive)

Theorem.

Let $\mathcal{M}_1: \mathcal{X}^n \to \mathcal{Y}_1$ be $\varepsilon_1$-DP and $\mathcal{M}_2: \mathcal{X}^n \times \mathcal{Y}_1 \to \mathcal{Y}_2$ be $\varepsilon_2$-DP for every fixed first output. Then releasing $(\mathcal{M}_1(D),\, \mathcal{M}_2(D, \mathcal{M}_1(D)))$ is $(\varepsilon_1 + \varepsilon_2)$-DP.

"Adaptive" means $\mathcal{M}_2$ may depend on $\mathcal{M}_1$'s output.

Proof — Chain Rule on the Ratio

For neighbors $D, D'$ and outputs $(a_1, a_2)$,

$$\frac{\Pr[(\mathcal{M}_1(D),\, \mathcal{M}_2(D, \mathcal{M}_1(D))) = (a_1, a_2)]}{\Pr[(\mathcal{M}_1(D'),\, \mathcal{M}_2(D', \mathcal{M}_1(D'))) = (a_1, a_2)]} \;=\; \frac{\Pr[\mathcal{M}_1(D) = a_1]}{\Pr[\mathcal{M}_1(D') = a_1]} \cdot \frac{\Pr[\mathcal{M}_2(D, a_1) = a_2]}{\Pr[\mathcal{M}_2(D', a_1) = a_2]}.$$

The first factor is $\le e^{\varepsilon_1}$; the second is $\le e^{\varepsilon_2}$ by hypothesis. Product $\le e^{\varepsilon_1 + \varepsilon_2}$. $\blacksquare$

$k$-fold Composition

Corollary.

If $\mathcal{M}_i$ is $\varepsilon_i$-DP for $i = 1, \dots, k$ (possibly adaptive), then $(\mathcal{M}_1, \dots, \mathcal{M}_k)$ is $\sum_i \varepsilon_i$-DP.

  • If each $\mathcal{M}_i$ is $\varepsilon$-DP, then $k$ rounds cost $k\varepsilon$ — linear in $k$.
  • The advanced composition theorem (Lecture 5) improves this to $\approx \sqrt k\,\varepsilon$ when small $\delta > 0$ is tolerated.
Dwork & Roth, "Algorithmic Foundations of Differential Privacy", 2014.
02

Post-Processing

Once private, always private.

Post-Processing Immunity

Theorem.

If $\mathcal{M}: D \to \mathcal{Y}$ is $\varepsilon$-DP and $f: \mathcal{Y} \to \mathcal{Y}'$ is any (possibly randomized) function, then $f \circ \mathcal{M}$ is $\varepsilon$-DP.

Proof.

For neighboring $D, D'$ and any $O' \subseteq \mathcal{Y}'$,

$$\Pr[f(\mathcal{M}(D)) \in O'] = \Pr[\mathcal{M}(D) \in f^{-1}(O')] \le e^{\varepsilon} \Pr[\mathcal{M}(D') \in f^{-1}(O')] = e^{\varepsilon} \Pr[f(\mathcal{M}(D')) \in O']. \;\blacksquare$$

Why Post-Processing Matters

  • Once an output is $\varepsilon$-DP, any further computation on it — by anyone, with any auxiliary data — is $\varepsilon$-DP.
  • Enables a clean separation: release once, downstream analytics inherit the guarantee.
  • Conversely: do not re-query the raw data to "refine" a DP release — that consumes budget again.
Post-processing is what makes Model DP (a single private release) possible — see Lecture 7.

Group Privacy & Subsampling

When $t$ records change, and when sampling amplifies.

Group Privacy

Theorem.

If $\mathcal{M}$ is $\varepsilon$-DP, then for any group of $t$ individuals, $\mathcal{M}$ is $t\varepsilon$-DP with respect to any change in that group.

Reason: $t$-record change = $t$ neighboring-pair changes; iterate composition (or use a coupling argument).

  • If a person appears in multiple records, their guarantee scales by their count.
  • Choose $\varepsilon$ small enough that moderate $t$ still yields useful $t\varepsilon$.

Privacy Amplification by Subsampling

Let $\mathcal{M}$ be $\varepsilon$-DP. Form $\mathcal{M}': \mathcal{X}^n \to \mathcal{Y}$ by:

  1. Sample a uniform subset $X' \subset X$ of size $m$ (without replacement);
  2. Output $\mathcal{M}(X')$.

Let $q = m/n$. Then $\mathcal{M}'$ is $\varepsilon'$-DP with

$$\varepsilon' \;=\; \ln\!\bigl(1 + q(e^\varepsilon - 1)\bigr) \;\approx\; q\varepsilon \quad \text{for small } q, \varepsilon.$$

Subsampling Proof — Setup

Let $X = (x, x_2, \dots, x_n)$ and $X' = (x', x_2, \dots, x_n)$. Let $I = (I_1, \dots, I_m)$ be a uniform random subset of $\{1, \dots, n\}$; $s(X; I) = (x_{I_1}, \dots, x_{I_m})$.

Condition on the event $\{1 \in I\}$ vs $\{1 \notin I\}$:

$$\Pr[\mathcal{M}'(X) \in T] = q\, \Pr[\mathcal{M}(s(X; I)) \in T \mid 1 \in I] + (1-q)\, \Pr[\mathcal{M}(s(X; I)) \in T \mid 1 \notin I].$$

Subsampling Proof — Three Inequalities

By $\varepsilon$-DP of $\mathcal{M}$ and a coupling of $\{1 \in I\}$ to $\{1 \notin I\}$ neighbors:

$$\begin{aligned} \Pr[\mathcal{M}(s(X;I)) \in T \mid 1 \in I] &\le e^\varepsilon\, \Pr[\mathcal{M}(s(X';I)) \in T \mid 1 \in I] \\ \Pr[\mathcal{M}(s(X;I)) \in T \mid 1 \in I] &\le e^\varepsilon\, \Pr[\mathcal{M}(s(X;I)) \in T \mid 1 \notin I] \\ \Pr[\mathcal{M}(s(X;I)) \in T \mid 1 \notin I] &= \Pr[\mathcal{M}(s(X';I)) \in T \mid 1 \notin I]. \end{aligned}$$

Exercise: subset sizes differ; formal coupling matches each $1 \in I$ subset to a $1 \notin I$ neighbor.

Subsampling Proof — Conclusion

Let $\alpha = q + (1-q) e^{-\varepsilon} < 1$. Combining the three inequalities,

$$\Pr[\mathcal{M}(s(X;I)) \in T \mid 1 \in I] \le \alpha\, e^\varepsilon\, \Pr[\mathcal{M}(s(X';I)) \in T \mid 1 \in I] + (1-\alpha) e^\varepsilon\, \Pr[\mathcal{M}(s(X;I)) \in T \mid 1 \notin I].$$

Rearrange:

$$\Pr[\mathcal{M}'(X) \in T] \;\le\; \bigl(1 + q(e^\varepsilon - 1)\bigr)\, \Pr[\mathcal{M}'(X') \in T]. \;\blacksquare$$

Take-away

  • A $1$-DP algorithm on a uniformly subsampled batch becomes $\Theta(q)$-DP on the full dataset.
  • Equivalently, blowing up the dataset by $O(1/\varepsilon)$ converts a $1$-DP base into $\varepsilon$-DP.
  • Critical for stochastic algorithms (DP-SGD, federated rounds) where each step touches only a fraction of users.
Subsampling is the cheapest privacy amplifier you have. Use it whenever the algorithm allows.

Additive DP — Don't

Why dropping the multiplicative factor breaks privacy.

Additive DP (Tentative Definition)

Definition (would-be additive DP).

For neighbors $D, D'$:

$$\Pr[\mathcal{M}(D) \in O] \;\le\; \Pr[\mathcal{M}(D') \in O] + \delta.$$

Satisfies composition, post-processing, group privacy — superficially attractive.

The catch: the additive slack alone admits catastrophic mechanisms.

Counterexample 1 — Leak Everything w.p. $\delta$

Mechanism.

$\mathcal{M}(X) = X$ with probability $\delta$, otherwise outputs nothing.

For neighbors $D, D'$, both events $\{\mathcal{M} = D\}$ and $\{\mathcal{M} = D'\}$ have probability $\delta$:

$$|\Pr[\mathcal{M}(D) \in O] - \Pr[\mathcal{M}(D') \in O]| \le \delta.$$
  • Satisfies $\delta$-additive DP; w.p. $\delta$ leaks the entire dataset.
  • Additive-only is useless unless $\delta$ is negligible — but then utility also collapses.

Counterexample 2 — Per-Element Leakage

Mechanism.

Output $Y = (Y_1, \dots, Y_n)$ where $Y_i = X_i$ with probability $\delta$, else $Y_i = \bot$.

Each $Y_i$ on its own is $\delta$-additive DP (exercise). But the probability that at least one record leaks is

$$1 - (1-\delta)^n \;\approx\; n\delta.$$

For useful applications $\delta > 1/n$, so this is non-negligible.

Moral: $\varepsilon$ must be multiplicative — additive slack alone cannot bound information leakage.

DP $k$-Means

A concrete worked example: clustering under $\varepsilon$-DP.

Standard $k$-Means (Lloyd's)

Algorithm — $k$-Means
# Input: $X = \{x_1, \dots, x_n\} \subset \mathbb{R}^d$, number of clusters $k$, iterations $T$.
Initialize centroids $\mu_1, \dots, \mu_k$ at random
for $t = 1, \dots, T$:
  for $j = 1, \dots, k$: $C_j \leftarrow \{x_i : \|x_i - \mu_j\|^2 \le \min_m \|x_i - \mu_m\|^2\}$
  for $j = 1, \dots, k$: $\mu_j \leftarrow \frac{1}{|C_j|}\sum_{x_i \in C_j} x_i$
return $\{\mu_j\}$

Minimizes the within-cluster variance $\mathcal{L} = \sum_j \sum_{x_i \in C_j} \|x_i - \mu_j\|^2$.

DP $k$-Means (Output Perturbation)

Algorithm — DP $k$-Means  $\mathcal{U}=\{x:\|x\|_1\le 1\}$, $\varepsilon' = \varepsilon/(2T)$
Initialize $\mu_1, \dots, \mu_k$ at random
for $t = 1, \dots, T$:
  for $j = 1, \dots, k$: $C_j \leftarrow \{x_i : \|x_i - \mu_j\|^2 \le \min_m \|x_i - \mu_m\|^2\}$
  for $j = 1, \dots, k$:
    $n_j \leftarrow |C_j| + Y, \quad Y \sim \mathrm{Lap}(2/\varepsilon')$
    $a_j \leftarrow \bigl(\sum_{x_i \in C_j} x_i\bigr) + Z^d, \quad Z^d \overset{\text{iid}}\sim \mathrm{Lap}(2/\varepsilon')$
    $\mu_j \leftarrow \begin{cases} a_j / n_j & n_j \ge 1 \\ \text{Uniform}(\mathcal{U}) & n_j < 1 \end{cases}$
return $\{\mu_j\}$

Privacy Guarantee

Theorem.

DP $k$-Means is $\varepsilon$-DP.

Proof.

Within one iteration:

  • Cluster sums. $(\sum_{x_i \in C_1} x_i, \dots, \sum_{x_i \in C_k} x_i) \to (a_1, \dots, a_k)$ has $\ell_1$-sensitivity 2 (one record moves $\sum$ by at most $\|x\|_1 + \|x'\|_1 \le 2$, splitting across clusters). Laplace noise with scale $2/\varepsilon'$ ⇒ $\varepsilon'$-DP.
  • Cluster counts. $(|C_1|, \dots, |C_k|) \to (n_1, \dots, n_k)$ has sensitivity 2 (a single record changes one count by $\pm 1$ in the old and new clusters). Laplace scale $2/\varepsilon'$ ⇒ $\varepsilon'$-DP.

By basic composition, one iteration is $2\varepsilon'$-DP. Over $T$ iterations, total $= 2T\varepsilon' = \varepsilon$. $\blacksquare$

Remarks & Extensions

  • Bounded domain. $\mathcal{U}$ caps individual influence — without it, sensitivity is unbounded.
  • Approximate DP variant. Replace Laplace with Gaussian (Lecture 4) for tighter $\ell_2$-sensitivity scaling — useful in high $d$.
  • Output update. Both the per-cluster sum and count are released; division is then post-processing, so it's free.
  • Sensitivity arguments often rely on bounded data (e.g. $\|x\|_1 \le 1$); always check that the modeling assumption is realistic.
Nissim, Raskhodnikova, Smith, "Smooth Sensitivity and Sampling in Private Data Analysis", STOC 2007.

Recap

  • Composition: $\varepsilon$'s add — even under adaptivity.
  • Post-processing: any downstream function preserves the bound.
  • Group privacy: $t$-record change ⇒ $t\varepsilon$.
  • Subsampling: $\varepsilon' = \ln(1 + q(e^\varepsilon - 1)) \approx q\varepsilon$.
  • Additive-only privacy fails; multiplicative $e^\varepsilon$ is essential.
  • DP $k$-Means: $\varepsilon$-DP via output perturbation per iteration.
