The codebook size — concrete numbers

Storage is $d \cdot 2^{dR} \cdot 4$ bytes (single-precision floats). The growth is exponential in $d$ at fixed rate.

  • $d=4, R=2$: $K = 256$ vectors $\to 4$ KB. Easily fits in cache.
  • $d=8, R=2$: $K = 65{,}536$ $\to 2$ MB. Fits in L2/L3.
  • $d=8, R=4$: $K = 4.29\times 10^9$ $\to 137$ GB. Won't fit anywhere on a single GPU.
  • $d=16, R=2$: $K = 4.29\times 10^9$ $\to 275$ GB. Same wall.

Shannon says the gain from $d \to \infty$ is large (the gap between $\mathbb{Z}^d$ and the sphere bound is $\le 1.53$ dB). Storage says $d$ cannot grow past single digits with an unstructured codebook. This mismatch is what motivates structure.

The encoder is the bottleneck — why search is intractable

Per block, optimal VQ computes

$\widehat{\mathbf{w}} = \arg\min_{k=1,\ldots,K} \|\mathbf{w} - \mathbf{c}_k\|^2.$

No clever data structure escapes this in the worst case for generic $\mathbf{c}_k$'s. Locality-sensitive hashing, kd-trees, and graph indexes give approximate speedups, but:

  • They still pay a constant fraction of $K$ in lookups, and $K = 2^{dR}$.
  • They require the codebook itself to be in memory.
  • They are not vectorizable in the way GPUs need.

For LLM inference, you re-encode activations (or at least look up indices) at every layer for every token. Even $K = 10^4$ is a non-starter at 1000 tokens/s. The bottleneck is fundamental: an unstructured codebook has no algebra you can exploit.

Decoding, in contrast, is one lookup per block — given the index $k$, fetch $\mathbf{c}_k$. The whole asymmetry of the problem is concentrated in the encoder.

Uniform quantization arithmetic — full picture

Encode $x$ as the integer $n = \mathrm{round}(x/\Delta)$. The reconstructed value is $\hat x = n\Delta$, with $|x - \hat x| \le \Delta/2$.

Addition.

$\hat a + \hat b = (n_a + n_b)\Delta \in \Delta\mathbb{Z}$. The sum is exactly representable on the grid — no requantization is needed for any finite chain of additions, only the final integer can grow.

Scaling by an integer.

$k\hat a = (kn_a)\Delta \in \Delta\mathbb{Z}$ for any $k \in \mathbb{Z}$. Same property: integer arithmetic in the index domain matches exact arithmetic in the value domain.

Inner product (the workhorse).

Quantize weights $\hat W_{ji} = n_{ji}\Delta_w$ and inputs $\hat x_i = m_i \Delta_x$. The layer output is

$\hat y_j = \sum_i \hat W_{ji}\,\hat x_i = \Delta_w \Delta_x \sum_i n_{ji}\,m_i.$

The accumulator $\sum_i n_{ji} m_i$ is pure integer multiply-accumulate. On modern GPUs (Ampere/Hopper/Blackwell), INT8 tensor cores run this at $\sim 4\times$ the throughput of FP16 and the weights are $\sim 4\times$ smaller, so the memory-traffic improvement is just as big as the compute one.

Only at the end does the result return to float, by multiplying by $\Delta_w \Delta_x$. (In practice you also add per-channel bias/scale terms, but the structure is the same.)

What we are not saying.

$Q_\Delta(a + b) \ne Q_\Delta(a) + Q_\Delta(b)$ in general — addition commutes with the grid representation, not with the rounding step. The arithmetic identity above assumes both operands are already on the grid. Quantizing $a$ and $b$ separately incurs $\le \Delta/2$ error each; combining them is then exact.

The MSE penalty of uniform quantization — 1.53 dB

For i.i.d. uniform data on a cell, the MSE of $\mathbb{Z}^d$ quantization is $\Delta^2/12$ per coordinate, at any $d$. The high-resolution Shannon lower bound says no quantizer at the same rate beats $\Delta^2/(2\pi e)$ per coordinate (the "sphere bound").

$\dfrac{\text{cube MSE}}{\text{sphere bound}} = \dfrac{1/12}{1/(2\pi e)} = \dfrac{\pi e}{6} \approx 1.42, \quad 10 \log_{10}(\pi e/6) \approx 1.53 \text{ dB}.$

So uniform quantization is $\le 1.53$ dB worse than any quantizer at the same rate, for all dimensions and rates (in the high-resolution limit). The remaining gap is what $E_8$, Leech, and other structured lattices close.

Lattice as additive subgroup — two consequences

$\Lambda = B\mathbb{Z}^d$ is closed under addition: $\lambda + \lambda' \in \Lambda$, $-\lambda \in \Lambda$. Two consequences shape everything that follows.

  1. Translation symmetry of Voronoi cells. $\mathcal{V}(\lambda) = \mathcal{V}(0) + \lambda$. Every Voronoi region is a translate of one fundamental cell — there is one cell shape, repeated.
  2. Algebraic encoding. Closest-point search in $\Lambda$ reduces to coset enumeration in $\mathbb{Z}^d$, which is just coordinate-wise rounding plus a constant number of corrections. No list is consulted.

Both properties fail for unstructured VQ: each codepoint has its own cell shape, and encoding is a list search over the full codebook.

Why $E_8$ in particular — kissing number 240

$E_8$ is the set of integer-or-half-integer 8-vectors with even coordinate sum:

$E_8 = \bigl(\mathbb{Z}^8 \cup (\mathbb{Z}^8 + \tfrac12 \mathbf{1})\bigr) \cap \{x : \mathbf{1}^\top x \in 2\mathbb{Z}\}.$

Its 240 minimum-norm vectors are the centers of the 240 spheres touching the origin sphere in the densest 8D packing. The $\mathbb{Z}^8$ cube lattice has only 16. More tangent neighbors $\Rightarrow$ rounder Voronoi cells $\Rightarrow$ smaller MSE per unit volume.

Numerically, the normalized second moment $G(E_8) \approx 0.0717$ vs. $G(\mathbb{Z}^8) = 1/12 \approx 0.0833$ — about 14% lower MSE at the same rate by swapping the lattice.

Viazovska proved in 2017 (Fields Medal) that $E_8$ is the unique densest sphere packing in $\mathbb{R}^8$. So $E_8$ is essentially as good as it gets at $d=8$. (Leech in $d=24$ is even tighter, but the codebook and rounding cost are higher.)

For QUIP\#, $d=8$ at $R \approx 1$ bit per coordinate gives $K = 256$ codewords — a 1 KB lookup table that fits in L1 cache. That's the sweet spot.

Fast rounding for $E_8$

The recipe in 4 steps, total cost $\mathcal{O}(d)$:

  1. Round to $\mathbb{Z}^8$: $\mathbf{n}_1 = \lfloor x + \tfrac12 \rfloor$ component-wise.
  2. Fix parity: if $\sum n_{1,i}$ is odd, find the index with the largest rounding error and flip its rounding direction. The result lands in $D_8$ (even-sum integer lattice).
  3. Try the half-integer translate: repeat steps 1-2 on $x - (\tfrac12,\ldots,\tfrac12)$, then add the shift back. Call the result $\mathbf{n}_2$.
  4. Pick the closer of $\mathbf{n}_1$ and $\mathbf{n}_2$ to $x$.

No codebook is consulted at any step. The 256-entry lookup table is only used at decode time to convert the integer index back into a vector.

The QUIP proxy loss — why this is the right objective

What we ultimately care about is the network's output: $\mathbb{E}_x \|f_{\widehat W}(x) - f_W(x)\|^2$. The full $f$ is a deep nonlinear function, intractable to analyze under quantization.

Approximation: if every layer's output is close to its original, the network's output is too. For a single linear layer $y = Wx$, replacing $W$ with $\widehat W$ produces output error

$\mathbb{E}_x \|(\widehat W - W) x\|^2 = \mathrm{tr}\bigl((\widehat W - W) H (\widehat W - W)^\top\bigr), \quad H = \mathbb{E}_x[xx^\top].$

This is the proxy loss. It is:

  • Per-layer — decouples a deep-network problem into one calibration per linear layer.
  • Per-row decoupled — different rows of $W$ multiply different rows of $\widehat W$ against the same $H$. So each output channel is quantized independently.
  • Differentiable in $\widehat W$ — for fixed $H$, this is a quadratic in the rounding residual.
  • Orthogonally invariant — for orthogonal $U, V$: $\mathrm{tr}((UWV^\top)(VHV^\top)(VW^\top U^\top)) = \mathrm{tr}(WHW^\top)$. So we can rotate without changing the loss.

The Hessian $H = \mathbb{E}_x[xx^\top]$ is positive semi-definite. High curvature along direction $v$ (eigenvalue $\lambda_v$ large) means the output is sensitive to weight error along $v$; the quantizer should be careful there.

The proxy is layer-wise greedy. It does not account for the network composing many such layers; in practice QUIP\# adds an inter-layer fine-tuning pass to recover some of this.

Why outliers wreck quantization — the dynamic range argument

A scalar quantizer with $K = 2^R$ bins covering $[-W_{\max}, W_{\max}]$ has step $\Delta = 2 W_{\max}/K$. So MSE per coordinate is $\sim \Delta^2/12 \sim W_{\max}^2 / K^2$.

If a single entry $W_{i^\star,j^\star}$ has $|W_{i^\star,j^\star}| = 10 \sigma$ while typical entries have $|W_{ij}| \sim \sigma$, then $\Delta \propto 10\sigma$ — but most entries only use one or two bins out of $K$. The bits are wasted on representing emptiness near the outlier.

Quantitatively, the dynamic-range penalty is $(W_{\max} / \text{typical})^2$ per coordinate. Reducing $W_{\max}/\text{typical}$ from $10$ to $1.5$ is a $\sim 44\times$ MSE reduction at the same rate.

This is why pre-LLM quantization papers spend so much effort on "outlier handling" — channel-wise scaling, mixed precision for outliers, weight clipping. Incoherence processing replaces all of those with a single random rotation that statistically bounds the maximum-to-typical ratio.

Incoherence — formal definition

(Chee et al. 2023, Defn. 1.) A symmetric PSD matrix $H \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ is $\mu$-incoherent if its eigendecomposition $H = Q\Lambda Q^\top$ has

$\max_{i,j} |Q_{ij}| = \max_{i,j} |e_i^\top Q e_j| \le \mu / \sqrt n.$

A matrix $W \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n}$ is $\mu$-incoherent if $\max_{i,j} |W_{ij}| \le \mu \|W\|_F / \sqrt{mn}$.

The intuition: a random orthogonal column has entries with squared magnitudes concentrating around $1/n$, so $|Q_{ij}| = \widetilde{\mathcal{O}}(1/\sqrt n)$. "Most" orthogonal matrices are $\mathcal{O}(\sqrt{\log n})$-incoherent.

Operationally: incoherent = no entry much larger than the typical scale. The maximum-to-typical ratio is bounded by $\mu$. A single uniform $\Delta$ then quantizes all entries to similar relative error.

Why Kronecker rotation — QUIP's original choice

A uniformly random orthogonal $V \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ gives the cleanest incoherence guarantee but costs $\Theta(n^2)$ to multiply by — same order as the linear layer itself, doubling inference cost.

QUIP factors $n = pq$ with $p \approx q \approx \sqrt n$, draws independent uniform orthogonals $V_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{p\times p}$ and $V_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{q\times q}$, and uses $V = V_1 \otimes V_2$. Multiplying $Vx$ reshapes $x$ as $p\times q$ and applies $V_1$ on the left, $V_2^\top$ on the right: cost $\Theta(n(p+q)) = \Theta(n\sqrt n)$.

QUIP Lemma 5: with $k=2$ Kronecker factors, $V H V^\top$ is $\mu_H$-incoherent with $\mu_H = \mathcal{O}(\log^2(n/\delta))$ w.p. $\ge 1-\delta$. Polylog is "almost as good as" the fully random $\sqrt{\log n}$ bound.

LDLQ — full statement and intuition

After incoherence processing, QUIP rounds per coordinate but with linear feedback from past errors. For $k = 1, \ldots, n$:

$\widehat W_k = Q\bigl(W_k + (W_{1:k-1} - \widehat W_{1:k-1})\,a_k\bigr),$

where $W_k$ is the $k$-th column, $a_k \in \mathbb{R}^{k-1}$, and $Q$ is element-wise rounding. Stacking columns: $\widehat W = Q(W + (W - \widehat W) U)$ with $U$ strictly upper triangular.

Why feedback helps. Without it (per-coordinate nearest), the error $W - \widehat W$ has variance $\sigma^2$ per entry, and the proxy loss is $\mathrm{tr}((W-\widehat W) H (W-\widehat W)^\top) \approx \sigma^2 \mathrm{tr}(H)$. Off-diagonal entries of $H$ correlate coordinates' contributions to the loss; without feedback, those correlations cost you.

Why $LDL^\top$. The optimal $U$ is the strict upper triangle of the Cholesky factor of $H$. Write $H = (\bar U + I) D (\bar U + I)^\top$ with $\bar U$ strictly upper triangular and $D$ diagonal. Then $U = \bar U$.

(Chee et al. Theorem 1.) Worst-case loss of LDLQ is $\tfrac{m}{4}\mathrm{tr}(D)$. Per-coordinate nearest is $\tfrac{m}{12}\mathrm{tr}(H)$; stochastic rounding is $\tfrac{m}{6}\mathrm{tr}(H)$. Since $\mathrm{tr}(D) \le \mathrm{tr}(H)$ with strict inequality unless $H$ is diagonal, LDLQ wins for any non-diagonal $H$.

Combined with Lemma 2 (incoherence implies $\mathrm{tr}(D) \le \tfrac{\mu^2}{n}\mathrm{tr}(H^{1/2})^2$): under $\mu$-incoherence, LDLQ improves over baselines by a factor $\mu^2 k/n$ where $k$ is the effective rank of $H$. This is what gives QUIP its 2-bit guarantees.

Compare with the classical signal-processing analogue: LDLQ is to per-coordinate rounding what DPCM is to PCM. Both predict the next sample from past quantized samples and quantize only the residual.

LDLQ ≡ OPTQ/GPTQ

QUIP Theorem 6: OPTQ (a.k.a. GPTQ; Frantar et al. 2023) is the same algorithm as LDLQ. OPTQ describes it iteratively with error correction; LDLQ describes it matrix-algebraically. They produce identical outputs.

Practical consequence: QUIP's implementation needs one Cholesky of $H$ and no inverse. OPTQ does an inverse plus two Choleskys. Same answer, less work.

This is also the first theoretical analysis of OPTQ — the QUIP framework retroactively explains why OPTQ works.

Hadamard matrices — basics

A Hadamard matrix $H_n \in \{-1, +1\}^{n\times n}$ has orthogonal rows: $H_n H_n^\top = n I_n$. So $\frac{1}{\sqrt n} H_n$ is orthogonal. Sylvester's construction:

$H_1 = [1], \qquad H_{2n} = \begin{bmatrix} H_n & H_n \\ H_n & -H_n \end{bmatrix}.$

This gives Hadamard matrices in every power-of-2 dimension. Other constructions (Paley, Williamson) give certain non-power-of-2 sizes; non-existence in others is the open Hadamard conjecture.

Fast Walsh-Hadamard Transform. Multiplying $H_n x$ takes $\Theta(n \log n)$ additions — no float multiplies. The recursion is

$H_{2n} x = \begin{bmatrix} x_{1:n} + x_{n+1:2n} \\ x_{1:n} - x_{n+1:2n} \end{bmatrix}\;\text{(after sub-recursion)}.$

Hardware loves this: branch-free, vectorizable, no multiplier units needed. On modern GPUs, FWHT is essentially free next to the quantized GEMM it precedes.

Why random signs are necessary

Plain $H_n$ on its own is not incoherence-inducing. If $W$ happens to align with a Hadamard column (e.g. $W$ is a known structured matrix), $H_n W$ concentrates mass in a few entries — exactly the outlier problem we wanted to avoid.

Random sign flips fix this. Let $S \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ be diagonal with independent $\pm 1$ entries (independent of $W$). The Randomized Hadamard Transform is $V = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt n} H_n S$. Two facts:

  1. Still orthogonal. $V V^\top = \tfrac{1}{n} H_n S S H_n^\top = \tfrac{1}{n} H_n H_n^\top = I$. So $V$ is a valid rotation.
  2. Worst-case immune. For any fixed $W$ (chosen before $S$), each entry of $VW$ is a sign-randomized linear combination of all of $W$. The CLT applies.

Quantitatively, QUIP\# Lemma 3.1: with $S_U, S_V$ as above, $\widetilde W = (\tfrac{1}{\sqrt m} H_m S_U) W (\tfrac{1}{\sqrt n} H_n S_V)^\top$ is $\mu_W$-incoherent with

$\mu_W = 2\sqrt{\log(4mn/\delta)}, \quad \mu_H = 2\sqrt{\log(2n^2/\delta)}.$

Compare QUIP's 2-factor Kronecker: $\mu_W^{\mathrm{Kron}} = A^2 \log^2(4Cmn/\delta)$. RHT has $\sqrt{\log}$ dependence vs. $\log^2$ for Kronecker — strictly tighter.

The CLT for RHT — Berry-Esseen detail

Each entry of $\widetilde W$ is

$\widetilde W_{ij} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{mn}} \sum_{k,\ell} (H_m)_{ik} S_{U,k} W_{k\ell} (H_n)_{j\ell} S_{V,\ell}.$

The signs $S_{U,k} S_{V,\ell}$ are independent $\pm 1$. Conditional on $W$, $\widetilde W_{ij}$ is a weighted sum of $mn$ independent random signs with weights $W_{k\ell}/\sqrt{mn}$. Variance: $\|W\|_F^2/(mn)$.

Berry-Esseen: the marginal CDF of $\widetilde W_{ij}$ deviates from a Gaussian of the same variance by $\mathcal{O}(\rho/(\sigma^3 \sqrt N))$ where $\rho$ is the third absolute moment of the summands and $N = mn$. For LLM matrices with $mn \gtrsim 10^6$, this is small.

Maximum-entry control (the actual incoherence statement) is a union bound: $\Pr[\max_{i,j}|\widetilde W_{ij}| > t] \le mn \cdot \Pr[|\widetilde W_{1,1}| > t]$. The per-entry tail is sub-Gaussian by Hoeffding (sum of $\pm 1$). Choose $t = \mu \|W\|_F/\sqrt{mn}$ with $\mu = 2\sqrt{\log(4mn/\delta)}$; union bound gives $\delta$.

BlockLDLQ — generalizing to vector quantizers

RHT-rotated weights are roughly Gaussian and ball-shaped. Per-coordinate rounding (LDLQ) packs them into a hypercube — mismatch.

BlockLDLQ extends LDLQ to vector quantization. Pick block size $g$ dividing $n$. Decompose $H = L^\top D L$ where $L$ is unit block-lower-triangular ($g\times g$ blocks; diagonal blocks all $I$, blocks above diagonal all $0$) and $D$ is block-diagonal. Set $U = L^\top - I$. Round in $g$-column blocks:

$\widehat W_k = Q_{E_8}\bigl(W_k + (W_{1:k-g} - \widehat W_{1:k-g}) A_k\bigr).$

Theorem 4.1: if $H$ is $\mu$-incoherent and $Q$ is a vector quantizer with $\mathbb{E}[(Q(x)-x)(Q(x)-x)^\top] \preceq \sigma^2 I$ for any $x \in \mathbb{R}^g$, then

$\mathbb{E}\,\mathrm{tr}\bigl((\widehat W - W) H (\widehat W - W)^\top\bigr) \le \dfrac{g m \mu^2}{n}\sigma^2 \mathrm{tr}(H^{1/2})^2.$

Same $\mathrm{tr}(H) \to \mathrm{tr}(H^{1/2})^2/n$ improvement that QUIP gets in the scalar case, now for VQ.

The E8P codebook — how 2 bits fit in 1 KB

QUIP\#'s 2-bit codebook for blocks of 8 has $2^{16}$ logical entries, but stores only $2^8$. The trick uses $E_8$'s symmetry.

$E_8 = \widehat D_8 \cup (\widehat D_8 + \tfrac12)$ where $\widehat D_8 = \{x \in \mathbb{Z}^8 + \tfrac12 : \mathbf{1}^\top x \in 2\mathbb{Z}\}$ is the half-integer even-parity sublattice. Each entry of $\widehat D_8$ factors as $|s| \odot \sigma$ where $|s| \in |\widehat D_8|$ (absolute values) and $\sigma$ is a sign vector of correct parity.

Storage: 8 bits to index into 256 absolute-value entries, 7 bits of explicit sign flips (the 8th sign is determined by parity), 1 bit to choose $\widehat D_8$ vs. $\widehat D_8 + \tfrac12$. Total: 16 bits = 2 bits/weight, 256-entry table = 1 KB. Fits in L1 even after $32\times$ bank-conflict duplication on a GPU.

Decode: lookup, sign-flip, optional half-shift. A handful of ALU ops per 8-block.

Practical caveats not on the slides

  • Calibration data. $H = \mathbb{E}_x[xx^\top]$ requires a small calibration set of activations. Quality depends on representativeness; QUIP/QUIP\# typically use a few hundred sequences.
  • Per-channel scales. Different output channels have different weight magnitudes. QUIP's Algorithm 1 line 4 diagonally rescales $W$ and $H$ before incoherence processing, trading off the spectrum.
  • Higher bitrates. $E_8$ at $K = 256$ is good at $\sim 2$ bits/weight. For 3 or 4 bits, QUIP\# uses Residual Vector Quantization (RVQ): quantize, then quantize the residual, repeat. Stays inside $E_8$'s structure at every step.
  • Fine-tuning. QUIP\# adds an inter-layer fine-tuning pass after quantization, recovering some perplexity. This is QAT-lite, much cheaper than full QAT, and sits on top of (not instead of) the lattice quantization.
  • Activation quantization. The deck and these notes cover only weight quantization. Activation and KV-cache quantization is the topic of Lecture 4 (TURBOQUANT).
  • Dimensions not power of 2. Sylvester Hadamard needs $n$ a power of 2. QUIP\# factors $n = pq$ with $p$ the largest power-of-2 factor, then uses $V = H_p \otimes H_q$ — a hybrid Hadamard-Kronecker scheme. RFFT (random FFT with random complex phase) is the fallback when no Hadamard factorization is available.
