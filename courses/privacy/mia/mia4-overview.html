MIA 4 · Modern Likelihood-Ratio Attacks — Overview

A one-page tour of Lecture 4. For full slides see mia4-modern.html; for detailed derivations see mia4-modern-note.html.

One-line summary. Lecture 4 traces the shift from balanced-accuracy MIA evaluations to per-example likelihood-ratio attacks (LiRA), then to the cost-reduced relative-comparison attack (RMIA). All modern MIAs share the same shape: a likelihood ratio over a per-example statistic, calibrated either by shadow models, reference models, or population samples.

§1 Defenses recap

Four families: regularization, output perturbation, training-time, architecture design. Only training-time defenses change the model's information content; everything else is an arms race that adaptive attackers undo. DP-SGD is the unique defense with formal guarantees: clipped gradients plus Gaussian noise yield an $(\varepsilon, \delta)$-DP bound, which in turn upper-bounds MIA advantage by $e^{\varepsilon}-1+\delta$.

§2 Why early evaluations misled

Balanced accuracy at the 50% threshold hides the vulnerable subpopulation. The right metric is TPR at low FPR (typically $10^{-3}$ or lower), evaluated on log-log ROC. Label-only attacks (Choquette-Choo et al. 2020) sweep away the "just hide the confidence vector" defense — even with predicted-class-only access, boundary-distance probes recover membership signal.

§3 LiRA (Carlini et al. 2022)

The Neyman–Pearson optimal attack, executed per example.

  • Idea. For each target $z$, train $N$ shadow models with $z$ in/out of their training sets. Fit Gaussians to the resulting "in" and "out" loss distributions. Compute the per-example LLR for the actual target model.
  • Score. $\Lambda(z) = p(\ell^* \mid z \in D) / p(\ell^* \mid z \notin D)$, where $\ell^*$ is the target's loss on $z$.
  • Cost. 64–256 shadow models per attack budget — research-grade compute.
  • Result. 8–10% TPR at 0.1% FPR on CIFAR-10, vs. <1% for prior attacks. The 10–50× gain is what made the community accept per-example calibration as the new default.
  • Online vs. offline. Online retrains shadows per query (best); offline reuses a fixed shadow set (practical).

§5 RMIA (Zarifzadeh et al. 2024)

Cuts LiRA's cost by 30–100× while preserving most of the power.

  • Idea. Replace the per-example Gaussian fit with a population-relative percentile rank.
  • Score. For each candidate $z$, compute $r(z) = p_\theta(z) / \frac{1}{R}\sum_r p_{\theta_r}(z)$ for a few reference models, then rank against the same ratio on a population sample.
  • Cost. 2–8 reference models, independent of how many points you query.
  • Result. ~11% TPR at 0.1% FPR on CIFAR-10 with 8 reference models — within a couple of points of LiRA at a fraction of the compute.

§6 Beyond classification — diffusion models

Diffusion training minimises a denoising loss across timesteps $t \in [0, T]$. Membership signal lives in the intermediate timesteps (small $t$ is too easy, large $t$ is pure noise). Aggregating the per-timestep reconstruction loss and applying LiRA-style per-example calibration recovers the same per-record auditing power as in classification. The lecture's "One Framework: All Attacks" slide makes this universality explicit: every modern attack is a likelihood ratio over some chosen statistic $\phi(z)$.

Three things to remember

  1. Per-example calibration is the entire game. A global threshold cannot distinguish a memorized outlier from an easy non-member.
  2. Evaluate at low FPR. Balanced accuracy is the wrong number for any threat model auditors actually care about.
  3. DP-SGD is still the only formal defense. Everything else loses to an adaptive attacker.

Bridge to Lecture 5

The LiRA recipe — "train many shadow models, fit per-example densities" — collapses for large language models. Pre-training a single 7B-parameter LLM costs millions of dollars; 256 shadows is impossible. Lecture 5 builds MIA techniques engineered around that constraint: shadow-free (neighbourhood, self-prompt, context-aware) and token-weighted (InfoRMIA).
