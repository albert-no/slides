Why we cannot just use $\log p_\theta(x)$ directly

The integral $\int p_\theta(x,z)dz$ requires summing over all possible latent codes, weighted by their joint likelihood with $x$. For a continuous latent of dimension $\sim 100$ and a flexible decoder neural network, this integral has no closed form and Monte Carlo estimation has astronomical variance: most $z$ samples have negligible $p_\theta(x,z)$, so a uniform-in-$z$ sampler almost never sees the relevant mass.

The variational posterior $q_\phi(z\mid x)$ is an importance-sampling proposal: it concentrates probability mass on the $z$ values that explain $x$, so the importance ratio $p_\theta(x,z)/q_\phi(z\mid x)$ is well-behaved. Jensen on the log-importance-ratio yields the ELBO.

The KL gap is informative, not just non-negative

From the second derivation:

\[ \log p_\theta(x)-\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ELBO}}=D_{\mathrm{KL}}\bigl(q_\phi(\cdot\mid x)\,\Vert\,p_\theta(\cdot\mid x)\bigr). \]

Two consequences:

  1. Maximizing the ELBO simultaneously increases $\log p_\theta$ (good for the model) and decreases the KL gap (better posterior approximation).
  2. The bound is tight when the variational posterior matches the true one. So ELBO maximization drives $q_\phi$ toward the true $p_\theta(z\mid x)$.

This double interpretation is why ELBO is also called variational EM: the encoder approximates the true posterior; the decoder refines the model.

The reparameterization trick is more than a coding hack

It is a special case of the pathwise derivative estimator in stochastic optimization. The general form is

\[ \nabla_\phi\,\mathbb{E}_{z\sim q_\phi}[f(z)] \;=\;\mathbb{E}_\varepsilon\bigl[\nabla_\phi f(g_\phi(\varepsilon))\bigr], \]

and applies whenever $z=g_\phi(\varepsilon)$ for parameter-free $\varepsilon$. The Gaussian case is the canonical example. Other cases:

  • Logistic ($z=\sigma(\mu+\sigma\,\varepsilon)$ with $\varepsilon$ logistic);
  • Categorical via Gumbel-softmax: $z\propto\operatorname{softmax}((\log\pi_i+G_i)/\tau)$ with $G_i$ Gumbel.

The alternative is the score-function estimator (REINFORCE): $\nabla_\phi\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}[f(z)]=\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}[f(z)\nabla_\phi\log q_\phi(z)]$. Always applicable but generally higher variance. VAEs use reparameterization for the variance benefit.

The closed-form Gaussian KL for VAE

Direct computation, using $p=\mathcal{N}(0,I)$ and $q=\mathcal{N}(\mu,\operatorname{diag}(\sigma^2))$:

\[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\,\Vert\,p)=\mathbb{E}_q[\log q-\log p]=\sum_j\Bigl[\tfrac{\mu_j^2+\sigma_j^2}{2}-\tfrac12-\log\sigma_j+\log 1\Bigr]. \]

Simplify: $\frac12\sum_j(\mu_j^2+\sigma_j^2-1-\log\sigma_j^2)$. Each term is non-negative; equals zero iff $\mu_j=0$ and $\sigma_j=1$, which is the prior.

This closed form makes ELBO completely differentiable with respect to encoder parameters — the KL term needs no Monte Carlo at all.

Posterior collapse and "free bits"

In language and image models with very expressive decoders, the optimization dynamics often push $q_\phi(z\mid x)\to p(z)$ during training. KL goes to zero; the latent codes nothing. Two diagnostics:

  1. Per-dimension KL: compute $D_{\mathrm{KL}}\bigl(q_\phi(z_j\mid x)\,\Vert\,p(z_j)\bigr)$ averaged over $x$. Healthy dimensions have KL $\gtrsim 0.1$ nats; collapsed dimensions sit near $0$.
  2. Mutual information: $I(X;Z)=\mathbb{E}_x[D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z\mid x)\,\Vert\,q_\phi(z))]$ where $q_\phi(z)=\mathbb{E}_x[q_\phi(z\mid x)]$ is the aggregate posterior. Should be positive.

The "free bits" trick clamps the per-dimension KL at a minimum: $\max(\lambda, D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_j\,\Vert\,p_j))$ for each dimension $j$. The optimizer can then "spend" KL up to $\lambda$ for free, encouraging usage.

The ELBO: one quantity, six faces

The slides present the ELBO in several forms across Lectures 1 and 2. They are all the same number, rewritten by choosing different factorizations of the joint $p_\theta(x,z)$. Here is the complete map.

Form 1 — Definition (log importance ratio)

\[ \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ELBO}} \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z\mid x)}\!\left[\log\frac{p_\theta(x,z)}{q_\phi(z\mid x)}\right]. \]

Origin: Jensen on $\log p_\theta(x) = \log\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}[p_\theta(x,z)/q_\phi(z\mid x)] \ge \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}[\log(\cdots)]$. This is the master form.

Form 2 — KL gap identity

\[ \log p_\theta(x) \;=\; \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ELBO}} \;+\; D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\bigl(q_\phi(\cdot\mid x)\,\Vert\,p_\theta(\cdot\mid x)\bigr). \]

Derivation: split $\log p_\theta(x,z) = \log p_\theta(z\mid x) + \log p_\theta(x)$ inside Form 1. The marginal $\log p_\theta(x)$ is constant under $\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}$ and pulls out; what remains is $-D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi \,\Vert\, p_\theta(\cdot\mid x))$. Names the gap: ELBO is loose by exactly the posterior KL.

Form 3 — Reconstruction minus Rate

\[ \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ELBO}} \;=\; \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}[\log p_\theta(x\mid z)]}_{\text{reconstruction}} \;-\; \underbrace{D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\bigl(q_\phi(z\mid x)\,\Vert\,p(z)\bigr)}_{\text{rate}}. \]

Derivation: split $p_\theta(x,z) = p(z)\,p_\theta(x\mid z)$ (the other factorization — prior times likelihood) inside Form 1. The $\log p(z) - \log q_\phi(z\mid x)$ piece becomes $-D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi\,\Vert\,p)$. This is the trainable decomposition: explain $x$ well, but stay close to the prior.

Key relationship: Forms 2 and 3 use different seams of the joint

Form 2 splits $p_\theta(x,z)$ as $p_\theta(z\mid x)\cdot p_\theta(x)$ (posterior $\times$ marginal), giving the gap identity. Form 3 splits it as $p(z)\cdot p_\theta(x\mid z)$ (prior $\times$ likelihood), giving the trainable loss. Same ELBO, different algebra.

Form 4 — Hierarchical ELBO (Lecture 2)

\[ \log p_\theta(x_0) \;\ge\; \mathbb{E}_q\!\bigl[\log p_\theta(x_{0:T}) - \log q(x_{1:T}\mid x_0)\bigr]. \]

Literally Form 1 with $z \to (x_1,\ldots,x_T)$.

Form 5 — Telescoped: $L_T + \sum L_{t-1} + L_0$

\[ -\log p_\theta(x_0) \;\le\; \underbrace{D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q(x_T\mid x_0)\,\Vert\,p(x_T))}_{L_T} + \sum_{t=2}^{T} L_{t-1} + \underbrace{(-\log p_\theta(x_0\mid x_1))}_{L_0}. \]

Derivation: expand both the forward chain $q(x_{1:T}\mid x_0)=\prod_t q(x_t\mid x_{t-1})$ and the reverse chain $p_\theta(x_{0:T})=p(x_T)\prod_t p_\theta(x_{t-1}\mid x_t)$ inside Form 4. Products telescope, leaving one KL per timestep plus boundary terms.

Form 6 — MSE on the mean

\[ L_{t-1} \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{q(x_t\mid x_0)}\!\bigl[D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\bigl(q(x_{t-1}\mid x_t,x_0)\,\Vert\,p_\theta(x_{t-1}\mid x_t)\bigr)\bigr] \;=\; \frac{1}{2\tilde\beta_t}\,\mathbb{E}\!\bigl[\|\tilde\mu_t(x_t,x_0)-\mu_\theta(x_t,t)\|^2\bigr]. \]

Both sides Gaussian with the same covariance $\tilde\beta_t I$. KL between same-covariance Gaussians is Mahalanobis distance between means, which is scaled MSE. This is the actual loss: predict $\tilde\mu_t(x_t,x_0)$ from $(x_t,t)$.

Summary: the derivation chain

Form 1 (definition) $\to$ Form 2 (split as posterior $\times$ marginal $\Rightarrow$ KL gap) or Form 3 (split as prior $\times$ likelihood $\Rightarrow$ recon $-$ rate). Form 4 = Form 1 with $T$ latents $\to$ Form 5 (telescope products $\Rightarrow$ $L_T+\sum L_{t-1}+L_0$) $\to$ Form 6 (both Gaussians, same $\Sigma$ $\Rightarrow$ MSE on mean). Each arrow is one algebraic step.

The information-theoretic ELBO interpretation in more detail

Rewrite the ELBO over the data distribution:

\[ \mathbb{E}_x[\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ELBO}}] \;=\;\mathbb{E}_{x,z\sim p_{\text{data}}q_\phi}[\log p_\theta(x\mid z)]\;-\;\mathbb{E}_x[D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(\cdot\mid x)\,\Vert\,p)]. \]

The KL term equals $I_{q_\phi}(X;Z)+D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z)\,\Vert\,p(z))$ (decomposing total variational rate into mutual information plus marginal mismatch).

Maximizing ELBO trades off:

  • better reconstruction (negative distortion),
  • against lower mutual information $I(X;Z)$ (rate),
  • plus a marginal-matching term that pulls the aggregate posterior toward the prior.

This is the "$\beta$-VAE" tradeoff family in information-theoretic clothing.

Why diffusion is "the next step"

Two unsatisfying aspects of vanilla VAEs that diffusion improves on:

  1. Posterior collapse. A trained encoder is fragile. A fixed forward process (Gaussian noise injection) cannot collapse.
  2. Latent is poorly structured. $z\in\mathbb{R}^d$ is informative but not interpretable. Diffusion's "latents" are noisy versions of $x$ at increasing noise levels — explicit, structured.

Lecture 2 unpacks this: a diffusion model is a hierarchical VAE with $T$ latents $X_1,\ldots,X_T$, a frozen forward process, and a learned reverse process.
