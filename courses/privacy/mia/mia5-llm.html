01

What Makes LLM MIA Hard

Threat models, perplexity, the calibration challenge

From Classifiers to LLMs

One-pass training

Trillions of tokens, each seen ~once.

No multi-epoch overfit to lean on.

Unknown training data

Web crawl; no clean "in/out" partition.

Shadows infeasible

Cannot retrain a 7B model 256 times.

LiRA template breaks.

Variable-length text

Score is a per-token NLL summary, not a scalar loss.

Three Threat Models

Pre-training

"In Pile / C4 / web crawl?"

Hardest. Very weak signal.

Fine-tuning

"In RLHF / instruction set?"

Moderate signal — multi-epoch, small data.

Context

"In current prompt?"

Easiest. Strong signal.

Research focus: pre-training (hardest) and fine-tuning.

The Perplexity Baseline

Per-token NLL summary — LLM analogue of the loss threshold:

$$\text{PPL}(x) \;=\; \exp\!\left(-\tfrac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^T \log p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t})\right)$$

Attack: $\mathcal{A}(x) = \mathbf{1}[\text{PPL}(x) < \tau]$. Low PPL $\Rightarrow$ member.

Failure: low PPL on "Hello, how are you?" — trained or not.

Conflates memorization with fluency.

The Calibration Challenge

Need a per-example null: how surprised would the model be if $x$ were not trained on?

Low PPL $\neq$ Member

Common boilerplate is low-PPL by design.

High PPL $\neq$ Non-member

Rare jargon stays high-PPL even after training.

Every modern LLM MIA is one question: how do we build that null cheaply?

Two Knobs Every Attack Turns

Signal

Per-sequence PPL, per-token NLL, variance across contexts, …

Reference (null)

Smaller LM, paraphrases, self-generations, population of texts.

The lecture is a tour of signal $\times$ reference choices.

02

Calibration Strategies

Four key ideas for building the null

Reference Model (Carlini et al. 2021)

Use a different LM as the null. Compare ratios.

$$s(x) \;=\; \log p_\theta(x) \;-\; \log p_{\text{ref}}(x)$$
  • $p_{\text{ref}}$ — smaller LM, or zlib compression length
  • Cancels per-sequence intrinsic difficulty
  • Cheap; signal still weak on pre-training
Mattern et al. 2023

Neighbourhood: Paraphrases as Null

Mask-and-fill with T5 to make $K$ paraphrases $\tilde{x}^{(k)}$.

$$s(x) \;=\; \log p_\theta(x) \;-\; \tfrac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K \log p_\theta(\tilde{x}^{(k)})$$

Memorized: $\log p_\theta(x) \gg$ paraphrases.

Generic: $\log p_\theta(x) \approx$ paraphrases.

Fu et al. 2023

SPV-MIA: Self-Prompted Null

The target LLM generates its own reference text.

  • Prefix-complete $x$ — members regenerate close to original
  • No external reference model needed
  • Strong for fine-tuning detection: 0.80–0.90 AUC

Key idea: fine-tuning collapses the model toward training texts.

Self-generations reveal the collapse.

Chang et al. 2024

Context-Aware: Variance as Signal

Score $x$ under many prefixes $c \in \mathcal{C}$:

$$s(x) \;=\; \text{Var}_{c \in \mathcal{C}}\bigl[\log p_\theta(x \mid c)\bigr]$$
  • Members: loss drops under the right context — high variance
  • Non-members: insensitive to context — low variance
  • Memorization is text-in-context, not text alone

Token-Level: Not All Tokens Are Equal

Per-token surprise $I_t = -\log p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t})$. Common tokens carry no signal.

The study was at Stanford by Dr. Sarah Chen in 2019 with 1,247 participants

filler predictable for anyone    specific predictable only if memorized

Takeaway: averaging over all tokens dilutes the signal.

Weighting or selecting high-surprise tokens amplifies it — motivates Min-K% & InfoRMIA next.

Shi et al. 2024 · Tao & Shokri 2025

Min-K% and InfoRMIA

Min-K% (Shi et al.): average the surprise of the $K\%$ least likely tokens.

InfoRMIA (Tao & Shokri): weighted token-wise log-ratio against a reference:

$$s(x) \;=\; \sum_{t \,\in\, \mathcal{T}_{\text{info}}} w_t \,\log \frac{p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t})}{p_{\text{ref}}(x_t \mid x_{<t})}$$

$\mathcal{T}_{\text{info}}$: high reference-surprise positions (entities, numbers, rare words).

$w_t$: informativeness weight.

Info-theoretic view: pick $\mathcal{T}_{\text{info}}, w_t$ to maximise total $I(z \in D ;\, x_t \mid x_{<t})$.

03

The Empirical Wall

Limits of MIA on pre-trained LLMs

The Headline Question

Modern LLM MIA papers publish steady AUC gains. Are these real?

  • Classifier MIA at scale: TPR@0.1% FPR around 10% (LiRA on CIFAR-10)
  • Pre-trained LLM MIA: TPR@1% FPR around 1–5%, AUC 0.55–0.70
  • The gap is large — and it does not close with bigger attacks

Three 2024–2025 papers argue the gap is structural, not just under-optimised.

Duan et al. 2024 · COLM

"Do MIAs Work on LLMs?"

Evaluated 5 leading MIAs against Pythia / GPT-Neo on the Pile.

  • AUC barely above 0.5 across settings
  • Apparent wins on WikiMIA came from temporal shift (older members vs. newer non-members)
  • model trained between the two — attack detects pre/post-cutoff style, not membership
  • Controlling for distribution: signal vanishes

Diagnosis: one-pass training over trillions of tokens leaves almost no overfit signal.

Hayes et al. 2025

Limits of Strong Attacks at Scale

Scaled LiRA — the strongest known attack — to GPT-2 (10M–1B params), 20B+ tokens on C4.

  • 256 shadow models, full per-example calibration
  • Best case: AUC < 0.7 in practical settings
  • Stronger attacks don’t close the gap — ceiling is structural
Hayes, Shumailov, Choquette-Choo, Jagielski, Kaissis, Nasr et al., Exploring the limits of strong membership inference attacks on large language models, 2025.
Hayes et al. 2025

Per-Record Instability

Even when AUC is above chance, individual predictions are unstable.

  • Re-running the same attack on the same record flips many calls
  • Training-seed variability swamps the membership signal
  • "Person $X$’s data is in the model" is not a reliable claim

Average AUC overstates what a real auditor can act on.

For high-stakes per-record claims, pre-training MIA is not reliable.

Maini et al. 2024

Blind Baselines Beat MIA

You can separate member / non-member sets without touching the model:

  • Text length, n-gram statistics, source domain
  • These features alone often beat published MIAs on benchmarks
  • Many "MIA wins" measure dataset construction, not memorization

If a blind baseline matches your attack, your benchmark has a distribution leak.

Why Pre-Training MIA Is Hard

No overfit

Yeom: $\text{Adv} \le R_{\text{pop}} - R_{\text{train}}$.

That gap is tiny for a one-pass LLM.

Massive class

Member & non-member texts both drawn from the same web.

Distributions overlap.

Seed noise

Training stochasticity perturbs per-record losses more than membership does.

No clean ground truth

Training data unknown, deduplication blurs "membership".

04

Where the Signal Still Lives

Fine-tuning, extraction, defenses, law

Fine-Tuning MIA Works

Why it’s easier

Multi-epoch training on small data.

Members are seen many times; non-members never.

Classical overfit signal returns.

What works

SPV-MIA, neighbourhood, reference-LM.

AUC 0.80–0.90 on LLaMA / GPT-2 fine-tunes.

Real privacy risk lives in fine-tuning, not pre-training.

Extractable $\subset$ Inferable $\subset$ Trained-on

Two notions of "the model saw $x$" — one strictly stronger than the other.

Trained-on — every $x$ the model saw during training
Inferable: $\exists$ test that calls $x$ a member > chance
Extractable: $\exists$ prompt $p$ with $f_\theta(p) = x$ verbatim

Point: extraction is a demonstration, MIA is a test.

After §03 — per-record MIA unreliable — extraction is the only signal that survives the calibration problem.

MIA as Extraction’s Ranker

Generate candidates
→
Rank by MIA score
→
Verify top-$k$ as training text
  • MIA can fail per-record but still rank well in bulk
  • Top-of-distribution leakage: rare, repeated, distinctive
  • This is the operational use of MIA on pre-trained LLMs

Defenses

DP fine-tuning

DP-SGD on the fine-tuning step.

Practical; bounds MIA advantage by $e^\varepsilon - 1 + \delta$.

DP pre-training

Open challenge.

Trade-off too severe at trillion-token scale.

Deduplication

Memorization scales with repetition (Carlini).

Dedup the corpus before training.

Curation & filtering

Strip PII, IDs, secrets pre-training.

Necessary; not sufficient.

Legal Landscape

GDPR Right to Erasure

Article 17: request deletion of personal data.

What does "delete from a trained model" mean?

MIA as test — if still detectable, not deleted.

NYT v. OpenAI

ChatGPT reproduces NYT articles verbatim.

Extraction (not MIA) provides clean legal evidence.

MIA → legal verification: useful, but the empirical wall bounds what auditors can claim.

Synthesis

Unified framework, open problems

Every Attack: One Template

$$\mathcal{A}(z) \;=\; \mathbf{1}\!\left[\frac{p(\text{signal} \mid z \in D)}{p(\text{signal} \mid z \notin D)} > \tau\right]$$

Methods differ in two choices — signal and null.

Classifier vs. LLM: the template is the same; only what you can build the null from changes.

Key Takeaways

  • LLM MIA = same LR template, harder null estimation
  • Four signature ideas: paraphrase, self-prompt, context variance, token weighting
  • On pre-training: AUC ceiling around 0.7 even with strong attacks (Hayes et al.)
  • On fine-tuning: AUC 0.80–0.90 — the practical risk
  • For legal evidence: prefer extraction over MIA when possible

Open Problems

Beat the AUC wall

Can any attack reach AUC 0.8 on pre-training without distribution-shift artefacts?

Per-record reliability

Auditing needs stable per-record calls, not just average AUC.

Multi-modal MIA

CLIP, GPT-4V, Gemini — almost unexplored.

MIA on agents / RAG

Training set vs. retrieval corpus — new threat models.
