01

Compression for Relevance

Not all bits are worth keeping

The Central Question

Observe input $X$. Care about target $Y$.

Compress $X$ into a representation $T$.

Goal: keep what $T$ says about $Y$, discard the rest.

Classic compression minimizes $I(X;T)$ for fixed distortion on $X$.
IB compression minimizes $I(X;T)$ for fixed information about $Y$.

From Source Coding to Relevance

Rate-distortion (lossy/)

Minimize $I(X;T)$

Subject to $\mathbb{E}[d(X,T)] \le D$

Distortion on input space

Reconstruct $X$ from $T$

Information Bottleneck

Minimize $I(X;T)$

Subject to $I(T;Y) \ge I_0$

Relevance in target space

Predict $Y$ from $T$

Both are MI minimization problems. The difference: what the representation must preserve.

$I(X;T)$ is the rate: the number of bits the representation carries about the input. This is the same rate as in Shannon's source coding theorem.

The Markov Constraint

$T$ depends on $X$ only, not on $Y$ directly:

$Y$ $\longleftrightarrow$ $X$ $\longrightarrow$ $T$

The encoder $p(t|x)$ is a stochastic map from $X$ to $T$.

Markov chain $Y - X - T$: all information $T$ has about $Y$ flows through $X$.

IB Lagrangian

Definition (Information Bottleneck, Tishby et al., 2000).

The IB problem minimizes over all stochastic maps $p(t|x)$:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{IB}} = I(X;T) - \beta\, I(T;Y), \qquad \beta \ge 0.$$

$I(X;T)$: compression cost (rate in the source coding sense).

$I(T;Y)$: relevance reward (bits $T$ preserves about $Y$).

$\beta$ trades rate against prediction. Single-letter: no block length, no asymptotics.

Tishby, Pereira, and Bialek, "The Information Bottleneck Method", 1999.

Two Extremes of $\beta$

$\beta \to 0$: maximum compression

Only $I(X;T)$ matters.

Optimal: $T \perp X$ (trivial map).

$I(T;Y) = 0$: no prediction.

$\beta \to \infty$: maximum relevance

Only $I(T;Y)$ matters.

Optimal: $T$ is a sufficient statistic of $X$ for $Y$.

$I(T;Y) = I(X;Y)$: full information.

Finite $\beta$ interpolates: a controlled lossy summary that keeps the relevant bits.

DPI Constrains the Problem

Recall (Data Processing Inequality). If $Y - X - T$ is Markov, then $I(T;Y) \le I(X;Y)$.

The ceiling on relevance is $I(X;Y)$: no representation can create information.

The IB problem asks: among all $T$ with $I(X;T) \le R$, which achieves the highest $I(T;Y)$?

Exercise. Show that DPI also gives $I(T;Y) \le I(X;T)$ when $Y - X - T$. Hint: apply DPI to the Markov chain $Y - X - T$, then to $T - X - Y$.

02

Self-Consistent Equations

The optimal encoder is a Gibbs distribution

Optimization Setup

Minimize over all stochastic maps $p(t|x)$:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{IB}}[p(t|x)] = I(X;T) - \beta\, I(T;Y),$$

subject to normalization $\sum_t p(t|x) = 1$ for each $x$.

The joint $p(x,y)$ is fixed and known. The only free variable is the encoder $p(t|x)$.

Both MI terms depend on $p(t|x)$, but also on $p(t)$ and $p(y|t)$, which are derived quantities.

Derived Quantities

Given the encoder $p(t|x)$ and the known joint $p(x,y)$:

$$\begin{aligned} p(t) &= \sum_x p(x)\, p(t|x), \\[6pt] p(y|t) &= \sum_x p(y|x)\, \frac{p(x)\, p(t|x)}{p(t)}. \end{aligned}$$

The cluster marginal $p(t)$ and the cluster-conditional $p(y|t)$ are both functions of the encoder.

Optimal Encoder

Theorem (IB Optimal Encoder, Tishby et al., 2000).

The encoder that minimizes $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{IB}}$ has the form:

$$p(t|x) = \frac{p(t)}{Z(x,\beta)}\, \exp\!\bigl(-\beta\, D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(p(y|x)\,\big\|\, p(y|t)\right)\bigr),$$

where $Z(x,\beta) = \sum_t p(t)\, \exp\!\bigl(-\beta\, D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p(y|x)\| p(y|t))\bigr)$ is the partition function.

Proof — Variational Derivative

Lagrange multipliers $\lambda(x)$ for normalization. Set $\frac{\delta\mathcal{L}}{\delta p(t|x)} = 0$.

The $I(X;T)$ term contributes:

$$\frac{\partial\, I(X;T)}{\partial\, p(t|x)} = p(x)\,\log \frac{p(t|x)}{p(t)}.$$

The $I(T;Y)$ term contributes:

$$\frac{\partial\, I(T;Y)}{\partial\, p(t|x)} = p(x)\sum_y p(y|x)\,\log \frac{p(y|t)}{p(y)}.$$

Proof — Isolate $p(t|x)$

Set derivative to zero, solve for $\log p(t|x)$:

$$\log p(t|x) = \log p(t) + \beta \sum_y p(y|x)\,\log \frac{p(y|t)}{p(y)} + c(x).$$

Rewrite the sum via KL decomposition:

$$\sum_y p(y|x)\log\frac{p(y|t)}{p(y)} = -D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p(y|x)\|p(y|t)) + D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p(y|x)\|p(y)).$$

Proof — Conclusion

$D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p(y|x)\|p(y))$ does not depend on $t$: absorb into $Z(x,\beta)$.

Exponentiate:

$$p(t|x) = \frac{p(t)}{Z(x,\beta)}\,\exp\!\bigl(-\beta\, D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p(y|x)\| p(y|t))\bigr). \quad\square$$

The encoder is a Gibbs distribution: assignment cost = KL between the input's and the cluster's predictive distributions.

Self-Consistency

The theorem gives three coupled equations:

$$\begin{aligned} p(t|x) &= \frac{p(t)}{Z(x,\beta)}\,\exp\!\bigl(-\beta\, D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p(y|x)\|p(y|t))\bigr), \\[6pt] p(t) &= \sum_x p(x)\, p(t|x), \\[6pt] p(y|t) &= \sum_x p(y|x)\, p(x|t). \end{aligned}$$

Each equation depends on the output of the others.

Solve by alternating iteration, exactly like Blahut–Arimoto for rate-distortion.

Reading the Encoder

Gibbs form: $p(t|x) \propto p(t)\,e^{-\beta\, D_{\mathrm{KL}}}$.

  • $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p(y|x)\|p(y|t))$ = cost of assigning $x$ to cluster $t$
  • Low KL: the cluster predicts $Y$ almost as well as $x$ does
  • High KL: the cluster distorts $x$'s predictive structure

$\beta$ controls softness: large $\beta$ sharpens the encoder toward hard assignment; small $\beta$ spreads mass across clusters.

The encoder is a soft relevance-weighted clustering of the input space.

03

IB as Rate-Distortion

Log-loss distortion unifies the two problems

Recall — Rate-Distortion

Recall (Rate-distortion function). $R(D) = \min_{p(t|x):\, \mathbb{E}[d(X,T)] \le D}\; I(X;T).$

Minimizes the rate $I(X;T)$ subject to a distortion constraint $\mathbb{E}[d(X,T)] \le D$.

The Lagrangian: $I(X;T) + \beta\,\mathbb{E}[d(X,T)]$.

Classical distortions: squared error, Hamming distance.

Question: is there a distortion that turns R(D) into the IB problem?

Key Identity

Lemma (Relevance Decomposition).

For $Y - X - T$ Markov:

$$I(T;Y) = I(X;Y) - \mathbb{E}_{p(x,t)}\!\left[D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(p(y|x)\,\big\|\,p(y|t)\right)\right].$$

Relevance = input MI minus expected prediction distortion.

The gap $\mathbb{E}[D_{\mathrm{KL}}]$ measures how much $T$ degrades prediction of $Y$ relative to $X$.

Proof of Relevance Decomposition

$$\begin{aligned} \mathbb{E}_{p(x,t)}\!\left[D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p(y|x)\|p(y|t))\right] &= \sum_{x,t} p(x,t)\sum_y p(y|x)\log\frac{p(y|x)}{p(y|t)} \\ &= -H(Y|X) + H(Y|T) \\ &= I(X;Y) - I(T;Y). \quad\square \end{aligned}$$

Split the log ratio. First sum gives $-H(Y|X)$ (marginalizing over $t$).

Second sum: $\sum_x p(x|t) p(y|x) = p(y|t)$, so the cross-entropy becomes $H(Y|T)$.

IB = R(D) with Log-Loss

Theorem (IB-RD Equivalence).

Define the log-loss distortion:

$$d_{\mathrm{IB}}(x,t) = D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(p(y|x)\,\big\|\,p(y|t)\right).$$

Then the IB Lagrangian $I(X;T) - \beta\,I(T;Y)$ is equivalent to the R(D) Lagrangian $I(X;T) + \beta\,\mathbb{E}[d_{\mathrm{IB}}(X,T)]$, up to a constant $\beta\, I(X;Y)$.

Proof. Substitute the Relevance Decomposition Lemma:

$$I(X;T) - \beta\, I(T;Y) = I(X;T) + \beta\,\mathbb{E}[d_{\mathrm{IB}}] - \beta\, I(X;Y). \quad\square$$

Why Log-Loss Is Natural

  • $d_{\mathrm{IB}}(x,t) = 0 \;\Longleftrightarrow\; p(y|x) = p(y|t)$: zero distortion iff the cluster predicts $Y$ perfectly from $x$'s perspective
  • Cross-entropy loss in supervised learning measures the same gap
  • The Blahut–Arimoto iteration for R(D) with $d_{\mathrm{IB}}$ recovers the IB self-consistent equations

IB is rate-distortion where the distortion measures prediction quality, not reconstruction fidelity.

04

The Information Plane

Visualizing the compression-relevance tradeoff

The Information Plane

Definition (Information Plane).

For a representation $T$ of $X$ with target $Y$, the information plane is the space $(I(X;T),\; I(T;Y))$.

Every encoder $p(t|x)$ maps to a single point in this plane.

$x$-axis: compression cost. $y$-axis: relevance reward.

Goal: move up (more relevance) and left (more compression).

DPI Constrains the Region

The Markov chain $Y - X - T$ imposes two ceilings:

$$\begin{aligned} I(T;Y) &\le I(X;Y), \\ I(T;Y) &\le I(X;T). \end{aligned}$$

The achievable region lies below both lines.

The identity $T = X$ sits at the corner $(H(X),\; I(X;Y))$: maximum cost, maximum relevance.

The trivial $T \perp X$ sits at the origin $(0, 0)$.

The IB Curve

The IB curve is the optimal frontier in the information plane:

$$I_{\mathrm{IB}}(R) = \max_{p(t|x):\; I(X;T) \le R}\; I(T;Y).$$

Properties:

  • Concave and monotonically non-decreasing
  • Starts at $(0,0)$ and ends at $(H(X), I(X;Y))$
  • Slope at each point is $1/\beta$ (Lagrange multiplier)

Analogous to the R(D) curve: a fundamental limit on the tradeoff.

Phase Transitions

As $\beta$ increases from $0$, the IB solution undergoes structural phase transitions:

  • $\beta < \beta_1$: trivial solution, $T \perp X$, single cluster
  • $\beta = \beta_1$: first bifurcation, two clusters emerge
  • $\beta$ increases: successive bifurcations, finer clustering
  • $\beta \to \infty$: each $x$ maps to its own cluster

The critical $\beta_1$ depends on the spectral gap of $p(y|x)$.

IB produces a hierarchy of representations at different resolutions, controlled by a single parameter.

DNN Layers in the Plane

An $L$-layer network produces representations $T_1, \ldots, T_L$:

$Y$ $\longleftrightarrow$ $X$ $\to$ $T_1$ $\to$ $T_2$ $\to$ $\cdots$ $\to$ $T_L$

DPI gives a monotone chain in the plane:

$$I(X;T_1) \ge I(X;T_2) \ge \cdots \ge I(X;T_L).$$

Each layer can only lose information about $X$ (and hence about $Y$).

Layers trace a path from $(H(X), I(X;Y))$ toward the origin.

Toward Deep Learning

The information plane gives a diagnostic lens for deep networks:

  • Does training move layers toward the IB curve?
  • Do networks compress during training?
  • How to compute $I(X;T)$ for continuous, high-dimensional $T$?

The IT Perspective

The IB reframes representation learning as an information-theoretic optimization:

  • MI as currency: both cost and reward measured in bits
  • DPI as constraint: no representation can exceed $I(X;Y)$
  • R(D) as structure: the IB curve is a rate-distortion function
  • Blahut-Arimoto as algorithm: the same iteration solves both problems

Every concept in the IB was already in the course: the IB is the bridge that connects classical information theory to modern deep learning.

Summary

IB problem: $\min_{p(t|x)} I(X;T) - \beta\, I(T;Y)$ subject to $Y - X - T$ Markov.

Optimal encoder: Gibbs form $p(t|x) \propto p(t)\,\exp\!\bigl(-\beta\, D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p(y|x)\|p(y|t))\bigr)$.

Rate-distortion view: IB $\equiv$ R(D) with log-loss $d(x,t) = D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p(y|x)\|p(y|t))$.

Information plane: $(I(X;T), I(T;Y))$ bounded by DPI; IB curve is the optimal frontier.
