Divergence 2 · Fisher Divergence and Score Matching — Notes

Why $f$-divergences don't work for energy-based models

The $f$-divergence ratio $p(x)/q(x)$ requires both $p$ and $q$ to be properly normalized. If $p_\theta(x) = e^{\phi_\theta(x)}/Z(\theta)$, evaluating any $f$-divergence requires knowing $Z(\theta)$. Estimating $Z$ is the central computational problem of energy-based models — importance sampling, annealed importance sampling, contrastive divergence, all are workarounds for the intractable partition function.

Fisher divergence side-steps the problem at the formulation level, not the algorithm level. The score function $\nabla_x \log p_\theta(x) = \nabla_x \phi_\theta(x)$ doesn't see $Z$ at all — the gradient kills the constant. This is the philosophical reason Fisher divergence exists: it's the divergence that can see energy-based models without first solving normalization.

The original score-matching trick (Hyvärinen 2005)

Before Vincent's denoising version, Hyvärinen handled the unknown data score by integration by parts. The Fisher divergence (after expanding the square and dropping $\theta$-free constants) reduces to:

\[ J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{p_{\mathrm{data}}}\!\left[\tfrac12 \|s_\theta(X)\|^2 + \mathrm{tr}\bigl(\nabla s_\theta(X)\bigr)\right]. \]

The trace-of-Jacobian term is $\sum_i \partial s_{\theta,i}/\partial x_i$ — the divergence (in the differential-geometry sense) of the score field. This works in principle but requires $K$ second-derivative evaluations per sample, which is prohibitive for neural networks with $K$ in the millions.

Vincent's denoising form replaces the trace term with a single noise sample and a stop-gradient on the noisy input. The price: a bias proportional to the smoothing $\sigma^2$.

Why integration by parts works for Gaussian smoothing

In the proof, Step 2 uses $\nabla f_{\mathrm{data}}(\mathbf{y}) = \int p_{\mathrm{data}}(\mathbf{x}) \nabla_{\mathbf{y}}\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{y};\mathbf{x},\sigma^2 I)\,d\mathbf{x}$. The gradient passes inside the integral because $\mathcal{N}$ is differentiable in $\mathbf{y}$. For the Gaussian, $\nabla_{\mathbf{y}}\log\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{y};\mathbf{x},\sigma^2 I) = (\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y})/\sigma^2$ — a closed form.

The trick fails for other smoothing kernels (e.g., uniform): you'd still get a tractable identity, but the conditional MSE form $\|(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y})/\sigma^2 - s_\theta(\mathbf{y})\|^2$ depends on the Gaussian's specific score. This is why denoising score matching is intimately tied to Gaussian noise — it's the kernel that gives a clean closed form.

Tweedie's formula — full statement and proof

Theorem (Tweedie / Robbins 1956 / Miyasawa 1961). Let $\mathbf{Y} = \mathbf{X} + \mathbf{Z}$ with $\mathbf{Z}\sim\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\sigma^2 I)$ independent of $\mathbf{X}\sim p_X$. Then

\[ \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{X}\mid\mathbf{Y}=\mathbf{y}] = \mathbf{y} + \sigma^2\nabla_{\mathbf{y}}\log p_Y(\mathbf{y}). \]

Proof. $p_Y(\mathbf{y}) = \int p_X(\mathbf{x})\,\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{y};\mathbf{x},\sigma^2 I)\,d\mathbf{x}$. Compute

\[ \nabla_{\mathbf{y}} p_Y(\mathbf{y}) = \int p_X(\mathbf{x})\,\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{y};\mathbf{x},\sigma^2 I)\,\tfrac{\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}}{\sigma^2}\,d\mathbf{x} = \tfrac{1}{\sigma^2}\bigl(\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{X}\mid\mathbf{y}] - \mathbf{y}\bigr)\,p_Y(\mathbf{y}), \]

where the last equality uses Bayes' rule $p_X(\mathbf{x})\,\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{y};\mathbf{x},\sigma^2 I)/p_Y(\mathbf{y}) = p_{X\mid Y}(\mathbf{x}\mid\mathbf{y})$. Divide by $p_Y$ to get $\sigma^2\nabla\log p_Y = \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{X}\mid\mathbf{y}] - \mathbf{y}$. $\quad\blacksquare$

Tweedie is the bridge: the conditional mean of the clean signal, an object you can train (it's a denoising regression target), equals the score of the noisy marginal, an object you want (it generates samples by Langevin dynamics).

Why diffusion training $=$ score matching $=$ denoising

The three equivalences in the deck collapse onto one chain:

\[ \underbrace{\text{ELBO MSE on } (\mathbf{m}_t, \boldsymbol\mu_t)}_{\text{Lecture 2 of Diffusion track}} \;\stackrel{\text{Tweedie}}{=}\; \underbrace{\sigma_t^4\,\|\Delta\text{score}\|^2}_{\text{Fisher divergence at } t} \;\stackrel{\text{Vincent}}{=}\; \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\,\|(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{z}_t)/\sigma_t^2 - s_\theta(\mathbf{z}_t)\|^2}_{\text{denoising score matching}}. \]

The reason this matters in practice: every diffusion implementation that "predicts noise $\hat\varepsilon_\theta(\mathbf{z}_t,t)$" is, by the second equality, an implicit score model up to scaling. The score-matching literature and the diffusion literature train identical networks with identical losses — just under different framings.

Sliced score matching (Song et al. 2019), conditional score matching (Song & Ermon 2019, NCSN), and the DDPM noise-prediction parameterization (Ho et al. 2020) are all instances of this equivalence. The "right" framing depends on what you want to compute next: ELBO bounds (variational), sampling (Langevin / SDE), or simply training loss (DSM).

Why sampling from a score model is Langevin dynamics

Once $s_\theta \approx \nabla\log p_{\mathrm{data}}$ is trained, sampling proceeds via discretized Langevin:

\[ \mathbf{x}_{k+1} = \mathbf{x}_k + \tfrac{\eta}{2}\,s_\theta(\mathbf{x}_k) + \sqrt{\eta}\,\boldsymbol\varepsilon_k, \qquad \boldsymbol\varepsilon_k\sim\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},I). \]

The stationary distribution of this chain (in the continuous-time limit) is exactly the pdf whose score is $s_\theta$ (Fokker–Planck argument). This is why score-based generative models generate: training fits the score, and the Langevin SDE turns the score field into samples.

The chain is also a continuous-time view of diffusion's reverse process — see privacy/generative/diffusion3-sde-score.html:339 for the SDE formulation.

Comparison — $f$, Fisher, Wasserstein

$f$-divergence. $D_f(p\Vert q) = \mathbb{E}_q\,f(p/q)$. Needs normalized $p,q$. Family includes KL, JS, $\chi^2$, TV. Variational dual gives BA / DV / NWJ bounds (MI) and $f$-GAN.

Fisher divergence. $D_F(p\Vert q) = \tfrac12\,\mathbb{E}_p\,\|\nabla\log p - \nabla\log q\|^2$. Works on energy models. Trainable by denoising score matching. Drives diffusion, NCSN, EBM training.

Wasserstein-$1$. $W_1(p,q) = \inf_\pi\,\mathbb{E}_\pi\|X-Y\|$. Cares about transport, not ratios. Survives disjoint supports. Drives WGAN; not covered in this course.

The choice is dictated by what the model class lets you compute: ratios (normalized models), scores (energy / diffusion), or distances (geometric models). The training algorithm follows from the divergence, not the other way around.

Closing thread — where this course goes next

Across the infotheory series, divergences have anchored every operational result: KL for source coding (Lecture 1–3), differential KL for AWGN capacity (Lecture 6–8), KL for rate–distortion (Lecture 9–12), KL again for ELBO and diffusion (Diffusion 1–3), variational KL for MI bounds (MI 1–2), and now the $f$-family and Fisher for modern generative training.

The unifying view: every operational task (compress, communicate, estimate, sample) is "match a distribution to a target", and the choice of divergence dictates the algorithm. Information theory gives you the divergence; statistical learning gives you the optimizer.
