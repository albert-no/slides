Why Membership Inference Matters

The central question, stakes, and threat landscape

The Central Question

Mechanism $M$ (training algorithm or query) on dataset $D$, target record $x$:

Was $x$ used to produce $M$'s output?

Membership inference: fundamental privacy attack against ML systems.

Spans genomics, statistics, ML. Foundation for extraction and inversion.

Throughout: $M$ = mechanism, $D$ = training dataset, $x$ (or $z$) = target record, $\theta$ = model parameters, $f_\theta$ = trained model, $\ell(f_\theta,z)$ = loss at $z$.

Why MIA Matters

Legal & Regulatory

GDPR: right to know.

MIA is the empirical test.

Privacy Auditing

Empirical lower bounds on leakage.

Gateway Attack

Foundation for extraction and inversion.

MIA is to ML privacy what IND-CPA is to crypto: the minimal attack any private system must resist.

IND-CPA (indistinguishability under chosen-plaintext attack): adversary picks $m_0,m_1$, sees one ciphertext, cannot tell which message was encrypted.

Real-World Stakes

  • 2008, Homer et al.: identifiable from aggregate genomic data; NIH closes public databases
  • 2017, Shokri et al.: MIA vs Google, Amazon ML-as-a-service
  • 2021, Carlini et al.: training data extracted from GPT-2
  • 2023, NYT v. OpenAI: MIA as legal evidence of data use

Homer et al. 2008

The genomic attack that started it all

Homer et al. 2008

Why This Paper Existed

Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) published aggregate allele frequencies for cases (e.g., cancer patients) so other labs could replicate findings.

  • NIH-funded GWAS routinely posted these summary tables on public portals (dbGaP).
  • Conventional wisdom: aggregates over hundreds of people reveal nothing about individuals.
  • Homer et al. asked: is that actually true?

This is the first membership inference attack: given the mechanism's output (published frequencies) and a target individual, decide $x \in D$ or $x \notin D$.

Homer et al., "Resolving Individuals Contributing Trace Amounts of DNA...", PLoS Genetics 2008
Homer et al. 2008

Homer et al. 2008

Genomics in 60 Seconds

  • SNP (single-nucleotide polymorphism): a position in the genome where individuals often differ — e.g., one person has A, another has G.
  • Allele: which letter you carry at that position. Each person has two copies (one from each parent), so the allele count at a SNP is $0$, $1$, or $2$.
  • Genotype array: ~500,000 SNPs measured per individual.
  • Allele frequency at SNP $j$: average allele count across some set of people — a single number in $[0,1]$ (after scaling).

GWAS published the per-SNP frequencies for the case cohort — that's the aggregate Homer attacked.

Homer et al. 2008

Three Ingredients

Individual

$Y_{i,j}$: target's allele at SNP $j$ (their own DNA).

Mixture / Study

$M_j$: published allele frequency at SNP $j$ — average over the study cohort.

Population

$\text{Pop}_j$: allele frequency at SNP $j$ in a reference population (e.g., HapMap).

Member: target's genotype $Y_i$ on average closer to mixture $M$ than to population $\text{Pop}$ (they helped shape $M$).

Homer et al. 2008

The Distance Statistic

For individual $i$, SNP $j$:

$$D(Y_{i,j}) = |Y_{i,j} - M_j| - |Y_{i,j} - \text{Pop}_j|$$

Member:

  • $Y_{i,j}$ close to $M_j$ (contributed)
  • $|Y_{i,j} - M_j|$ small
  • $D(Y_{i,j}) < 0$ on average

Non-member:

  • $Y_{i,j}$ close to $\text{Pop}_j$ (population baseline)
  • $|Y_{i,j} - \text{Pop}_j|$ small
  • $D(Y_{i,j}) \approx 0$ on average
Homer et al. 2008

Aggregation: Weak to Strong

Each SNP: tiny signal. Aggregate ~500,000 SNPs via one-sample $t$-test:

$$T(Y_i) = \frac{\overline{D}(Y_i)}{s_D / \sqrt{n_{\text{SNP}}}}$$

Many weak signals $\to$ $p < 10^{-6}$. Same principle powers MIA in ML.

Homer et al. 2008

Key Results

  • Detected 0.1% contributions to mixture DNA
  • Zero false positives across 3 microarray platforms
  • Works in 40+ individual mixtures
  • 10,000 to 50,000 SNPs suffice for $p < 10^{-6}$
  • Parental genotypes usable as proxies
SNPs UsedDetection Threshold
10,000~10% mixture contribution
50,000~1% mixture contribution
500,000~0.1% mixture contribution

More SNPs: smaller detectable contributions, stronger attack.

Homer et al. 2008

Real-World Impact: NIH Database Closure

Direct response: NIH emergency closure of public GWA databases.

  • Summary statistics (allele frequencies, genotype counts): withdrawn
  • Replaced with controlled-access protocols (data use agreements)
  • Precedent: aggregate statistics ≠ privacy protection
  • Motivated differential privacy for genomic data

First major policy change from an MI-style attack.

Recap: Reconstruction Attacks

Reconstruction attacks: adversary recovers training data from mechanism output.

$$\text{Reconstruction:} \quad \mathcal{A}(M(D)) \to \hat{D} \approx D$$
  • Strong attack: recovers actual training records
  • Natural for aggregate queries (census, histograms)
  • Dinur-Nissim: reconstruction from $O(n)$ linear queries

Question: Is defeating reconstruction sufficient for privacy?

Reconstruction: Insufficient for Privacy

Mechanism that samples a random training record:

$$M(D) = D_i \quad \text{where } i \sim \text{Uniform}(\{1, \ldots, n\})$$

Defeats Reconstruction

One record says nothing about the other $n-1$.

Fails Privacy

Adversary sees own record $\to$ certain membership.

Defeating reconstruction $\neq$ privacy.

Need a stronger notion.

Cryptographic Analogy: IND-CPA

Crypto security: not "recover the plaintext" but:

IND-CPA: Distinguish encryptions of $m_0$ vs $m_1$?

Plaintext Recovery

Recover the message.

Too weak: partial leaks pass.

Indistinguishability

Cannot tell which message.

Correct notion.

Same shift for privacy: reconstruction to indistinguishability.

The Sampling Mechanism Failure

Neighboring databases differing in one record $x$:

$$D = \{x_1, \ldots, x_n\} \qquad D' = D \setminus \{x\}$$

Sampling mechanism $M(D) = D_i$:

  • $M(D)$ outputs $x$ with probability $\frac{1}{n}$
  • $M(D')$ outputs $x$ with probability $0$
  • Perfectly distinguishable: check if output is $x$

The right question: can an adversary distinguish $M(D)$ from $M(D')$?

Privacy = Indistinguishability → MIA

Reconstruction

Recover training data.

Too strong: defeat doesn't imply privacy.

Membership Inference

Distinguish if $x$ was in training.

Right notion: aligns with DP.

Key insight: MIA is the fundamental privacy attack.

04

The Membership Inference Game

Formal framework and hypothesis testing

The Membership Inference Game

MIA as a game: challenger (system, with secret membership bit) vs adversary $\mathcal{A}$ (an algorithm with some access to the mechanism's output).

  • Challenger holds database $D$, target $x$
  • Coin flip: $b \xleftarrow{\$} \{0, 1\}$
  • $b = 1$: run on $D_1 = D \cup \{x\}$. $b = 0$: run on $D_0 = D \setminus \{x\}$
  • Adversary $\mathcal{A}$ observes $M(D_b)$, outputs guess $\hat{b}\in\{0,1\}$
  • Wins if $\hat{b} = b$

Binary hypothesis test: distinguish $M(D_0)$ from $M(D_1)$.

Neighboring Databases

MI game on neighboring databases: differ in one individual.

$$D_1 = D \cup \{x\}, \quad D_0 = D \setminus \{x\}$$

Same adjacency as DP:

$$(\varepsilon, \delta)\text{-DP:} \quad \Pr[M(D_1) \in S] \leq e^\varepsilon \Pr[M(D_0) \in S] + \delta$$

DP: statistical closeness across databases. MIA tests it empirically.

Adversary's Advantage

Adversary's advantage:

$$\text{Adv}_{\text{MI}}(\mathcal{A}) = \left|\Pr[\mathcal{A}(M(D_1)) = 1] - \Pr[\mathcal{A}(M(D_0)) = 1]\right|$$

Random guess

$\text{Adv} = 0$

No info gained

Perfect attack

$\text{Adv} = 1$

Full leakage

Typical attack

$\text{Adv} \in (0, 1)$

Partial leakage

Total variation distance between $M(D_1)$ and $M(D_0)$ as seen by $\mathcal{A}$.

Connection to Hypothesis Testing

MIA is a binary hypothesis test against the mechanism's output distribution:

$H_0$: Non-member

$x \notin D$, observation $\sim M(D_0)$

$H_1$: Member

$x \in D$, observation $\sim M(D_1)$

Adversary observes $T = g(M(D_b))$, decides hypothesis.

  • Type I (FPR): $\alpha = \Pr[\hat{b}=1 \mid b=0]$
  • Type II (FNR): $\beta = \Pr[\hat{b}=0 \mid b=1]$
  • Power (TPR) $= 1 - \beta$

Exactly the framing from DP Lecture 2 (HT interpretation of DP) — now we run the adversary's test against the mechanism, not the other way around.

Test Statistic

Test statistic $T(x) = g(M(D), x)$ — function of observable signal.

Neyman–Pearson: Setup

Simple-vs-simple test: observation $T$, two hypotheses with known densities $p_0, p_1$.

A (randomized) test is a function $\phi(T)\in[0,1]$ — the probability of declaring $H_1$.

Size (FPR)

$\alpha(\phi) = \mathbb{E}_{H_0}[\phi(T)]$

Power (TPR)

$\beta(\phi) = \mathbb{E}_{H_1}[\phi(T)]$

Goal: at significance level $\alpha_0$, find $\phi$ maximizing power $\beta(\phi)$ subject to $\alpha(\phi)\le\alpha_0$.

Neyman–Pearson Lemma

The most powerful test at level $\alpha_0$ is the likelihood-ratio test:

$$\phi^*(T) \;=\; \mathbb{1}\!\left[\;\Lambda(T) \;=\; \frac{p_1(T)}{p_0(T)} \;>\; \tau\;\right],$$

with threshold $\tau$ chosen so $\alpha(\phi^*) = \alpha_0$.

  • No other test achieves higher TPR at the same FPR.
  • The shape of the optimal decision boundary is a level set of $\Lambda$.
  • Ties on $\{\Lambda = \tau\}$ may be broken with a randomized fraction $\gamma$ to hit $\alpha_0$ exactly.

Neyman–Pearson: One-Line Proof

Compare $\phi^*$ to any test $\phi$ with $\alpha(\phi)\le\alpha_0=\alpha(\phi^*)$. For every $T$:

$$\big(\phi^*(T)-\phi(T)\big)\,\big(p_1(T)-\tau\, p_0(T)\big) \;\ge\; 0.$$
  • If $\Lambda(T)>\tau$: $\phi^*=1\ge\phi$ and $p_1-\tau p_0>0$ — product $\ge 0$.
  • If $\Lambda(T)<\tau$: $\phi^*=0\le\phi$ and $p_1-\tau p_0<0$ — product $\ge 0$.

Integrate over $T$:

$$\beta(\phi^*)-\beta(\phi)\;\ge\;\tau\big(\alpha(\phi^*)-\alpha(\phi)\big)\;\ge\;0.\quad\square$$

What This Means for MIA

Plug in the MIA hypotheses: $p_b(T)=$ density of test statistic $T$ when $b\in\{0,1\}$.

$$\Lambda(T) \;=\; \frac{p(T\mid x\in D)}{p(T\mid x\notin D)}\;\gtrless\;\tau$$

Take-away

Every "optimal" MIA, at any access level, is a likelihood-ratio test.

Each later attack just estimates $p_0,p_1$ differently.

Practical challenge

$p_0,p_1$ unknown — depend on $D$, training algorithm, target $x$.

Drives shadow models (Lecture 2) and LiRA (Lecture 4).

Error Types in MIA Context

Type I (False Positive)

Declare $x \in D$ when $x \notin D$

Rate: FPR = $\alpha$

Type II (False Negative)

Declare $x \notin D$ when $x \in D$

Rate: FNR = $\beta$

Low FPR matters most. Legal/audit: TPR at FPR $\leq 0.1\%$.

DP, MI, and the Attack Taxonomy

Connections to differential privacy and evaluation

Recap: HT Interpretation of DP

From DP Lecture 2: if $M$ is $\varepsilon$-DP, then for any binary test between $M(D_1)$ and $M(D_0)$ with errors $(\alpha, \beta)$ (= FPR, FNR):

$$1 \;\le\; \alpha + \beta\, e^{\varepsilon}, \qquad 1 \;\le\; \beta + \alpha\, e^{\varepsilon}.$$

For $(\varepsilon,\delta)$-DP, the same bounds hold with an extra $\delta$ slack on each side.

Both errors cannot be small at once — exactly what a defender wants against MIA.

DP Caps the MIA ROC

Rearrange the second inequality with $\text{TPR}=1-\beta$:

$$\text{TPR} \;\le\; e^{\varepsilon}\,\text{FPR} + \delta.$$
  • Under $(\varepsilon,\delta)$-DP, the ROC of every adversary lies below the line $y = e^{\varepsilon} x + \delta$.
  • The other inequality gives a symmetric upper bound from the top: $\text{TPR}\le 1-(1-\text{FPR})e^{-\varepsilon}+\delta$.
  • Intersected, these define the Kairouz–Oh–Viswanath privacy region (also $f$-DP).

Auditing flip: if measured (FPR, TPR) violates the inequality, the claimed $\varepsilon$ is wrong.

Corollary: Advantage Bound

Setting $|\text{TPR}-\text{FPR}|$ over all thresholds:

$$\text{Adv}_{\text{MI}}(\mathcal{A}) \;\le\; e^{\varepsilon}-1+\delta.$$
$\varepsilon$$\delta$Max MI Advantage
0.1$10^{-5}$$\le 0.105$
1.0$10^{-5}$$\le 1.718$
8.0$10^{-5}$$\le 2980$ (vacuous)

Small $\varepsilon$: strong MIA protection. Large $\varepsilon$: bound is vacuous — but the ROC bound on the previous slide may still be useful at low FPR.

MI as Privacy Auditing Tool

MI advantage $\alpha$ against $M$ $\Rightarrow$ $M$ is not $(\varepsilon, \delta)$-DP for any $e^\varepsilon - 1 + \delta < \alpha$.

  • Train model with your pipeline
  • Run best available MI attack
  • Attack success: empirical lower bound on leakage
  • Bound exceeds target $\varepsilon$: pipeline not private enough

Privacy Attack Taxonomy

Membership Inference

Was $x$ in training?

Binary decision on a known target.

Attribute Inference

Given partial record $x_{\setminus s}$, infer sensitive attribute $s$.

E.g.: adversary knows ZIP+age of a training patient, queries the model to recover diagnosis.

Data Extraction

Recover verbatim training records from the model alone.

E.g.: prompt GPT-2 to regurgitate a memorised email/phone number.

Model Inversion

Reconstruct a representative input for a given class/label.

E.g.: synthesise an "average face" of class $y$ from a face-recognition model.

MIA is the weakest (easiest) of the four: any system that fails MIA also fails the rest.

Black-box ≈ White-box

Under a Bayesian model of training (posterior on $\theta$ given $D$), the optimal MI test reduces to:

$$\Lambda(z) \;\approx\; \frac{p(\theta\mid z\in D)}{p(\theta\mid z\notin D)} \;\propto\; \exp\!\big(-\beta\,[\ell(\theta,z)-\tau(z)]\big).$$
  • Optimal attack depends on $\theta$ only through the loss $\ell(\theta,z)$ at $z$.
  • Extra white-box features (gradients, activations, full $\theta$) add no asymptotic advantage.
  • The per-sample threshold $\tau(z)$ calibrates difficulty — foreshadows LiRA.
  • Empirical: black-box and white-box attacks track within $<1\%$ AUC.
Sablayrolles et al., "White-box vs Black-box: Bayes Optimal Strategies for Membership Inference", ICML 2019

ROC Curve — Definition

Score-based attack uses a real-valued statistic $T$ and threshold $\tau$:

$$\hat b \;=\; \mathbb{1}[\,T > \tau\,].$$

Sweeping $\tau$ traces the ROC curve:

$$\text{ROC} \;=\; \{(\text{FPR}(\tau),\;\text{TPR}(\tau)) \,:\, \tau\in\mathbb{R}\}.$$

Starts at $(0,0)$, ends at $(1,1)$ — each point is one operating threshold.

ROC Curve — Diagram

AUC: Area Under the ROC

Integral form:

$$\text{AUC} \;=\; \int_0^1 \text{TPR}(\text{FPR})\,d(\text{FPR}).$$

Equivalent probabilistic form — pick a random member $z^+$ and non-member $z^-$:

$$\text{AUC} \;=\; \Pr\!\big[\,T(z^{+}) > T(z^{-})\,\big].$$

AUC — Diagram & Reference Values

  • $\text{AUC}=0.5$: random — score gives no info.
  • $\text{AUC}=1.0$: every member scores above every non-member.
  • Typical reported MIA: $0.55$–$0.7$.

Why AUC Misses MIA Risk

AUC averages over all thresholds. Most of that area lives at high FPR — useless for privacy.

Two attacks with the same AUC can differ wildly in the low-FPR regime auditors care about.

55% balanced accuracy can hide:

  • 10% TPR at 0.1% FPR (one attack)
  • 0% TPR at 0.1% FPR (another)

Average hides the vulnerable subpopulation.

TPR @ Low FPR — The Right Metric

Carlini et al. 2022: "60% balanced accuracy with 0% TPR at 0.1% FPR is useless in practice."

  • Threat model: auditor flags high-confidence positives — only low FPR matters.
  • Report TPR @ FPR = $10^{-3}$ (and $10^{-4}$, $10^{-5}$).
  • Plot ROC on log-log axes — stretches the low-FPR regime instead of compressing it.
