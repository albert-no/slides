Diffusion 4 — Companion Notes

Speaker's reading companion. Formal statements and full proofs for the DDIM deck (non-Markovian forward, σ-budget, pair-vs-triple invariance, deterministic sampling, probability-flow ODE).

1. What the DDPM loss actually sees

1.1 The training loss

The DDPM ε-prediction loss reads

$$L(\theta) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{n,\,X^{(0)},\,\epsilon}\!\left[\tilde\lambda_n\,\bigl\|\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta\bigl(\sqrt{\bar\alpha_n}\,X^{(0)} + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_n}\,\epsilon,\,n\bigr)\bigr\|^2\right].$$

Both inputs to $\epsilon_\theta$ are sampled by drawing $X^{(0)} \sim p_{\mathrm{data}}$, $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,I)$, $n \sim \mathrm{Unif}\{1,\ldots,N\}$, and forming $X^{(n)}$ as a single linear combination.

1.2 Only the single-time marginal enters

Proposition 1 (loss invariance to joint structure). Suppose two forward laws $q_1$ and $q_2$ on $X^{(0:N)}$ share the conditional marginal
$$q_1(X^{(n)} \mid X^{(0)}) \;=\; q_2(X^{(n)} \mid X^{(0)}) \quad\text{for every } n = 1, \ldots, N.$$
Then $L(\theta)$ takes the same value under either forward law.

Proof. The expectation in $L(\theta)$ marginalizes over $(X^{(0)}, n, X^{(n)})$ with $X^{(n)} \mid X^{(0)} \sim q_i$. The integrand depends only on $(X^{(0)}, n, X^{(n)})$, not on the rest of the chain. By assumption, the joint $(X^{(0)}, n, X^{(n)})$ has the same law under $q_1$ and $q_2$. $\square$

1.3 Consequence — design freedom

Any forward law that preserves the DDPM single-time conditional marginals $\mathcal{N}(\sqrt{\bar\alpha_n}\,X^{(0)},\,(1-\bar\alpha_n)I)$ admits the same trained ε-network. DDIM exploits this: it constructs a non-Markovian joint with the same marginals but a tunable joint structure parameterized by $\{\sigma_n\}_{n=1}^N$.

2. The non-Markovian forward construction

2.1 Construction order

For each $X^{(0)}$, the DDIM forward joint $q_\sigma(X^{(0:N)})$ is built backward in $n$:

  1. Sample the endpoint marginal $X^{(N)} \mid X^{(0)} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\bigl(\sqrt{\bar\alpha_N}\,X^{(0)},\,(1-\bar\alpha_N) I\bigr)$.
  2. For $n = N{-}1, N{-}2, \ldots, 0$, sample $X^{(n)} \mid (X^{(n+1)}, X^{(0)})$ from a Gaussian to be specified.

The joint conditions on both the next step and the clean signal — this is what makes it non-Markovian in $X^{(n)}$ alone (Markov in the augmented state $(X^{(n)}, X^{(0)})$).

2.2 Mixing recipe (signal + recycle + fresh)

Given $(X^{(n+1)}, X^{(0)})$, define

$$X^{(n)} \;=\; \underbrace{\sqrt{\bar\alpha_n}\,X^{(0)}}_{\text{signal}} \;+\; \underbrace{a_n\,\epsilon_{n+1}}_{\text{recycle}} \;+\; \underbrace{\sigma_{n+1}\,\eta}_{\text{fresh}},$$

where

  • $\epsilon_{n+1} := \dfrac{X^{(n+1)} - \sqrt{\bar\alpha_{n+1}}\,X^{(0)}}{\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_{n+1}}}$ — the noise inside $X^{(n+1)}$ given $X^{(0)}$. Distribution: $\mathcal{N}(0, I)$ given $X^{(0)}$.
  • $\eta \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$, independent of $\epsilon_{n+1}$ given $X^{(0)}$.
  • $a_n,\,\sigma_{n+1} \geq 0$ are coefficients to be determined.

The "recycle" term reuses the noise already injected at step $n+1$; the "fresh" term adds new noise. The coefficient $\sigma_{n+1}$ controls how much fresh noise enters; $\sigma_{n+1} = 0$ means the step is purely deterministic given $(X^{(n+1)}, X^{(0)})$.

2.3 Pinning down $a_n$

Demand the DDPM single-time marginal $X^{(n)} \mid X^{(0)} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\bigl(\sqrt{\bar\alpha_n}\,X^{(0)},\,(1-\bar\alpha_n) I\bigr)$. The mean of the recipe is $\sqrt{\bar\alpha_n}\,X^{(0)}$ (matching). Variance, conditional on $X^{(0)}$, by independence:

$$\mathrm{Var}(X^{(n)} \mid X^{(0)}) \;=\; a_n^2 + \sigma_{n+1}^2 \;\stackrel{!}{=}\; 1-\bar\alpha_n.$$

Solving for $a_n$ in terms of $\sigma_{n+1}$:

$$a_n \;=\; \sqrt{1 - \bar\alpha_n - \sigma_{n+1}^2}, \qquad \sigma_{n+1} \in \bigl[0,\,\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_n}\bigr].$$

One linear equation in two unknowns leaves $\sigma_{n+1}$ as a free knob: it splits the variance budget $1-\bar\alpha_n$ between recycled and fresh noise.

2.4 Forward conditional in closed form

Given $(X^{(n+1)}, X^{(0)})$, only $\eta$ is random. Reading off the recipe:

$$\begin{aligned} q_\sigma\bigl(X^{(n)} \mid X^{(n+1)}, X^{(0)}\bigr) &= \mathcal{N}\!\bigl(\mu_n,\, \sigma_{n+1}^2 I\bigr), \\ \mu_n &= \sqrt{\bar\alpha_n}\,X^{(0)} + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_n-\sigma_{n+1}^2}\,\frac{X^{(n+1)} - \sqrt{\bar\alpha_{n+1}}\,X^{(0)}}{\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_{n+1}}}. \end{aligned}$$

The mean depends on both $X^{(n+1)}$ and $X^{(0)}$, so $q_\sigma$ is not a simple chain conditioned on $X^{(n+1)}$ alone — it is non-Markovian in the unaugmented state.

3. Pair invariance: same pair, different triple

3.1 Theorem

Theorem 1 (pair invariance). For every $n \in \{0, 1, \ldots, N\}$ and every choice of $\{\sigma_n\}$,
$$q_\sigma\bigl(X^{(n)} \mid X^{(0)}\bigr) \;=\; \mathcal{N}\!\bigl(\sqrt{\bar\alpha_n}\,X^{(0)},\,(1-\bar\alpha_n) I\bigr) \;=\; q_{\mathrm{DDPM}}\bigl(X^{(n)} \mid X^{(0)}\bigr).$$
In particular, the joint law of the pair $(X^{(0)}, X^{(n)})$ under $q_\sigma$ matches its DDPM counterpart.

3.2 Proof — backward induction

Base case ($n = N$). By construction, $X^{(N)} \mid X^{(0)} \sim \mathcal{N}(\sqrt{\bar\alpha_N}\,X^{(0)},\,(1-\bar\alpha_N) I)$.

Inductive step. Assume $X^{(n+1)} \mid X^{(0)} \sim \mathcal{N}(\sqrt{\bar\alpha_{n+1}}\,X^{(0)},\,(1-\bar\alpha_{n+1}) I)$. Conditional on $X^{(0)}$, $\epsilon_{n+1}$ and $\eta$ are independent standard Gaussians. The recipe gives

$$\mathbb{E}[X^{(n)} \mid X^{(0)}] = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_n}\,X^{(0)},\qquad \mathrm{Var}(X^{(n)} \mid X^{(0)}) = a_n^2 + \sigma_{n+1}^2 = (1-\bar\alpha_n - \sigma_{n+1}^2) + \sigma_{n+1}^2 = 1-\bar\alpha_n.$$

Note the cancellation: $\sigma_{n+1}^2$ enters $a_n^2$ with a minus and $\sigma_{n+1}^2 \cdot 1$ with a plus, leaving $1-\bar\alpha_n$ independent of $\sigma_{n+1}$. $\square$

3.3 Triple — what changes with $\sigma$

The joint $(X^{(0)}, X^{(n+1)}, X^{(n)})$ does depend on $\sigma_{n+1}$: the conditional mean $\mu_n$ above and the conditional variance $\sigma_{n+1}^2$ both vary. So:

  • The DDPM ε-loss, which depends only on the pair, is invariant to $\sigma$. ε-networks trained on DDPM are reusable for any DDIM sampler.
  • The sampler's behavior — degree of stochasticity, step accuracy, inversion property — depends entirely on $\sigma$.

3.4 DDPM as a special case

Choose

$$\sigma_{n+1}^2 \;=\; \beta_{n+1}\,\frac{1-\bar\alpha_n}{1-\bar\alpha_{n+1}} \;=\; \tilde\beta_{n+1}.$$

This is exactly the closed-form posterior variance from Lecture 2 (Theorem 5). One can verify that with this choice, the DDIM mean $\mu_n$ collapses to the DDPM posterior mean $\tilde\mu_{n+1}(X^{(n+1)}, X^{(0)})$, and the joint becomes Markov.

3.5 The deterministic limit

Set $\sigma_n = 0$ for all $n$. Then $a_n = \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_n}$ and $q_\sigma(X^{(n)} \mid X^{(n+1)}, X^{(0)})$ is a Dirac at

$$X^{(n)} \;=\; \sqrt{\bar\alpha_n}\,X^{(0)} + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_n}\,\epsilon_{n+1},$$

the recycled noise alone. Pair invariance still holds — the marginal stays Gaussian — but the joint is now functional: given $X^{(0)}$ and any one $X^{(n)}$, every other $X^{(k)}$ is determined.

4. Sampling: predicted clean signal

4.1 Setup

At sampling time, $X^{(0)}$ is unknown. The forward conditional $q_\sigma(X^{(n)} \mid X^{(n+1)}, X^{(0)})$ requires it. DDIM substitutes a network-derived estimate.

4.2 The estimator

Invert the marginal $X^{(n+1)} = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_{n+1}}\,X^{(0)} + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_{n+1}}\,\epsilon$ for $X^{(0)}$ and substitute the network's noise prediction $\epsilon_\theta(X^{(n+1)}, n+1)$ for $\epsilon$:

$$\hat X^{(0)}\bigl(X^{(n+1)}\bigr) \;=\; \frac{X^{(n+1)} - \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_{n+1}}\,\epsilon_\theta(X^{(n+1)}, n+1)}{\sqrt{\bar\alpha_{n+1}}}.$$

If $\epsilon_\theta$ were exact, $\hat X^{(0)}$ would equal the true conditional mean $\mathbb{E}[X^{(0)} \mid X^{(n+1)}]$ (this is Tweedie applied at level $n+1$). In practice, $\hat X^{(0)}$ is the network's running guess for the clean sample.

4.3 The DDIM reverse update

Substitute $\hat X^{(0)}$ into the forward conditional. The "recycle" term simplifies:

$$\frac{X^{(n+1)} - \sqrt{\bar\alpha_{n+1}}\,\hat X^{(0)}}{\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_{n+1}}} \;=\; \epsilon_\theta(X^{(n+1)}, n+1).$$

So the update reads

$$X^{(n)} \;=\; \underbrace{\sqrt{\bar\alpha_n}\,\hat X^{(0)}}_{\text{toward data}} \;+\; \underbrace{\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_n - \sigma_{n+1}^2}\,\epsilon_\theta(X^{(n+1)}, n+1)}_{\text{noise direction}} \;+\; \underbrace{\sigma_{n+1}\,Z^{(n)}}_{\text{fresh noise}},$$

with $Z^{(n)} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$ independent of all else.

5. Deterministic DDIM and the probability-flow ODE

5.1 Deterministic update

Set $\sigma_n = 0$. The reverse step is

$$X^{(n)} \;=\; \sqrt{\bar\alpha_n}\,\hat X^{(0)} + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_n}\,\epsilon_\theta(X^{(n+1)}, n+1).$$

Substituting $\hat X^{(0)} = (X^{(n+1)} - \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_{n+1}}\,\epsilon_\theta)/\sqrt{\bar\alpha_{n+1}}$:

$$X^{(n)} \;=\; \sqrt{\frac{\bar\alpha_n}{\bar\alpha_{n+1}}}\,X^{(n+1)} + \!\left(\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_n} - \sqrt{\frac{\bar\alpha_n(1-\bar\alpha_{n+1})}{\bar\alpha_{n+1}}}\right)\epsilon_\theta(X^{(n+1)}, n+1).$$

This is a deterministic map from $X^{(n+1)}$ to $X^{(n)}$ — a discrete dynamical system with no randomness once $X^{(N)}$ is sampled.

5.2 Continuous-time limit

Introduce continuous time $t \in [0, T]$ via $\bar\alpha_t$, identify $X_t = X^{(\lfloor tN/T \rfloor)}$, and Taylor-expand the deterministic update around $\Delta t = T/N \to 0$. A standard manipulation (see e.g. Song et al. 2021) gives the ODE in $\epsilon$-form:

$$\frac{dX_t}{dt} \;=\; -\frac{\beta_t}{2}\,X_t \;+\; \frac{\beta_t}{2\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}}\,\epsilon_\theta(X_t, t), \qquad \beta_t = -\frac{d\log\bar\alpha_t}{dt}.$$

Equivalently, with the change of variable $Y_t = X_t / \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}$ and $\tau_t = \sqrt{(1-\bar\alpha_t)/\bar\alpha_t}$,

$$dY_t \;=\; d\tau_t \cdot \epsilon_\theta(X_t, t),$$

i.e. $Y_t$ is a deterministic function of the cumulative noise-to-signal ratio.

5.3 Probability-flow ODE in score form

Use the score-noise identity $\epsilon_\theta = -\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,s_\theta$. The ODE rewrites as

Theorem 2 (probability-flow ODE). For the VP forward SDE $dX_t = f(X_t,t)\,dt + g(t)\,dW_t$ with marginals $\{p_t\}$, the deterministic ODE
$$\frac{dX_t}{dt} \;=\; f(X_t, t) - \tfrac{1}{2}\,g^2(t)\,\nabla\log p_t(X_t)$$
has the same marginal law $\{p_t\}$ as the forward SDE.

Comparison to Anderson's reverse SDE (Lecture 3): the SDE uses drift $f - g^2\,\nabla\log p_t$ and adds noise $g\,d\bar W_t$. The ODE uses half the score correction and zero noise. Both produce the same one-time marginals.

5.4 Why the marginals match — Fokker–Planck

Forward FP (Lecture 3, Theorem):

$$\partial_t p_t \;=\; -\nabla\!\cdot(f\,p_t) + \tfrac{g^2}{2}\,\Delta p_t.$$

Continuity equation for the deterministic flow with velocity $v(x,t) = f - \tfrac{1}{2}g^2\,\nabla\log p_t$:

$$\partial_t p_t \;=\; -\nabla\!\cdot(v\,p_t).$$

Using $p_t\,\nabla\log p_t = \nabla p_t$:

$$-\nabla\!\cdot\!\bigl[(f - \tfrac{1}{2}g^2\,\nabla\log p_t)\,p_t\bigr] \;=\; -\nabla\!\cdot(f\,p_t) + \tfrac{1}{2}g^2\,\nabla\!\cdot(\nabla p_t) \;=\; -\nabla\!\cdot(f\,p_t) + \tfrac{g^2}{2}\,\Delta p_t.$$

This is exactly forward FP. Both dynamics share the same evolution equation for $p_t$, hence the same marginals (with matching initial condition). $\square$

5.5 Three dynamics, one marginal law

Summarizing:

$$\begin{aligned} \text{Forward SDE:}\quad &dX_t \;=\; f\,dt + g\,dW_t, \\ \text{Reverse SDE (Anderson):}\quad &dX_t \;=\; \bigl(f - g^2\,\nabla\log p_t\bigr)\,dt + g\,d\bar W_t, \\ \text{Reverse ODE (PF):}\quad &dX_t \;=\; \bigl(f - \tfrac{1}{2}g^2\,\nabla\log p_t\bigr)\,dt. \end{aligned}$$

All three share $\{p_t\}$. The first two are stochastic and "share noise paths" only in distribution; the ODE is a single deterministic flow once $X_T$ is fixed.

6. Consequences of determinism

6.1 Acceleration via coarse Euler

Discretize the PF-ODE with a Runge–Kutta or higher-order solver. Empirically, $\sim 50$ function evaluations of $\epsilon_\theta$ approach the FID of $\sim 1000$-step DDPM. The reason: deterministic ODEs admit higher-order solvers (RK4, DPM-Solver), while stochastic SDEs are stuck at $O(\sqrt{\Delta t})$ for Euler–Maruyama.

6.2 Inversion

The deterministic update $X^{(n+1)} \mapsto X^{(n)}$ is a function. Run the recursion in reverse to obtain $X^{(0)} \mapsto X^{(N)}$ — image to latent. Used in editing, faithful reconstruction, and prompt-to-prompt manipulation.

6.3 Smooth latent interpolation

Given two latents $X^{(N)}_a, X^{(N)}_b \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$, spherical interpolation $X^{(N)}_t = \mathrm{slerp}(X^{(N)}_a, X^{(N)}_b, t)$ followed by deterministic decoding produces semantically smooth interpolations between $X^{(0)}_a$ and $X^{(0)}_b$. This requires the decoding map to be a continuous function — exactly what $\sigma \equiv 0$ provides.

6.4 DDPM vs DDIM — choosing $\sigma$

  • $\sigma = \tilde\beta$ (DDPM): maximum stochasticity, diversity of samples, slowest convergence.
  • $\sigma = 0$ (deterministic): fewest steps, invertibility, smoothest latent space; potentially less sample diversity per latent budget.
  • Intermediate $\sigma$: tunes the bias–variance / diversity–speed trade-off.

7. Summary and bridge to Lecture 5

  • The DDPM training loss only sees single-time conditional marginals.
  • DDIM constructs a non-Markovian forward joint with the same marginals, indexed by $\{\sigma_n\}$.
  • Pair invariance ($X^{(0)}, X^{(n)}$) lets DDIM reuse DDPM-trained networks. Triple invariance fails — sampler behavior depends on $\sigma$.
  • $\sigma_n \equiv 0$ yields a deterministic flow: the probability-flow ODE, with half the score correction of the reverse SDE.
  • Determinism unlocks ODE solvers, inversion, and latent interpolation.
