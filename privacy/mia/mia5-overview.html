MIA 5 · LLM Membership Inference — Overview

A one-page tour of Lecture 5. For full slides see mia5-llm.html; for detailed walkthroughs see mia5-llm-note.html.

One-line summary. The classical MIA toolkit (Lecture 4) does not transfer to LLMs because shadow-model training is infeasible and the training distribution is unknown. Lecture 5 surveys shadow-free LLM MIAs — perplexity baselines, neighbourhood comparison, self-prompted calibration, context-aware variance, and token-weighted likelihood ratios (InfoRMIA) — and the sobering benchmark evidence that pre-training MIA on open LLMs is still close to random.

§1 Why LLMs break the classical toolkit

  • Scale. LiRA needs 64–256 shadow models. For a 7B-parameter LLM trained on a trillion tokens, this is impossible compute.
  • Unknown training distribution. No public reference distribution exists for frontier model training mixes.
  • One-pass training. Modern LLMs see most pre-training data once, weakening the overfitting signal Yeom-style attacks rely on.
  • Three threat models: pre-training (hardest), fine-tuning (most tractable), context/RAG (a different problem).

§2 Perplexity baseline and why it fails

The natural first attack is to threshold $\text{PPL}(x) = \exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{T}\sum_t \log p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t})\right)$ — members should have lower perplexity. The failure mode: perplexity confounds memorization with intrinsic predictability. "The cat sat on the mat" has low perplexity even if unseen; a technical sentence has high perplexity even if memorized. Every subsequent LLM MIA is some form of calibration that strips out the predictability term.

§3 Neighbourhood attack

Generate $K$ neighbours of $x$ by masking spans and filling with a separate reference LM. Score:

$$s(x) = \log p_\theta(x) - \frac{1}{K}\sum_k \log p_\theta(\tilde x^{(k)}).$$

If the target memorized $x$, it strongly prefers the exact wording over perturbations. No shadow training needed — practical for frozen LLMs. Weakens on highly structured text (code, math).

§4 SPV-MIA — self-prompted calibration

For fine-tuned models, prompt the target with a prefix of $x$ and sample $K$ continuations from the target itself. Score is the gap between the actual $x$'s log-probability and the average log-probability of the self-generated continuations. AUC ~0.85 on LLaMA fine-tuning regimes. Strong for fine-tuning, degrades to baseline on pre-training.

§5 Context-aware MIA

If $x$ is truly memorized, $p_\theta(x \mid c)$ should be high and stable across different prefixes $c$. Score is the variance of $\log p_\theta(x \mid c)$ over a set of prefixes — members have low variance. Complementary signal to perplexity: combining the two outperforms either alone.

§6 InfoRMIA — token-weighted likelihood ratio (SOTA)

Perplexity averages all tokens equally, but memorization signal lives in rare informative tokens. InfoRMIA weights each token by its informativeness under a reference model:

$$s(x) = \sum_t w_t \cdot \big[\log p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t}) - \log p_{\text{ref}}(x_t \mid x_{<t})\big],$$

with $w_t$ large when the reference is uncertain. This is a Monte-Carlo estimator of the mutual information $I(\mathbf{1}[z\in D]; x_t \mid x_{<t})$. State of the art on WikiMIA: ~0.74 AUC, ~12% TPR at 1% FPR.

§7 Scaling and benchmarks

  • Bigger models memorize more verbatim text, log-linearly with capacity (Carlini scaling laws).
  • More duplicates → more memorization — a document appearing 100× is memorized far more reliably than one appearing once.
  • WikiMIA (pre/post-cutoff Wikipedia): MIA AUC ~0.6–0.75 with modern attacks. Easy to construct but leaks distributional shift signal.
  • MIMIR (known-training-set open LLMs): MIA AUC ~0.55 — pre-training detection on Pythia-scale models is barely above random with current methods. The honest benchmark.

§8 From MIA to extraction

Pipeline: prompt the model with diverse continuations → score each with an MIA detector → surface top-scoring outputs as candidate verbatim training data. Distinguish extractable (model spontaneously emits the text) from inferable (MIA can recognise it when offered). Inferable set is larger; both notions create legal obligations under regimes like GDPR.

§9 Defenses for LLMs

  • DP fine-tuning: practical, $\varepsilon \in [1, 8]$ typically costs <2% accuracy, reduces SPV-MIA AUC substantially. DP pre-training at frontier scale still open.
  • Deduplication: highest-impact, cheapest defense. Removing exact/near-duplicates cuts extractable memorization by an order of magnitude.
  • Machine unlearning: post-hoc removal of specific documents, with MIA as the verification tool.
  • Legal context: GDPR right to erasure, NYT v. OpenAI (2023), and ongoing litigation use MIA-style evidence — MIA is now a regulatory tool, not just research.

Three things to remember

  1. Calibration is the entire problem. Raw perplexity confounds memorization with predictability. Every LLM MIA strips one of these out.
  2. Shadow-free or it doesn't ship. Anything requiring re-pretraining a frontier LLM is academically interesting but practically irrelevant.
  3. Honest benchmarks lower expectations. Pre-training MIA on open LLMs (MIMIR) is barely above random with current methods. Fine-tuning MIA is reliable; pre-training MIA is an open problem.

Open problems

  • Reliable pre-training MIA on frontier-scale LLMs (MIMIR baseline is ~random).
  • Multi-modal MIA for vision-language models.
  • Reference-free attacks beyond neighbourhood.
  • MIA in agent/RAG settings, where "training data" is a moving target.
  • Audit-grade DP certificates derived from MIA results.

Where this series ends

Five lectures have traced MIA from Homer's genomic attack (Lecture 1) through Shokri's shadow paradigm (Lecture 2) and the theoretical clarification (Lecture 3) to the modern per-example era (Lecture 4) and finally to LLMs (Lecture 5). The unifying claim: every optimal MIA is a likelihood-ratio test; what changes across settings is how the null and alternative distributions are calibrated. The frontier is shadow-free calibration at LLM scale, and the next frontier is per-example DP audits derived from those attacks.
