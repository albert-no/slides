IB 2 — Notes: IB and Deep Learning

Companion notes for ib2-deep-learning.html.

§01 — DNN as Markov chain

DPI for deterministic functions

For a deterministic function $T = f(X)$, the Markov chain $Y - X - T$ holds trivially (T is a function of X, so $p(t|x,y) = p(t|x)$). DPI then gives $I(T;Y) \le I(X;Y)$.

For invertible functions, equality holds: $I(f(X);Y) = I(X;Y)$ when $f$ is bijective. This is why the first layer of a network (if it has more units than the input dimension) can preserve all information. Only layers that reduce dimension or apply non-invertible nonlinearities lose information.

When does DPI give strict inequality?

Strict inequality $I(T;Y) < I(X;Y)$ occurs when $T = f(X)$ is not a sufficient statistic of $X$ for $Y$. In practice, most hidden layers are not sufficient statistics, so strict inequality is the norm.

The ReLU subtlety

ReLU($a$) = max(0, $a$) is not invertible (negative values map to 0), so information is lost. However, for wide layers with many neurons, the layer-wise mapping $T_l = \text{ReLU}(W_l T_{l-1} + b_l)$ may lose very little information in practice if most activations are positive.

§02 — Information plane hypothesis

Original experimental setup (Shwartz-Ziv & Tishby)

Network: 5 fully-connected hidden layers (12-10-7-5-4-3-2 architecture), tanh activations, trained on a synthetic binary classification task (12-dimensional input, 2 classes defined by a spherical boundary).

MI estimation: activations were binned into 30 equal-width bins per dimension. Discrete MI was computed from the binned joint distributions.

Key observation: in the information plane, layers first move right and up (fitting), then move left while staying roughly at the same height (compression).

The SGD noise argument

Shwartz-Ziv & Tishby argued that SGD noise drives compression via a diffusion-like process in weight space. The argument is that once the network is near a good solution, gradients are small and SGD noise dominates, causing a random walk that effectively averages out input-specific information.

This argument has parallels to the stochastic relaxation view: if the dynamics of the weights are diffusive (Brownian), then by the second law of thermodynamics (entropy increase), the mutual information $I(X;T)$ should decrease over time.

Generalization bounds via MI

The Xu-Raginsky (2017) bound states:

$$|\text{gen}(\mu, P_{S})| \le \sqrt{\frac{2\sigma^2\, I(S; W)}{n}},$$

where $S$ is the training set, $W$ is the learned weights, $\sigma^2$ is a sub-Gaussian parameter of the loss, and $n$ is the sample size. This is a PAC-Bayes-style bound connecting MI to generalization.

The bound is typically loose for practical networks but establishes the principle that lower MI between training data and weights correlates with better generalization.

§03 — Variational Information Bottleneck

Proof of the rate upper bound

We need to show $I(X;T) \le \mathbb{E}_{p(x)}[D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p(t|x) \| r(t))]$ for any $r(t)$.

$$\begin{aligned} \mathbb{E}_{p(x)}[D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p(t|x) \| r(t))] &= \mathbb{E}_{p(x)}\left[\sum_t p(t|x) \log \frac{p(t|x)}{r(t)}\right] \\ &= \mathbb{E}_{p(x)}\left[\sum_t p(t|x) \log \frac{p(t|x)}{p(t)} + \sum_t p(t|x) \log \frac{p(t)}{r(t)}\right] \\ &= I(X;T) + \mathbb{E}_{p(x)}\left[\sum_t p(t|x) \log \frac{p(t)}{r(t)}\right] \\ &= I(X;T) + \sum_t p(t) \log \frac{p(t)}{r(t)} \\ &= I(X;T) + D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p(t) \| r(t)) \\ &\ge I(X;T). \end{aligned}$$

The key step is that $\mathbb{E}_{p(x)}[\sum_t p(t|x) \cdot g(t)] = \sum_t p(t) \cdot g(t)$ by the tower property.

Why $r(t) = \mathcal{N}(0,I)$?

With Gaussian encoder $p_\phi(t|x) = \mathcal{N}(\mu_\phi(x), \text{diag}(\sigma^2_\phi(x)))$ and standard Gaussian prior, the KL has a closed form (same as the VAE, see diffusion/diff1-vae-elbo-note.html):

$$D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_\phi(t|x) \| r(t)) = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{k=1}^K \left(\mu_{\phi,k}^2(x) + \sigma_{\phi,k}^2(x) - 1 - \log \sigma_{\phi,k}^2(x)\right).$$

Connection to Barber-Agakov

The lower bound on $I(T;Y)$ used in VIB is exactly the Barber-Agakov bound from mi/mi1-bounds.html:

$$I(T;Y) = H(Y) - H(Y|T) = H(Y) + \mathbb{E}_{p(t,y)}[\log p(y|t)].$$

Since $p(y|t)$ is unknown, replace with a variational approximation $q_\psi(y|t) \le p(y|t)$ in cross-entropy sense. The gap is exactly $\mathbb{E}_{p(t)}[D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p(y|t) \| q_\psi(y|t))] \ge 0$.

VIB vs $\beta$-VAE

The $\beta$-VAE (Higgins et al., 2017) also multiplies the KL term by a coefficient $\beta$. The parallel is exact:

  • $\beta$-VAE: trades reconstruction quality against disentanglement of the latent code.
  • VIB: trades prediction quality against compression of the representation.

Both are instances of the IB principle applied to different decoders.

Reparameterization trick

Same as in the VAE (see diffusion/diff1-vae-elbo.html): to backpropagate through the sampling $t \sim p_\phi(t|x) = \mathcal{N}(\mu_\phi(x), \sigma^2_\phi(x) I)$, reparameterize as $t = \mu_\phi(x) + \sigma_\phi(x) \odot \varepsilon$ where $\varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,I)$.

§04 — The debate

Saxe et al. (2019) in detail

Key experiments:

  1. Linear networks: no compression phase at all (MI is constant after convergence). This shows compression is not a generic property of gradient-based training.
  2. tanh networks: compression observed, but it also occurs with full-batch gradient descent (no SGD noise needed). The saturation of tanh effectively bins the activations.
  3. ReLU networks: no clear compression phase. Since ReLU is piecewise linear (not saturating), activations are not discretized.

The binning critique

For a deterministic layer $T_l = f(X)$, if $X$ is continuous and $f$ is smooth and injective on a set of positive measure, then $I(X;T_l) = \infty$ (the function is invertible, so knowing $T_l$ determines $X$ exactly).

Binning regularizes: $I_{\Delta}(X;T_l) = I(X; \lfloor T_l / \Delta \rfloor)$ is finite and depends on the bin width $\Delta$. As $\Delta \to 0$, $I_{\Delta} \to \infty$. The observed "compression" in the information plane may reflect the network's activations concentrating into fewer bins, not genuine information loss.

Geometric compression

Even when MI-based compression is not observed, hidden representations do undergo geometric compression: within-class variance decreases relative to between-class variance. This is measurable without binning and correlates with generalization.

Goldfeld et al. (2019) proposed using neural network-based MI estimators (building on the bounds from mi/mi1-bounds.html) to avoid binning artifacts. The results are more nuanced: some compression is observed, but the magnitude and timing depend on architecture, activation, and estimator choice.

Current consensus (as of 2025)

  • The IB objective is a principled regularizer (VIB). This is not in dispute.
  • Whether standard training implicitly solves IB is architecture- and activation-dependent.
  • The information plane is a useful visualization tool, but MI values for deterministic networks require careful estimation.
  • Information-theoretic generalization bounds ($I(S;W)$) provide valid theoretical guarantees, independent of the compression debate.
